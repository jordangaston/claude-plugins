# The Brand Gap

A framework from Marty Neumeier for building a **charismatic brand** — one customers feel has no substitute — by closing the gap between business strategy (analytical, logical, verbal) and creative execution (intuitive, emotional, visual). Most brands underperform not because strategy or creativity is weak, but because the two are disconnected.

This skill applies that framework to real brand work: diagnosing positioning, sharpening differentiation, evaluating names and identities, and keeping a brand alive over time.

## First principle: what a brand actually is

A brand is **a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or organization.** Internalize the three consequences, because they govern every judgment this skill makes:

- It's a *gut feeling* — people are emotional and intuitive, so logic alone never builds a brand.
- It's a *person's* gut feeling — the brand lives in the customer's mind, not in the company's slide deck. You don't control it; you can only influence it.
- It's defined by *individuals but owned by the public* — when enough people share the same gut feeling, you have a brand.

A brand is therefore **not** a logo, **not** a visual identity system, and **not** a product. Those are tools that shape the gut feeling. The goal of all brand work is **trust**, and the practical proxy for trust is this: *the value of a brand grows in proportion to how quickly and easily customers say "yes" to its promise.* When you evaluate anything, ask whether it makes the customer's "yes" faster and more certain.

## How to run this skill

Figure out where the user is and act:

- **"Diagnose / review / audit our brand," or symptoms like "we feel generic," "positioning is muddy"** → run the **Brand Gap Diagnostic** (below). This is the default.
- **"Help us with differentiation / naming / validation / etc." or a question scoped to one discipline** → go straight to that discipline. Read the matching section of `references/five-disciplines.md` and work through its tools with the user's specifics.
- **"Explain the Brand Gap / what are the five disciplines"** → teach from this file and the reference, using the user's own brand for examples wherever possible. Don't just recite — apply.
- **"Is this name/logo/icon/tagline any good?"** → use the naming criteria and the five validation areas (Discipline 3 and 4 in the reference).

Whatever the entry point, **always pull in the user's real context.** A diagnostic of a brand you know things about should reference the actual product, vertical, and competitors — not generic advice. Ask for what you're missing rather than guessing.

### When to produce a written artifact

Default to conversational analysis. Produce a written **brand audit** document (markdown, or docx if they want something formal to circulate) when the user asks for a deliverable, says they want to share it with a cofounder/team/investor, or when the analysis spans all five disciplines and benefits from being captured. Use the structure in "Brand audit format" below. When in doubt, do the analysis in chat and offer the document at the end.

## The Brand Gap Diagnostic

Work through the brand in this order. Each discipline has concrete tools in `references/five-disciplines.md` — read it before going deep on any one. Don't treat this as a checklist to recite; treat it as a sequence of judgments, and surface the one or two places where the brand is actually weakest rather than padding all five equally.

**0. The gap itself.** Before the disciplines, locate the gap. Where is this brand's strategy disconnected from its execution? Common tells: a sharp positioning deck but a generic website; a clever name nobody can act on; leadership that can answer "what do we do" but not "why does it matter." Name the gap in one sentence.

**1. Differentiate — do you stand out?**
The litmus test is three questions: **Who are you? What do you do? Why does it matter?** Unless the answers are compelling *and* the third is about the customer (not the company), there's no brand yet. The most important word here is **focus** — an unfocused brand is so broad it stands for nothing. Probe for focus erosion, especially ill-considered brand extensions chasing short-term revenue at the expense of long-term meaning. (See reference for the features→benefits→experience→identification shift and design's five functions.)

**2. Collaborate — is the brand built coherently?**
A brand is the cumulative output of everyone who touches the company: employees, agencies, partners, customers. Diagnose *how* brand decisions get made. Is there a coherent model, or is it fragmented across owners with no steward? (Reference covers the three collaboration models and their tradeoffs.)

**3. Innovate — is the execution brave enough?**
Creativity is what ignites passion; copying competitors guarantees you can't lead. "You know an idea is good when it scares the hell out of everybody." Evaluate the brand's most visible expressions — name, icon/avatar, packaging, website — against the criteria in the reference (the seven naming criteria, the five reading sequences for packaging/landing pages). Look for safe, derivative, committee-flattened work.

**4. Validate — are you testing with real people?**
Brand communication is two-way now; customers expect to interact. Favor quick, cheap, realistic tests over long quantitative surveys. Score every brand expression — from icon to product — on the five areas: **distinctiveness, relevance, memorability, extendibility, depth.** (Reference details each.)

**5. Cultivate — is the brand alive?**
Brands are living systems: they must adapt to market, culture, and time. A living brand is a pattern of behavior growing out of character, not a style veneer — it resonates when external actions match internal culture. Brands may be *inconsistent* as long as they never abandon their defining attributes. Over time a brand grows *more* fragile (one mistake away from damage), so success depends on constantly regenerating "corporate memory" — often via a brand steward / Chief Brand Officer and an internal education program.

After working the five, **synthesize**: name the single biggest constraint on this brand becoming charismatic, and the highest-leverage move to close its specific gap. One clear recommendation beats five vague ones.

## Brand audit format

When producing a written audit, use this structure:

```
# Brand Audit: [Brand name]
## The gap in one sentence
[Where strategy and execution are disconnected for this specific brand]

## 1. Differentiate
- Who are you / What do you do / Why does it matter — [the three answers, assessed]
- Focus: [is it focused or diluted? extension risks?]
- Verdict + the one thing to fix

## 2. Collaborate
[How brand decisions get made; coherence; steward or no steward]

## 3. Innovate
[Bravery and quality of name, icon, packaging, site; what's playing it safe]

## 4. Validate
[What's been tested; scores on distinctiveness/relevance/memorability/extendibility/depth]

## 5. Cultivate
[Is it a living brand? culture-action alignment; fragility; memory/stewardship]

## The one move
[Single highest-leverage recommendation to close this brand's specific gap]
```

Keep it sharp and specific to the brand. Cut any line that could apply to any company.

## Voice and judgment

- Be direct. Neumeier's framework rewards courage; wishy-washy brand advice helps no one. If a name is weak or a brand is unfocused, say so and say why.
- Stay anchored to the customer's gut feeling and to trust. Re-derive recommendations from "does this make the customer's *yes* faster" rather than from taste.
- Prefer one strong, focused recommendation over a long balanced list — focus is the meta-lesson of the whole framework.
- Use the user's real brand, vertical, and competitors throughout. Generic branding advice is exactly what this framework exists to defeat.
