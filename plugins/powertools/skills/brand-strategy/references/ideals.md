# The Nine Ideals — Evaluation Rubric

These nine ideals describe what the strongest brands have in common. Functional criteria (memorable, legible, protectable, works across media) are table stakes; the ideals are what separate good from great. Use them as a **rubric** to pressure-test a brand strategy before finalizing — go through each, ask the diagnostic question, and flag anything weak.

| Ideal | What it means | Diagnostic question |
|---|---|---|
| **Vision** | A compelling, articulate vision from committed leadership is the foundation of every strong brand. | Is there a real vision here, held by someone with conviction — or just a desire for a logo? |
| **Meaning** | The brand stands for something specific: a big idea, a position, a set of values, a distinct voice. | Can we state in one sentence what this brand *stands for*? Would employees say the same thing? |
| **Authenticity** | The expression fits the organization's actual mission, history, culture, and capability. Requires clarity about who you really are. | Is this who they *are*, or who they wish they were? Can they deliver on it today? |
| **Differentiation** | Brands compete in their category and against everything else for attention and loyalty. The difference must stand out. | Is the difference true, provable, relevant, and hard to copy — or a claim anyone could make? |
| **Sustainability** | The ability to endure in a world of constant change and unpredictable futures. | Will this still hold up in 5–10 years, across changes no one can predict? |
| **Coherence** | Every touchpoint feels familiar and produces the desired effect; the pieces hold together without feeling rigid. | Does the whole system feel like one company across every interaction? |
| **Flexibility** | The identity positions the company for change and growth; it supports an evolving strategy. | Can this stretch to new products, markets, and channels without breaking? |
| **Commitment** | The organization actively manages and protects the brand as an asset — top-down mandate, bottom-up understanding. | Is leadership committed to governing this over time, and are employees equipped to live it? |
| **Value** | Building awareness, recognition, uniqueness, and quality creates measurable business value. Brand is an asset, not an expense. | Does this strategy connect to measurable value — recognition, loyalty, premium, equity? |

## How to apply

1. After drafting the brand brief, walk each ideal in order.
2. For each, answer the diagnostic honestly and mark **strong / weak / unclear**.
3. For every *weak* or *unclear*, name the specific gap and propose a fix — usually a sharper positioning, a more authentic claim, or a more durable big idea.
4. Pay special attention to **Authenticity** and **Differentiation** — these are the two most common failure points. A strategy that's aspirational-but-untrue, or distinctive-but-unprovable, will collapse on contact with customers.
5. Summarize the check in a few lines at the end of the strategy document so decision-makers can see the strategy was stress-tested, not just asserted.

The ideals are about substance, not aesthetics — design excellence is assumed. A beautiful identity on a hollow strategy still fails the rubric.
