# Naming & Taglines

A name is the most-used, hardest-working brand asset — repeated in conversation, email, signage, search, and code, day in and day out. The wrong name creates friction (people can't say, spell, or remember it), legal risk, or alienation. Naming is a rigorous, exhaustive, strategic process — often hundreds of candidates are generated to find one that is strong *and* legally available. Don't treat it as a quick brainstorm.

## Process

1. **Anchor in strategy.** A name expresses the big idea and positioning. Start there, not from clever wordplay.
2. **Generate broadly across name types** (below). Quantity first — go wide before you filter.
3. **Filter against the qualities of a strong name** (below).
4. **Screen for availability.** Trademark conflicts and domain/handle availability kill most candidates. Flag that real legal clearance by an IP attorney is required — searches must be thorough because a name has to last.
5. **Test connotations** in every market and language served, to avoid negative meanings.
6. **Expect to compromise.** There's no perfect name. The goal is the best *available* strong name, not perfection.

Beware the common myths: that naming is easy ("like naming a baby"), that you'll "know it when you hear it" (good names are strategies that need testing and proving), and that you can skip legal search to save money (the risk compounds over the brand's life).

## Types of names

Generate candidates in each bucket — different types carry different tradeoffs.

| Type | What it is | Tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| **Founder** | Named after a person (Tory Burch, Ben & Jerry's) | Easier to protect; ego-satisfying — but tied to a real human. |
| **Descriptive** | States what the business does (E*TRADE, Toys "R" Us) | Communicates intent clearly — but can constrain as the company diversifies. |
| **Fabricated** | Invented word (Kodak, TiVo, Pinterest) | Distinctive, easier to trademark — but requires investment to teach the market what it means. |
| **Metaphor** | A thing/place/animal/myth/foreign word evoking a quality (Nike, Patagonia, Amazon) | Evocative and ownable — but indirect. |
| **Acronym** | Initials (IBM, GE, USAA) | Hard to remember and trademark; usually needs heavy spend to mean anything. Often earned only after the full name is established. |
| **Magic spell** | Altered spelling (Flickr, Netflix, Tumblr) | Distinctive and protectable — watch for spelling friction. |
| **Combination** | Blends types (Citibank = metaphor + descriptive) | Often the sweet spot; understandable yet ownable. |

## Qualities of a strong name

Filter candidates against these:

- **Meaningful** — communicates something about the brand's essence; supports the intended image.
- **Distinctive** — unique, memorable, easy to say and spell; differentiated; shareable.
- **Future-oriented** — has "long legs"; positions for growth and change; preserves possibilities.
- **Modular** — enables brand extensions (sub-products, lines) without breaking.
- **Protectable** — can be owned, trademarked, and secured as a domain/handle.
- **Positive** — good connotations in every market served; no strong negatives.
- **Visual** — works in a logo, in body text, and within the brand architecture.

## Taglines

A tagline is a short phrase capturing the brand's essence, personality, and positioning, and setting it apart from rivals. The best ones outlast campaigns and become part of the culture. They're deceptively simple and grow out of an intensive strategic process — not arbitrary.

**Types** (by rhetorical strategy):
- **Imperative** — commands action, usually starts with a verb ("Just do it," "Think different").
- **Descriptive** — describes the service, product, or promise ("The ultimate driving machine").
- **Superlative** — positions as best in class ("King of beers").
- **Provocative** — thought-provoking, often a question ("Got milk?").
- **Specific** — reveals the business category outright.

**Essential characteristics:** short; differentiated; unique; captures essence and positioning; easy to say and remember; no negative connotations; legible in a small font; protectable/trademarkable; evokes an emotional response. They are genuinely difficult to create — and require consistent, repeated use to take hold.

Note the relationship: the **big idea** is the internal strategic core (see `positioning.md`); a **tagline** is one possible *external expression* of it. Don't conflate them. Some brands keep the big idea private and never say it out loud.
