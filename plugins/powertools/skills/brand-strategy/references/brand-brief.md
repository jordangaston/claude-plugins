# The Brand Brief

The brand brief is the central deliverable of a strategy engagement. It distills research and decisions into a short, agreed-upon document that everything downstream — naming, identity design, messaging, marketing — flows from. Its job is alignment: get the decision-makers to agree on *what the brand is* before anyone debates how it looks. A brief that fits on one or two pages and survives scrutiny is worth more than an exhaustive deck nobody internalizes.

## When to produce it

Produce the brief at the end of Step 3 (clarify strategy), once research insight exists and positioning has been worked out. Treat it as a consensus-building artifact, not a private summary — the wording is meant to be argued over and ratified.

## Template

Use this structure. Keep each field tight; if a field needs a paragraph, it isn't distilled yet.

```
# Brand Brief: [Brand Name]

## Situation
Why this work is happening now (new venture / rename / reposition / merger / system).
One or two sentences.

## Vision
The future the brand is trying to create. What changes in the world because this
exists? Tie to the founder's or leadership's conviction.

## Target audience
Primary customer(s) and the key stakeholders who affect success (including internal:
employees, board, partners). Their needs, perceptions, and what they care about most.

## Positioning
The distinct place the brand owns in the customer's mind, relative to the alternatives.
Format that works well: "For [audience] who [need/context], [brand] is the [category]
that [single differentiating benefit], because [reason to believe]."

## Differentiation
The competitive difference that is true, provable, and hard to copy. What can this
brand demonstrate that rivals can't credibly claim?

## Big idea
The simply-worded core idea (often 2–5 words) that acts as the organizing totem —
internal beacon and external edge. Should pass the litmus test: does a proposed
decision serve this idea or not?

## Brand essence & attributes
The 3–5 adjectives the brand should consistently feel like across every touchpoint.

## Value proposition
The concrete value delivered, in the customer's own terms — not features, outcomes.

## Voice & key messages
The tone the brand speaks in, plus up to three key messages that support the big idea.
(People retain about three messages — don't exceed it.)

## Competitive landscape
The handful of alternatives the customer actually weighs, and the white space this
strategy claims.

## What this brand is NOT
Optional but clarifying. Boundaries prevent dilution.
```

## Facilitation questions

Use these to extract the raw material from a founder or team. Ask the ones you're missing; don't run the whole list mechanically.

**Vision & purpose**
- If this brand wins completely, what's different about the world in ten years?
- Why does this company exist beyond making money?
- What does leadership believe that competitors don't?

**Audience**
- Who is the single most important customer? Describe a real one.
- What do they believe, fear, want, and currently settle for?
- Who else has to believe in this brand for it to succeed (internally and externally)?

**Positioning & differentiation**
- What category is the customer putting you in — and is that the category you want?
- If a customer chose a competitor instead, what would have made them do it?
- What's the one thing you do that you could *prove* on the spot, not just assert?
- Where do all the competitors sound the same? That sameness is your opening.

**Big idea & essence**
- Finish this: "We're the ___ that ___."
- If the brand were a person walking into a room, how would people describe them after?
- What three words should every employee be able to say the brand stands for?

**Reality check**
- Is this who you actually are, or who you wish you were? (Authenticity beats aspiration.)
- Will this still fit when you've grown 10x or added new products?

## Quality bar

A strong brief is:
- **Decisive** — makes choices and closes off options, rather than listing everything true about the company.
- **Plain** — readable by anyone, free of marketing jargon.
- **Distilled** — every line earns its place; cut modifiers and hedges.
- **True** — grounded in what the organization genuinely is and can deliver.
- **Repeatable** — a person can paraphrase the positioning and big idea after one read.

Before finalizing, run the brief through `ideals.md` as a rubric.
