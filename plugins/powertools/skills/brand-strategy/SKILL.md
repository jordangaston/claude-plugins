# Brand Strategy

Brand strategy is the central, unifying idea that all behavior, communication, and design align around. It builds on a vision, fits the business strategy, grows out of the company's values and culture, and reflects a real understanding of the customer. A good strategy defines **positioning, differentiation, and the value proposition** clearly enough that anyone — CEO or new hire — can repeat it. The deliverable is a **brand brief**: a short, decisive document that downstream naming, design, and marketing work flows from.

This skill produces strategy, not logos. It covers the upstream thinking — research, positioning, the big idea, naming, architecture, and messaging — that makes design decisions defensible instead of arbitrary.

## The five-phase process

Wheeler's full brand identity process has five phases. Strategy lives in phases 1–2; the rest are downstream and this skill only frames them.

1. **Conducting research** — gain insight into the market, competitors, customers, and the brand's current language.
2. **Clarifying strategy** — synthesize research into positioning, a big idea, and a brand brief. *(This skill's center of gravity.)*
3. **Designing identity** — logotype, color, typography, look and feel. (Downstream of this skill.)
4. **Creating touchpoints** — website, packaging, signage, environments, etc.
5. **Managing assets** — launch, guidelines, brand governance.

## Workflow

Work through these steps. Don't skip the framing step — the trigger ("new company" vs. "rebrand" vs. "merger") changes everything downstream. Ask focused questions when you're missing what you need, but make reasonable assumptions and keep momentum rather than interrogating the user.

### Step 1 — Frame the engagement

Establish *why now*. Brand work usually starts from one of these situations, and each shifts the emphasis:
- **New company / new product** — naming and positioning from scratch.
- **Name change** — trademark conflict, bad connotations, post-merger, misleading name.
- **Revitalize a brand** — reposition, enter new markets, appeal to a new audience.
- **Revitalize an identity** — the strategy is fine but expression looks dated.
- **Create an integrated system** — inconsistent nomenclature/look across divisions.
- **Merger** — two equities to reconcile; signal a new leader.

Also nail down: who the customer is, what business strategy this brand serves, who the stakeholders are (external customers, media, *and* internal customers — employees, board, suppliers), and who the decision-makers are.

### Step 2 — Research and audit

Goal: **insight**, not a data dump. Run only the audits that fit the budget and situation:
- **Competitive audit** — how rivals position, name, and sound. Where's the white space?
- **Language / verbal audit** — collect everything the brand currently says about itself (taglines, boilerplate, key messages) and find the contradictions.
- **Market / customer research** — needs, perceptions, preferences, dreams, values, lifestyles. See the world through the customer's eyes.
- **Internal audit** — vision, values, culture, what leadership believes the company is for.

Close research with an **audit readout**: a synthesis of findings and implications, not raw notes. If you have web access and the brand/competitors are real, search to ground the competitive and language audits in reality.

→ For positioning and competitive-audit method, read `references/positioning.md`.

### Step 3 — Clarify strategy

Synthesize the research into the core of the strategy:
- **Positioning** — the distinct place the brand owns in the customer's mind, relative to alternatives. Narrow the focus; a brand gets stronger as it narrows.
- **Differentiation** — the competitive difference that's true, provable, and hard to copy. Go beyond *declaring* an advantage — be able to demonstrate it.
- **Big idea** — a simply-worded statement (often 2–5 words) that acts as an organizational totem: an internal beacon and an external competitive edge. It's a springboard for creative work and a litmus test for decisions. Getting to simple is hard and takes dialogue.
- **Brand essence & attributes** — the 3–5 adjectives the brand should consistently feel like.
- **Value proposition** — the concrete value delivered, in the customer's terms.

→ For the alignment model and how to facilitate the big idea, read `references/positioning.md`.

### Step 4 — Write the brand brief

The brand brief is the deliverable — the agreement that precedes any visual or naming work. It captures vision, positioning, big idea, essence, attributes, audience, value proposition, and the competitive landscape on a single page or two. Build consensus on the brief *before* moving to expression.

→ Use the template and facilitation questions in `references/brand-brief.md`. This is the default output of a strategy engagement.

### Step 5 — Naming and architecture (when in scope)

- If the engagement needs a **name** (company, product, feature, or a rename), run the naming process: generate broadly across name types, then filter against the qualities of a strong name, then flag the need for trademark/domain clearance. → `references/naming.md`
- If the brand is part of a **portfolio** (parent + products/divisions/acquisitions), decide the **brand architecture** — how the names relate and what they borrow from each other. → `references/architecture.md`

### Step 6 — Pressure-test against the ideals

Before finalizing, hold the strategy up against the nine ideals of strong brands (vision, meaning, authenticity, differentiation, sustainability, coherence, flexibility, commitment, value). Use them as a checklist to find weak spots — e.g. "is this *authentic* to who they actually are?" or "will this *flex* as they grow?"

→ Read `references/ideals.md` and apply it as a rubric.

## Output

Default deliverable is a **brand strategy document** built on the brand brief: framing → audit insights → positioning → differentiation → big idea → essence & attributes → value proposition → (name/architecture if in scope) → ideals check. Keep it short and decisive — a brief that's hard to argue with beats an exhaustive one nobody reads. Distill, cut, and favor plain language over jargon.

If the user wants a polished, shareable file (e.g. a Word doc), create one; otherwise deliver the brief inline or as markdown.
