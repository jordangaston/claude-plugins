# Extreme Brainstorming Questions — Full Reference

Each question below stretches one dimension of reality to an extreme. Use the sub-prompts to dig deeper — they're the real engine of the exercise. Don't ask them all at once; pick the most relevant ones based on the user's context and go deeper from there.

Source: Jason Cohen, "Extreme brainstorming questions to trigger new, better ideas"
https://longform.asmartbear.com/extreme-questions/

---

## 1. 10x Prices

**The extreme:** If you were forced to increase your prices by 10x, what would you have to do to justify it?

**Sub-prompts:**
- What sort of brand looks and feels like something that expensive? What positioning would you take?
- How would the design of both the website and the product need to change?
- What subset of your market would you have to target? Do they have different problems or needs?
- Would they consider the high price a positive — as a badge of success or "buying from the best" to lower decision risk?
- What expensive services might you need to supply? Human support? Dedicated infrastructure?
- Is it possible for those costs to be only 2-3x more than today, so the net impact is massive profitability?
- What kind of relationship would you want to cultivate with each customer?
- How would your business model change?

**Why it works:** Early startups often charge too little, and established companies struggle to raise prices on existing customers. Thinking about what justifies a massive hike often reveals things you could do to justify at least 2x.

## 2. No Customers

**The extreme:** If all your customers vanished and you had to earn growth and brand from scratch, what would you do?

**Sub-prompts:**
- How would you distinguish yourself with a unique, compelling message as a fresh competitor?
- What would you put on your home page?
- Would you use a different pricing model to compete better?
- Would your brand need to change to align with the new message?
- Would you throw out features that take lots of work but few people actually use?
- Would you build some new feature that makes you more competitive?
- Would you change infrastructure, architecture, or UX dramatically based on what you now know?

**Why it works:** We don't make important changes because we're complacent with marketing, never got a truly compelling positioning statement, or don't want to incur the penalty of big changes on the existing customer base — even when we're doing the wrong thing competitively. There will be 10x more customers in the future than now, but only if you build for them today.

## 3. No Tech Support

**The extreme:** If you were never allowed to provide tech support in any form, what would have to change?

**Sub-prompts:**
- How would onboarding need to improve so customers self-serve happily? (Hardest step — least familiar, least motivated to power through.)
- Where would the product give users more control, since they can't ask support to do it for them?
- What information needs better visibility, since they can't ask support to look it up?
- What actions need to become more intuitive, since they can't ask "how do I do X?"

**Why it works:** Self-service isn't just better for costs and scaling — it makes customers happier and more loyal. Dramatically improving self-service is often profitable for both top- and bottom-line.

## 4. Maximize Fun

**The extreme:** What would be the most fun thing to build?

**Constraint:** It has to make the product better — not a random technology or unrelated market. Aside from that, anything goes.

**Sub-prompts:**
- Is there a feature you think would be cool for customers to use?
- Is there a technology that would be fun to work on that happens to deliver customer value?
- Could you refactor infrastructure using interesting modern tech that also improves cost, scalability, testability, or maintainability?
- What if you held a one-week hackathon and actually tried to build one of these things?

**Why it works:** When we work on fun things, we work better and harder yet are happy to do it. Biasing toward fun biases toward productivity and long-term fulfillment. Typical prioritization under-appreciates the power of fun.

## 5. Complete Rip-Off

**The extreme:** If your biggest competitor copied every feature you have, how would you still win?

**Sub-prompts (inside the product):**
- Is the differentiator something other than feature utility? Ease of use? Ease of sharing? Great design?
- Is it building the next unique feature so quickly no competitor can catch up? What's that killer next feature?

**Sub-prompts (outside the product):**
- Is it a higher purpose or brand promise customers buy into?
- Is it because you communicate how you make the world better?
- Is it customers rooting for David against Goliath?
- Is it your culture, trust, customer service, or how you handle things that go wrong?
- Is it giving back through open-source, community, or philanthropy?

**Why it works:** All good ideas get copied eventually. Competing only on features results in bullet-point battles — the weakest way to win sales. Creating bigger, more emotional distinction is a powerful way to win.

## 6. No Time

**The extreme:** Ship a full, completed MVP (SLC — Simple, Lovable, Complete) new feature in just two weeks that would delight and surprise some fraction of your customers.

**Sub-prompts:**
- Every bit of complexity has to be stripped out — what's left?
- Can you stub out an API, implementing it manually for now?
- Can you hook up a 3rd-party system, implementing efficiency later?
- Can you skip documentation, support training, and perfect error handling — just long enough to see how people like it?
- A 1000-page book can be a 1-page blog post. It can always be smaller if you trade off detail, precision, or completeness. What's the one-sentence version of the feature?

**Why it works:** Hackathons prove we can build cool things in short time when we want to and make certain trade-offs. If you've lapsed into waterfall with two-week report-outs, this is the shake-up everyone needs.

## 7. Flipped Business Model

**The extreme:** Forced to charge customers in a completely different manner. Usage-based becomes flat monthly; monthly tiers become daily formula-based. The product isn't free, but value and costs must be justified differently.

**Sub-prompts:**
- How would you change how you position the value you deliver?
- Would you have different kinds of customers due to prices effectively being much higher or lower?
- How would that affect brand, messaging, marketing, or sales?
- How would costs have to change so the vast majority of customers are still profitable?
- Would software architectures need to change?
- Are there features you'd need to build (e.g., cost controls for customers) or could now supply?
- Would you create features that cause higher prices but with proportionally higher value?

**Why it works:** Business models force certain frames of thinking about monetizing value. The general idea is to generate far more value than you charge for. Shaking up the model shakes up the value/cost equation — sometimes a different model is actually better for everyone.

## 8. No Website

**The extreme:** Not allowed to have a website. How do you still grow?

**Sub-prompts:**
- Could you grow by word-of-mouth? Does the product help people do that proactively?
- Could you create advocacy — channel partners, ambassadors?
- Could your social media be so good someone signs up without needing to know more?
- Could your product BE marketing? Could your "website" live inside the product, catching potential customers in the flow of learning and trying?
- What if "in-product marketing" is more impactful on growth than any feature?

**Why it works:** Advocates are already using your product, yet we leave "getting more customers" to the marketing department. Non-viral products can still create growth machines from inside the product.

## 9. No Meetings

**The extreme:** No more synchronous meetings, ever again.

**Sub-prompts:**
- How would you onboard new employees without meetings?
- How would you operate, deploy, and navigate the software? What systems and materials would need to exist?
- How would you make decisions — the process and the result? Deciding what to build, prioritizing, brainstorming?
- Could brainstormed ideas be better without meetings (no groupthink ahead of individual thought)?
- Could decisions be stronger (time and space to think, research, explore)?
- How would you create social ties, inside jokes, and relationships without shared space or time?

**Why it works:** One of the most common complaints is "too many meetings." Beyond meeting hygiene, the bigger question is whether systems and processes can prevent them AND increase utility. How much faster could a new team member ramp if they could read "the why" behind the last 20 major decisions?

## 10. No Customer Contact

**The extreme:** Can never talk to customers again. How do you figure out what to build?

**Sub-prompts:**
- Could you measure behavior so well that you quickly know if any change is positive or negative?
- Are there clear signs of value or happiness you could use like conversion in A/B testing?
- Could you test faster — a button that says "coming soon" or "why did you want this?" or "get emailed when we build this" — to measure demand before investing?
- Could you analyze what customers, competitors, or observers say online to inform strategy?
- Could you do better than just copying competitors? Could you analyze what people say about you or competitors online?

**Why it works:** Sometimes you interview customers and come away with the wrong feature ideas anyway. Sometimes they say they'd use something, you build it for five months, and they don't. Stretching your ability to get empirical signal helps avoid those issues and might create breakthroughs.

## 11. Cost Is No Object

**The extreme:** Doesn't matter how unprofitable you are. Prices stay the same but you can spend 10x or 100x more — even wildly unprofitably. Or the product is free too. How do you spend the money?

**Sub-prompts:**
- How much value could you deliver if you thought of the product as philanthropy?
- It could be infrastructure or software, but what about the personal touch?
- What would a dedicated, personal concierge do for each customer?
- If software could do just 30% of that concierge job at 1/100th the cost, could that be a good feature?

**Why it works:** It can be surprisingly difficult to convert money to real customer or business value, even when money is free. If there is a way, it might lead to a new pricing tier that leverages the idea with a sustainable business model added back.

## 12. Sociopathic CEO

**The extreme:** You can change anything, regardless of what anyone thinks or feels.

**Sub-prompts:**
- What if terminating an entire product line is the best thing to do? What would that be like?
- Could you make that pivot by reorganization rather than layoffs, and now the company is executing a much better strategy?
- What transformation is required to fix the strategy that you've been avoiding because it's scary?
- What sacred cows would you slaughter if there were no political consequences?

**Why it works:** You never want to contemplate catastrophic actions, and for good reason — the consequences are dire. But that aversion prevents you from thinking certain thoughts. You should at least explore the thoughts. The very best ideas should not be avoided only because they are hard.

## 13. Mortal Wound

**The extreme:** What externality has the potential to kill the entire company?

**Sub-prompts:**
- Is there a company X that could release product Y at price Z that would reduce your new sales to a trickle and cause half your customers to leave within a year?
- Can you think of a security breach so significant that most customers leave within a year?
- How could the economy change so that no one buys your software?
- Is there something you could do to mitigate the most likely threat?
- Is there something that pivots the company so the threat is no longer relevant?

**Why it works:** You might come up with dozens of existential threats; it's not wise to worry about all of them. But sometimes you find an idea that's wise to do anyway that also shores up your defenses. Or one threat sticks out as so likely it warrants acting ahead of time.

## 14. Philanthropist

**The extreme:** Your only goal is to create the most good in the world, personally for your customers. Ignore how to make money — assume that works itself out.

**Sub-prompts:**
- How could you make their lives better not through greater utility but by doing something important?
- How could you help them achieve their ultimate goals rather than just "use features?"
- If your customers are small businesses, could you help them grow and thrive?
- If they're creators, could you help them improve their craft or get more attention?
- If they're large companies, could you help them use their scale to create more good?
- Could you increase access and equity in your customers' reach and power?

**Why it works:** Strong evidence shows that a mission bigger than yourself creates more and more loyal customers, and more fulfilled employees. Gen Z cares about whether a company does good just as much as compensation. Even from a purely self-interested perspective, there are business reasons to create more good, not just more MRR.

## 15. Only One Thing This Year

**The extreme:** You can only ship one thing this year. One initiative, the entire year. What is it?

**Sub-prompts:**
- What single initiative would make the most difference?
- What's so impactful it would actually be OK if you shipped nothing else?
- What would generate the most revenue?
- What would be so differentiated and desirable that customers buy and stay even with no other product changes?
- What would be so exciting that customers stay even if other things are missing or have bugs?

**Why it works:** When you look back over years of a business, the whole trajectory often comes down to 1-2 big decisions per year. A critical product launch, a key market decision, a key hire, a competitive insight. Thinking this way forces you to think of only the most impactful ideas — which are probably what you should focus on regardless.
