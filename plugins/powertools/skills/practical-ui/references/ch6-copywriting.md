# Practical UI - Chapter 6: Copywriting Guidelines

## 1. Be Concise

Remove every word that does not add information or improve usability. Shorter text is faster to read, easier to understand, and more likely to be read at all. Attention spans are short; respect them.

- **Do:** "Save article?" with a one-line explanation.
- **Don't:** "Would you like to save the article? Don't worry, you'll still be able to publish it at a later date. You can always find saved articles in your library."

### Sub-rules for conciseness

- **Drop filler words** like "actually", "basically", "really", "truthfully", and "quite".
- **Drop short joining words** ("a", "an", "the") where grammar allows.
- **Drop introductory phrases** like "would you like to", "in order to", "when it comes to", "are you sure", "there are", and "it is".
- **Choose shorter synonyms** when they convey the same meaning.
- **Keep sentences under 20 words.** Long, comma-heavy sentences cause readers to lose focus.

---

## 2. Use Sentence Case

Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. Sentence case is simple, grammatically correct, and easy to read. Title case interrupts scanning because uppercase letters where the reader expects lowercase increase cognitive load.

- **Do:** "Save post for later?"
- **Don't:** "Save Post for Later?"

---

## 3. Use Plain and Simple Language

Write as though explaining the topic to a 6th-grade student unfamiliar with it. Simple, conversational language makes your interface accessible to more people, including those with reading difficulties or mental disabilities.

- **Avoid jargon** (specialized or technical language understood only by certain groups).
- **Avoid slang** (very informal language used within a social group).
- **Choose short, simple words** over long, complex ones.
- **Use contractions** (who's, they're, you'll) to sound natural.

**Do:** "Look professional and help your customers find you online by adding a custom domain."
**Don't:** "Look slick and help your customers locate you online by executing a custom domain."

---

## 4. Front-Load Text

Place the most important information at the beginning of headings, lists, and links. Readers scan from left to right down the page; front-loading lets them capture value without reading to the end.

- **Do:** "5 UI design eBooks you should read."
- **Don't:** "You should read these 5 UI design eBooks."

---

## 5. Use the Inverted Pyramid

Structure longer text so the most important information comes first, followed by supporting details, then background. This mirrors journalism practice and ensures people who only skim the first line still get the main point.

- **Heading:** Contains the most important information so most users can act without reading further.
- **Subtext:** Adds supporting detail for those who need it.
- **Background:** Moved to a secondary screen or section to avoid clutter.

---

## 6. Limit Abbreviations and Acronyms

Abbreviations save space but increase cognitive load. Spell out words fully when possible. If you must abbreviate, define the acronym on first use.

- **Do:** "Apartment number"
- **Don't:** "Apt. no."

---

## 7. Limit the Use of UPPERCASE

Uppercase text is harder to read because all uppercase words share the same rectangular shape, forcing letter-by-letter reading instead of word-shape recognition. Reserve uppercase for short, small, bold labels with increased letter spacing where visual differentiation is needed.

- **Do:** Use uppercase sparingly, in bold weight, at a small font size, with extra letter spacing.
- **Don't:** Set headings, body text, or names in all caps.

---

## 8. Break Up Content with Descriptive Headings and Bullets

Split large blocks of text into smaller, grouped sections with descriptive headings. This makes content faster to scan and easier to understand.

- **Do:** Separate features into individual groups, each with its own heading and supporting text.
- **Don't:** Merge all features into a single unbroken paragraph.

---

## 9. Make Headings Descriptive

Headings must convey key points, not just label categories. Many readers scan only headings. Screen reader users navigate by listening to a list of headings, so each heading must make sense out of context.

- **Do:** "Beautiful waterfront location", "Fast check-in experience", "Free secure parking"
- **Don't:** "Location", "Check-in", "Parking"

---

## 10. Avoid "My" and "Your" on Form Labels

Think of the interface as a person speaking to the user. A label reading "My email" ambiguously refers to the interface's email, not the user's. "Your email" is clearer but usually unnecessary. Omit possessives entirely for the simplest result, and stay consistent throughout the interface.

- **Do:** "Email"
- **Don't:** "My email" or mixing "my" and "your" across the same interface.

---

## 11. Use Vocabulary Consistently

Use the same word for the same concept everywhere in the interface. Inconsistent terms (e.g., "cart" vs. "bag", "sign up" vs. "register", "log in" vs. "sign in", "delete" vs. "remove") confuse users and erode trust.

- **Do:** If the button says "Add to cart", label the destination page "Cart".
- **Don't:** Label the button "Add to cart" but call the page "Bag".

---

## 12. Use Numerals for Numbers

Write numbers as numerals (e.g., 899) rather than spelling them out ("eight hundred and ninety nine"). Numerals are easier to scan, faster to read, and more concise.

- **Format consistently:** Use commas for large numbers (1,000 not 1000).
- **Simplify very large numbers:** Write "1 billion" instead of "1,000,000,000" to reduce cognitive load.

---

## 13. Avoid Full Stops (Periods) When Unnecessary

Most UI text is short enough to stand without punctuation. Omit full stops unless the text forms a complete sentence with commas. When you do use them, apply them consistently across all similar elements.

- **Do:** Consistent omission of periods across a set of feature descriptions.
- **Don't:** Adding a period to some items and omitting it on others in the same group.

---

## 14. Match Text Length Across Similar Elements

When multiple blocks of text sit side by side (e.g., feature cards in a row), keep their lengths roughly equal. Aligned, even-length text makes the layout look organized and reduces visual noise.

- **Do:** Write descriptions of similar length for items displayed in a row.
- **Don't:** Have one description that is two lines and another that is five lines in the same row.

---

## 15. Make Text Links Describe Their Destination

Link text must tell the reader where the link leads. Generic labels like "Learn more", "Read more", or "Click here" force users to read surrounding text for context and fail completely for screen reader users who navigate by link lists.

- **Do:** "Explore templates", "Email marketing features", "How affiliates work" -- or turn the heading itself into the link.
- **Don't:** Three identical "Learn more" links in a row.

### Never use "click here"

"Click here" is inaccessible, unhelpful for scanning, and inaccurate for users on touch or voice interfaces. Use the destination page name as the link text instead.

- **Do:** "5 UI design eBooks to improve your interface design skills."
- **Don't:** "Click here for 5 UI design eBooks..."

---

## 16. Write Clear Error Messages

Error messages must tell the user what happened, why, and how to fix it. Poor error messages leave users stuck; clear ones restore momentum.

- **Never blame the user.** Stay positive and helpful.
- **Be concise.** Drop filler like "please", "sorry", and "oops".
- **Avoid robot-speak.** Use clear, conversational language without technical jargon.
- **Make headings and buttons descriptive** so users can act without reading supporting text.

**Do:** Heading: "Payment failed." Body: "Update your payment details and try again." Button: "Update payment details."
**Don't:** Heading: "Oops, something went wrong!" Body: "Your payment wasn't successful as an error occurred." Button: "Ok."
