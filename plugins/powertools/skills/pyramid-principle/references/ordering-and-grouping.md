# Ordering, Grouping, Deduction & Induction

This file covers how ideas relate *within* a grouping: how to order them, how to keep them clean (MECE), and the two ways they can logically connect (deduction vs. induction).

## Logical ordering: every group needs a reason for its sequence

Rule 3 of the pyramid says ideas in a grouping must be logically ordered. There are only three valid bases for that order. If a grouping's sequence doesn't fit one of these, the thinking behind the group is incomplete.

1. **Time / sequential order.** The ideas are steps in a process or links in a cause-and-effect chain. Order them as they occur: first, second, third. Use for action plans, processes, and "how" arguments. *Test: would reordering them break the sequence?*

2. **Structural / spatial order.** The ideas are the parts of a whole — divided by geography, organization, function, or component. Order them the way you'd naturally walk the structure (north to south, largest division to smallest, front of the funnel to back). Use for descriptions of systems, markets, or organizations. *This order demands MECE (see below).*

3. **Degree / comparative order (ranking).** The ideas are a class of similar things ranked by a shared property — importance, size, urgency. Order them most-to-least (almost always lead with the most important). Use for "reasons," "problems," "priorities." *Test: can you state the property they're ranked on?*

If you find a grouping you can't order by any of these three, it's a sign the items aren't actually the same kind of idea — go back and regroup.

## MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)

A clean grouping is **MECE**:
- **Mutually exclusive** — the items don't overlap. Overlap signals fuzzy thinking and makes the reader wonder where a thing belongs.
- **Collectively exhaustive** — together they cover the whole space, with nothing important missing.

MECE matters most for structural groupings (where you're carving up a whole) and for problem analysis. A quick check: if you can name a real item that fits in two of your buckets, you're not mutually exclusive; if you can name a real item that fits in none, you're not collectively exhaustive.

## Deduction vs. Induction

Within a grouping, the ideas connect to their summary in one of two ways. Choosing well is what makes an argument feel either crisp or labored.

### Deduction (the chain)

A deductive argument walks the reader through a sequence where each step follows from the last:

1. A statement about a situation. *(The market is shifting to usage-based pricing.)*
2. A statement about a related situation — usually commenting on the subject or predicate of the first. *(Our flat-rate pricing can't capture the value of high-usage accounts.)*
3. The "therefore" the first two imply. *(Therefore we should move to usage-based pricing.)*

Deduction is natural for the writer and sometimes unavoidable, but it has a cost: to follow it, the reader must hold each step in mind and wait for the conclusion. That's tolerable at the very top of a document but tiring if every level is deductive. **Avoid stacking deductions** — by the third "therefore" the reader is lost.

### Induction (the grouping)

An inductive argument groups ideas that are the *same kind* and gives them a plural label, then states what they collectively imply:

> **We should move to usage-based pricing** *(summary — the implication)*
> - because the market is shifting that way,
> - because our costs now scale with usage,
> - because our biggest accounts are underpaying relative to value. *(three "reasons" — same kind of idea)*

Induction is **clearer for most business writing** and should be your default. The reader sees the point first and the support as parallel items they can scan. The discipline it demands: the items truly must be the same kind (all reasons, here), and the summary must state the genuine implication — not just "there are three reasons."

### Choosing between them

- Default to **induction**. It's easier to read, easier to skim, and forces you to find the real grouping.
- Use **deduction** only where the logic genuinely is a chain (often the single top-level move of a document), and never more than one level deep if you can help it.
- **Never mix** the two inside one grouping. A group is either parallel reasons (induction) or a sequential argument (deduction), not both.

### Converting deduction to induction

Most deductive chains can be flattened into inductive groups, which usually reads better:

- *Deductive:* "Sales are down. When sales are down we lose runway. Therefore we must cut costs."
- *Inductive:* "We must cut costs — sales have dropped 20% and we have nine months of runway left." (The two facts are grouped as the *reasons* the cut is needed.)
