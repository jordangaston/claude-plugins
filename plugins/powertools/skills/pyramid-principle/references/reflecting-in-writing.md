# Reflecting the Pyramid in Writing & Slides

A correct pyramid still fails if the prose hides it. This file covers turning structure into readable output.

## Make the structure visible

The reader should be able to reconstruct your pyramid from the surface of the document:

- **Headings mirror groupings.** Each top-level grouping becomes a heading; each heading states the *point* of its section, not a topic label. "Why onboarding is the right bet" beats "Onboarding analysis."
- **State the answer first in every section,** then support it — the pyramid principle applies recursively, at the document level and within each section.
- **Use visual hierarchy** (indentation, numbering, nesting) so the levels of the pyramid are legible at a glance. In dense documents a numbered "dot-dash" hierarchy (1, 1.1, 1.1.1) makes the structure explicit.
- **Keep groupings small.** Three to five items per group is comfortable; beyond about seven, the reader can't hold the set, which is a sign the group should be split or summarized one level up.

## Transitions carry the Q&A logic

Between sections, write a short bridge that closes the prior question and opens the next — this is the vertical Q&A logic made audible. A transition reminds the reader where they are in the argument:

> "Having seen *why* onboarding drives the churn, the question is *which* fix moves the needle fastest."

For longer documents, open a major section with a miniature SCQA of its own, so the section stands on its own while still nesting in the whole.

## "Tell them, tell them, tell them" — but lightly

The old saw (tell them what you'll say, say it, tell them what you said) is right in spirit and overdone in practice. The pyramid version: state the answer up front (the introduction), support it in the body, and — only for long documents — close with a brief synthesis. Don't pad short documents with a summary that repeats the introduction.

## Slides and decks

Slides are pyramids with a stricter discipline:

- **Each slide's title is its summary line** — a full-sentence assertion (the "So what?"), not a noun phrase. "Q3 results" is a topic; "Q3 margin recovered to 68% after repricing" is a point.
- **The body of the slide supports its own title** with same-kind, ordered items — usually inductive.
- **The sequence of slide titles, read alone, should tell the whole story** (the "horizontal logic" of the deck). If reading just the titles doesn't give the argument, the deck isn't structured.

## Common pitfalls when writing it up

- **Buried lede.** The answer arrives in paragraph six. Move it up.
- **Topic headings instead of point headings.** "Background / Analysis / Recommendation" tells the reader nothing; replace with the actual points.
- **Blank summaries.** A heading or lead sentence that could top any list. Apply the "So what?" test.
- **Order of discovery ≠ order of delivery.** You investigated A then B then C; the reader wants C (the conclusion) first. Don't narrate your process.
- **Smuggling analysis into the introduction.** The intro is a story of what the reader already accepts; the moment it contains a claim the reader would contest, that claim belongs in the body as a supported point.
- **Over-deep deduction.** Chains of "therefore" past one level. Flatten to inductive groupings.
