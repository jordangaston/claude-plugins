# StoryBrand Implementation Guide

Read this file when the user has completed their BrandScript and wants to apply it to real marketing assets.

---

## The One-Liner

The one-liner is a single memorable statement that describes what you do. It follows a simple formula:

**Problem + Solution + Result**

Structure: "Most [customers] struggle with [problem]. Our [product/service] [solution], so they can [result]."

### Examples
- "Most small business owners struggle to be heard online. Our marketing framework clarifies your message so customers actually listen — and your revenue grows."
- "Most parents feel overwhelmed trying to take great photos of their kids. Our online course teaches simple techniques so you capture memories you'll treasure forever."
- "Most retirees worry they'll outlive their savings. Our financial planning service builds a personalized roadmap so you can enjoy retirement without anxiety."

### Rules
- Memorizable — if you can't say it from memory, it's too long
- Should make the listener say "Tell me more" or "I need that"
- Every employee should be able to recite it
- Use it everywhere: email signatures, social bios, networking events, business cards

## Website Wireframe

The BrandScript maps directly to your website's above-the-fold section and page structure. The website is where most customers will first encounter your brand, so it must pass the Grunt Test within 5 seconds.

### Above the Fold (the first screen visitors see)

Must contain:
1. **An image of success** — show the aspirational result, not your building or your team. If you're a resort, show someone relaxed by a pool. If you sell productivity software, show someone looking confident and in control.
2. **A headline** — your promise of value, drawn from Module 1 (Character desire) and Module 7 (Success). Keep it short. Examples: "Find the Luxury and Rest You've Been Looking For." "Schedule Meetings in Minutes, Not Hours."
3. **A direct CTA button** — prominent, contrasting color, clear language ("Get Started," "Schedule a Call," "Buy Now")
4. **A transitional CTA** — secondary, less prominent ("Watch a Demo," "Download the Free Guide")
5. **Minimal text** — the fewer words, the better. Every word must earn its place.

### What NOT to put above the fold
- Your company's founding story
- A photo of your building or headquarters
- Your mission statement
- A rotating carousel of unrelated images
- Jargon or insider language

### Page Structure (scrolling down)

Below the fold, lay out sections in this order:

1. **The Stakes** — briefly describe the problem/pain (Module 2 + Module 6). Agitate gently.
2. **The Value Proposition** — what you offer and how it resolves the problem (Modules 1 + 7). Paint the success picture.
3. **The Plan** — your 3-step process (Module 4). Show how easy it is to engage.
4. **Explanatory Paragraph** — a brief paragraph that empathizes with the customer's situation and positions you as the guide (Module 3). This is where your empathy statement lives.
5. **Testimonials / Authority** — social proof (Module 3 authority). 3 testimonials, relevant stats, client logos.
6. **Another Direct CTA** — repeat the primary call to action
7. **Junk Drawer** — the footer. Links to About, FAQ, Contact, Legal, etc. All the stuff that's important but doesn't need to be above the fold.

### Key Principles
- **Fewer words.** The best websites use minimal copy. If you had 300 words on your entire home page, that would be plenty.
- **Images > text.** Show happy customers using your product. Show the aspirational result.
- **Repeat the CTA.** The direct CTA should appear multiple times as the user scrolls.
- **One focus per page.** Don't offer 47 links. Guide the visitor toward ONE action.

## Email Campaigns

The BrandScript fuels two types of email campaigns:

### Nurture Campaign (Transitional CTA Follow-Up)

When someone opts in via your transitional CTA (downloads a guide, signs up for a webinar), send a nurture sequence:

1. **Email 1: Deliver the asset.** Give them what they asked for. Brief, helpful, no hard sell.
2. **Email 2: Address the problem.** Talk about the villain and the internal frustration (Module 2). Show empathy (Module 3).
3. **Email 3: Position as guide.** Share a testimonial or case study. Demonstrate authority.
4. **Email 4: Show the plan.** Describe how easy it is to work with you (Module 4).
5. **Email 5: Call to action.** Direct CTA. Show the stakes (Module 6) and paint the success (Module 7).

### Sales Campaign

For direct promotional emails:

- **Subject line:** Address the problem or promise the result. Not clever — clear.
- **Opening line:** Empathize with the pain.
- **Body:** Present your solution as the plan. Keep it short.
- **CTA:** One clear action. One button. Repeated if the email is long.
- **P.S. line:** Restate the stakes or the success vision. Many readers skip to the P.S.

### Rules for All Emails
- Short paragraphs (1–3 sentences)
- One CTA per email (don't confuse with multiple asks)
- Write as the guide, not the hero
- Every email should make the customer the hero of the story

## Elevator Pitch

Your BrandScript IS your elevator pitch. Practice this flow:

1. Start with the problem: "You know how [target customer] struggles with [problem]?"
2. Position your solution: "What we do is [solution]."
3. Show the result: "So our customers can [success/transformation]."
4. Call to action: "If you'd like to learn more, [transitional CTA]."

### Example
"You know how most small business owners feel like their marketing is a money pit? What we do is give them a messaging framework that clarifies exactly what they offer and why it matters. Our clients typically see significant revenue growth within months of clarifying their message. If you want, I can send you our free 5-minute messaging audit."

## Transforming Company Culture

The BrandScript's most powerful long-term application: aligning your entire team around the customer's story.

### The Narrative Void
When a company doesn't have a clear story, employees fill the void with their own narratives — usually about internal politics, their own careers, or organizational drama. A BrandScript gives everyone a shared mission.

### Implementation Steps

1. **Post the BrandScript.** Put it on office walls, in onboarding materials, in Slack channels.
2. **Make every employee memorize the one-liner.** From the CEO to the intern. If someone asks "What do you do?" every person should give the same compelling answer.
3. **Align departments.** Each department should know how their work serves the customer's story:
   - Marketing → communicates the BrandScript externally
   - Sales → uses the BrandScript language in conversations
   - Product → builds features that resolve the customer's problems
   - Support → embodies the guide role daily
4. **Hire for the story.** In interviews, look for people who are excited about helping the customer win, not just about the company's prestige.
5. **Repeat relentlessly.** Clarity comes from repetition, not from saying something once. Use the BrandScript language in all-hands meetings, quarterly reviews, and daily standups.

### The Test
Stop any employee in the hallway and ask:
- "What does our customer want?"
- "What's their biggest problem?"
- "What do we offer them?"
- "What happens if they don't engage us?"
- "What does their life look like after working with us?"

If they can answer all five, your culture is aligned.

## Sub-BrandScripts

Most businesses need more than one BrandScript:

1. **Overall Brand BrandScript** — the master narrative (do this first)
2. **Division BrandScripts** — one per major business unit
3. **Product BrandScripts** — one per product or service line
4. **Audience BrandScripts** — one per customer segment (if segments have meaningfully different desires/problems)

Each sub-BrandScript follows the same SB7 structure but narrows the focus. The overall Brand BrandScript is the umbrella; sub-BrandScripts are the spokes.

## Quick-Start Checklist

After completing the BrandScript, prioritize implementation in this order:

1. ☐ Write your one-liner
2. ☐ Rebuild your website above-the-fold section
3. ☐ Create or revise your direct and transitional CTAs
4. ☐ Set up a nurture email sequence for your transitional CTA
5. ☐ Train your team on the one-liner and BrandScript language
6. ☐ Audit all existing marketing materials against the BrandScript
7. ☐ Create a lead-generating PDF or resource (transitional CTA asset)
8. ☐ Revise your email signature with the one-liner
9. ☐ Update social media profiles with BrandScript language
10. ☐ Build sub-BrandScripts for divisions/products as needed
