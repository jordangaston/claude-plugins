# SB7 Deep Dive: Module-by-Module Reference

Read this file when working through each module of the BrandScript with a user. It contains detailed guidance, examples, and common pitfalls for each of the 7 elements.

---

## Module 1: Character (The Hero = Your Customer)

### Why This Matters
A story doesn't engage an audience until the hero wants something. The desire opens a "story gap" — an unresolved tension the audience needs to see closed. Without a defined desire, there is no story, and the customer has no reason to pay attention.

### The Survival Connection
The desire must connect to one of these primitive survival mechanisms:
- **Conserving financial resources** — saving money (Walmart: "Save Money. Live Better")
- **Conserving time** — reclaiming hours for what matters
- **Building social networks** — finding community, belonging, tribe
- **Gaining status** — projecting power, prestige, refinement (Rolex, Mercedes)
- **Accumulating resources** — making money, increasing productivity
- **The desire to be generous** — helping others survive too
- **The desire for meaning** — participating in something larger than oneself

### How to Choose
1. List everything your customer might want from you
2. Find the ONE desire that encompasses the broadest swath of your offering
3. Verify it connects to survival (if you can't trace it back, it won't compel action)
4. State it simply — short enough to repeat, clear enough to remember

### Examples
| Business | Customer Desire |
|----------|----------------|
| Financial Advisor | "A Plan for Your Retirement" |
| College Alumni Association | "Leave a Meaningful Legacy" |
| Fine-Dining Restaurant | "A Meal Everybody Will Remember" |
| Real Estate Agent | "The Home You've Dreamed About" |
| Bookstore | "A Story to Get Lost In" |
| Breakfast Bars | "A Healthy Start to Your Day" |
| Landscaper | "A Yard That Looks Better Than Your Neighbor's" |
| Caterer | "A Mobile Fine-Dining Experience in the Environment of Your Choice" |
| University | "A Hassle-Free MBA You Can Complete After Work" |

### Common Mistakes
- **Too many desires.** One BrandScript = one desire. Create sub-BrandScripts for each division or product line.
- **Desire isn't related to survival.** "Inhale knowledge, exhale success" sounds nice but means nothing concrete. "Helping you become everyone's favorite leader" taps into status, tribe, and meaning.
- **Too vague.** The desire must be specific enough that the customer can picture it.

### Hitchhiker Test
Imagine your customer is a hitchhiker and you pull over. The one question burning in their mind: "Where are you going?" If you start talking about your mission statement or your grandfather's legacy, they'll walk away. They just want to know: can you take me to San Francisco?

## Module 2: Problem (The Hook)

### Why This Matters
The problem is the hook. It deepens interest. The more you talk about the problems your customers face, the more they'll lean in. If you resolve the problem too early (or never define it), the audience checks out.

### The Villain
Every story needs an antagonist. The villain doesn't have to be a person — it can be a force, a situation, or a philosophy — but it should have personified characteristics.

**Four rules for a good villain:**
1. Must be a ROOT SOURCE (not a symptom — "frustration" is what a villain causes, not the villain itself)
2. Must be RELATABLE (customers immediately recognize it as something they disdain)
3. Must be SINGULAR (one villain per BrandScript — too many villains = lack of clarity)
4. Must be REAL (don't fearmonger — there are plenty of actual villains to fight)

### The Three Levels of Problems

The villain creates problems on three levels. All three should appear in your BrandScript:

**External Problem (tangible/physical)**
- The surface-level thing your product solves
- Easy to identify — it's usually what you literally do
- Examples: leaky pipe (plumber), hunger (restaurant), unsightly home (painter)

**Internal Problem (emotional/frustration)**
- How the external problem makes the customer FEEL
- This is the real motivator for purchasing decisions
- The universal hero question: "Do I have what it takes?"
- Examples: embarrassment, self-doubt, intimidation, overwhelm, frustration, feeling cheated

**Philosophical Problem (the deeper "why")**
- Why this matters in the larger story of humanity
- Uses "ought" and "shouldn't" language
- Examples: "People shouldn't have to fight with their software." "Everyone deserves clean water." "Bad people shouldn't be allowed to win."

### Worked Examples

**Tesla:**
- Villain: Gas-guzzling, inferior technology
- External: I need a car
- Internal: I want to be an early adopter of new technology
- Philosophical: My choice of car ought to help save the environment

**CarMax:**
- Villain: Predatory used-car dealers
- External: I need a used car
- Internal: I don't want to feel lied to or worked over
- Philosophical: Buying a car shouldn't be an adversarial experience

**Nespresso:**
- Villain: Coffee machines that make bad coffee
- External: I want better-tasting coffee at home
- Internal: I want my coffee machine to make me feel sophisticated
- Philosophical: I shouldn't have to be a barista to make a gourmet coffee at home

### The Key Insight
Companies tend to sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to internal problems. Apple didn't win by selling better computers — they won by resolving the intimidation people felt around technology. CarMax doesn't advertise cars — they advertise the relief of not dealing with a sleazy salesman.

If you only address external problems, you leave enormous value on the table.

## Module 3: Guide (Your Brand = Yoda, Not Luke)

### Why This Matters
If the hero could solve their own problem, they wouldn't be in trouble. Every hero needs a guide. In movies: Yoda for Luke, Haymitch for Katniss, Gandalf for Frodo, Lionel Logue for King George VI.

**The fatal mistake:** positioning your brand as the hero. Heroes are weak, self-doubting, and looking for help. Guides have "been there and done that." The story is always ABOUT the hero, but the guide has the authority.

### The Two Characteristics

**1. Empathy (builds trust)**
- Show you understand the customer's pain
- Phrases: "We understand how it feels to...", "Nobody should have to experience...", "Like you, we are frustrated by..."
- Maps to Amy Cuddy's first impression question: "Can I trust this person?"
- Think of it as: "I see you. I hear you. I understand."

**2. Authority (earns respect)**
- Demonstrate competence without bragging
- Four tools:
  - **Testimonials** — Let others do the talking. 3 is ideal. Keep them brief.
  - **Statistics** — "125,000 users trust our software." Concrete numbers.
  - **Awards** — Small logos at the bottom of a page. No fanfare needed.
  - **Logos** — Of businesses you've helped. Social proof.
- Maps to Cuddy's second question: "Can I respect this person?"

### The Balance
Lead with empathy, support with authority. Too much authority without empathy = arrogant know-it-all. Too much empathy without authority = well-meaning but incompetent (imagine a nutritionist who says "I want to lose 30 pounds too!").

### The Tidal Cautionary Tale
Jay Z launched Tidal music streaming by putting artists on stage talking about how artists deserve more money. Social media destroyed it. The mistake: he made artists the heroes instead of customers. He never answered the one question every customer asks: "How are you helping ME win the day?"

## Module 4: Plan (The Bridge)

### Why This Matters
Even when customers like and trust you, making a purchase feels risky. They're standing at the edge of a rushing creek, hearing a waterfall downstream. The plan places stepping stones in the creek so they can cross safely.

### Two Types of Plans

**Process Plan (removes confusion)**
- 3–6 simple steps showing how to engage
- Can be pre-purchase, post-purchase, or combined
- Makes the complex feel simple

Pre-purchase example:
1. Schedule an appointment
2. We create a customized plan
3. We execute the plan together

Post-purchase example:
1. Download the software
2. Integrate your database
3. Revolutionize your customer interaction

Combined example:
1. Test-drive a car
2. Purchase the car
3. Enjoy free maintenance for life

**Agreement Plan (removes fear)**
- A list of commitments that address the customer's specific fears
- Works like a guarantee or quality promise
- CarMax example: no haggling, no commission-based salespeople, quality certification, inspection on every car

### Naming Your Plan
Give plans a title that increases perceived value:
- "The Easy Installation Plan"
- "Our Quality Guarantee"
- "The World's Best Night's Sleep Plan"
- "Customer Satisfaction Agreement"

### Rules
- Process plans: 3–6 steps max. If your process has 30 steps, group them into phases.
- Agreement plans: list every fear the customer might have, then counter each with a commitment.
- Every brand should have at least a process plan. Agreement plans are a powerful bonus.

## Module 5: Call to Action (The Challenge)

### Why This Matters
Characters in stories don't act on their own — they must be challenged by an outside force. The same is true for customers. If you don't ask, they won't buy.

### Two Types of CTAs

**Direct CTA (the proposal)**
- Asks for the sale, the appointment, the registration
- Must be clear, bold, and repeated throughout your marketing
- Examples: "Buy Now," "Schedule a Call," "Register Today," "Get Started"
- Should be the most prominent visual element on your website — typically a button in a contrasting color
- Think of it like a marriage proposal: confident, clear, not tentative

**Transitional CTA (the coffee date)**
- Offers value in exchange for the relationship deepening
- Positions you as the guide by providing something helpful for free
- Examples: free PDF guide, webinar, trial period, email course, free samples, educational video
- Builds reciprocity and trust over time
- Think of it like asking someone out for coffee before proposing marriage

### Rules
- Every page of your website should have a direct CTA
- Transitional CTAs should be available but visually secondary
- The direct CTA button should appear in the top right of your navigation AND in the center of your hero section
- Don't be shy. You're not being pushy; you're being clear.

## Module 6: Failure (The Stakes)

### Why This Matters
If nothing is at stake, there is no story. If there's no cost to NOT buying your product, customers have no urgency to act. Stakes are the engine of engagement.

### The Salt Metaphor
Failure is like salt in a recipe:
- Too much = fearmongering, turns people off
- Too little = bland, no urgency
- Just right = compelling, creates healthy tension

### How to Define Stakes
Ask: "If my customer does NOT engage my brand, what negative things will happen to them?"

Categories of failure:
- Financial loss or waste
- Continued frustration or stress
- Missed opportunities
- Embarrassment or status loss
- Wasted time
- Deteriorating relationships or health
- Falling behind competitors

### Examples
- Wendy's "Where's the beef?" = you'll get a wimpy sandwich elsewhere
- Whole Foods = you'll eat overly processed food
- Insurance company = financial devastation if the unexpected happens
- Time management software = distractions will continue stealing your potential

### Practical Tip
In your marketing, you don't need a whole page about failure. A few sentences or a short section is enough. Just make sure the customer understands the cost of inaction.

## Module 7: Success (The Resolution)

### Why This Matters
You must paint the picture. Never assume customers know how their lives will improve. Close the story gap opened in Module 1 by showing them the "after" state.

### Three Forms of Success Resolution

**1. Winning Power or Position**
- Status, authority, resources, access
- The hero gains something tangible in the world
- Example: "You'll be the go-to expert in your field"

**2. Union That Makes the Hero Whole**
- Completeness, reduced anxiety, belonging, acceptance
- Something that was missing is now present
- Example: "Finally feel at peace about your finances"

**3. Self-Realization / Transformation**
- The hero becomes a better version of themselves
- The aspirational identity is achieved
- Example: "From overwhelmed parent to confident family photographer"

### How to Paint the Picture
Be specific and vivid. Don't just say "your life will be better." Show them:
- What does their morning look like?
- How do they feel?
- What do other people say about them?
- What have they achieved?

### Before/After Grid
A useful exercise: create a simple before/after for each dimension:

| Dimension | Before Your Brand | After Your Brand |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| What they have | | |
| How they feel | | |
| Their average day | | |
| Their status | | |

### Examples
- Apple: You express yourself and are heard
- Weight Watchers: You lose weight and feel great
- Men's Wearhouse: "You're going to like the way you look"
- Reagan: "A shining city on a hill"

### The Transformation
The most powerful version of success is identity transformation. People don't just want to solve a problem — they want to become someone new. The best brands participate in their customer's transformation.

Ask: "Who does our customer want to become? And how does engaging our brand help them get there?"
