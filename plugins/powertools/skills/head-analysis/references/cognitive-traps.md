# HEAD Analysis — Cognitive Trap Catalog

Use this reference during Phase 8 (Trap Scan) and throughout the analysis process. For each trap, there is a description, detection pattern, and mitigation.

---

## Category 1: Narrative and Coherence Traps

### Narrative Coherence Bias
**What it is:** Experts assemble information into a clean, coherent story. Because the story makes sense, it feels true. But the coherence hides information gaps — you don't notice what's missing because the story works without it.

**Detection:** Ask "Is this analysis suspiciously clean? Does everything fit a little too neatly?" Review your red and yellow drivers — if the conclusion seems solid despite weak drivers, the narrative may be papering over gaps.

**Mitigation:** Deliberately list what you DON'T know. For each gap, ask: "Would knowing this change my conclusion?" If yes, the narrative is premature.

---

## Category 2: Information Weighting Traps

### Availability Bias
**What it is:** You give more weight to information you received recently or that is vivid/memorable, regardless of whether it's the most relevant or representative.

**Detection:** Ask "When did I learn the most influential pieces of evidence? Am I overweighting the last thing I read or the most dramatic data point?" Check if older, less dramatic data contradicts your recent impressions.

**Mitigation:** Chronologically list your evidence and check whether older data is underrepresented in your conclusion. Weight evidence by relevance and quality, not by recency or emotional impact.

### Anchoring Bias
**What it is:** The first number, estimate, or framing you encounter disproportionately shapes all subsequent analysis. In negotiations, the first price sets the range. In analysis, the first data point sets expectations.

**Detection:** Identify the first piece of information you encountered on this topic. Ask "If I had started with a completely different piece of data, would I reach the same conclusion?"

**Mitigation:** Deliberately start your analysis from a different data point and see if you reach the same place. If not, your anchor was distorting your conclusion.

### Halo Effect
**What it is:** A strong positive impression in one area creates a positive distortion across all other areas. First love makes everything about the person seem wonderful; a company with great PR gets benefit of the doubt on governance.

**Detection:** Ask "Am I giving this option/entity/person a pass on weak drivers because they're strong on other drivers?" Check: are your assessments for different drivers suspiciously correlated (all good or all bad)?

**Mitigation:** Evaluate each driver independently. Cover the other driver assessments while evaluating each one. Would this driver's assessment change if it belonged to a different entity?

## Category 3: Sampling and Evidence Traps

### Sampling Bias
**What it is:** Drawing conclusions from a sample that is too small, too narrow, or unrepresentative. Often the sample is just personal experience.

**Detection:** Ask "How many data points support this assessment? Who or what is NOT represented in my sample?" Check if your evidence comes from a narrow slice of the relevant population — one geography, one time period, one type of source.

**Mitigation:** Explicitly state your sample size and composition. Identify what's missing. Ask "If I included [missing population/timeframe/source], would the picture change?" If you only have personal experience, acknowledge that as a sample of one.

### Survivorship Bias
**What it is:** Analyzing only the successes (survivors) and ignoring failures. Leads to conclusions that overstate the likelihood of success and misidentify the causes.

**Detection:** Ask "Am I only looking at cases that succeeded/existed/survived? What about the ones that failed/disappeared/were excluded?"

**Mitigation:** Deliberately seek out failure cases. If analyzing market entry strategy by studying successful companies, also study the ones that entered and failed. What was different?

## Category 4: Self-Perception Traps

### Superiority Bias
**What it is:** Giving more weight to people who agree with your analysis than to those who disagree. You unconsciously treat agreement as evidence of competence and disagreement as evidence of ignorance.

**Detection:** Ask "Am I dismissing any dissenting views? If someone who agreed with me had made the same argument as a dissenter, would I take it more seriously?"

**Mitigation:** Steel-man every disagreement. State the strongest version of the opposing view before evaluating it. If you can't state it charitably, you don't understand it well enough to dismiss it.

### Confirmation Bias
**What it is:** Seeking facts that support your existing beliefs and rejecting, de-emphasizing, or not seeking facts that contradict them. The single most pervasive analytical trap.

**Detection:** Ask "Did I actively search for evidence that my conclusion is wrong? Can I list three strong pieces of counter-evidence?" If you can't, you probably didn't look hard enough.

**Mitigation:** Before finalizing any conclusion, spend dedicated time seeking disconfirming evidence. Assign someone (or a phase of your analysis) specifically to this task. Treat disconfirming evidence as MORE informative than confirming evidence — it tells you where your model is wrong.

### Reasonable Person Bias
**What it is:** Assuming others think the way you do. Assuming that because something seems rational to you, others will also see it that way. Even when people act rationally, they reason from different premises, values, and information.

**Detection:** Ask "Am I assuming the other party/actor is reasoning from the same premises and values I am? What if their priorities are completely different from mine?"

**Mitigation:** Explicitly state the assumptions you're making about other actors' reasoning. For each assumption, ask: "Is this an observation or a projection?" Interview or research how the other party actually frames the situation.

### Variable Bias
**What it is:** Focusing on only a couple of variables and ignoring others because simplification feels manageable. The desire to simplify is human but narrowing your focus too much generates misunderstanding.

**Detection:** Count how many variables your conclusion depends on. If it's fewer than 3, you're probably oversimplifying. Ask "What variables did I initially consider but then drop? Why did I drop them?"

**Mitigation:** Return to your driver list. If your conclusion only draws on 2–3 drivers, explicitly assess whether the remaining drivers could alter it. Sometimes simplification is warranted — but document the choice and its risks.

## Category 5: Time-Related Traps

### Anchoring to Initial Numbers
**What it is:** In negotiations, sales, or any sequential data encounter, the first number introduced serves as an anchor for all subsequent discussion, even if it has no analytical basis.

**Detection:** Identify the first quantitative data point in your analysis. Ask "Is my range of estimates clustered around this number? Would I estimate differently if I hadn't seen it first?"

**Mitigation:** Generate estimates independently before looking at anchor numbers. Use multiple independent estimation methods and compare them.

### Predictive Bias
**What it is:** Confusing expertise about the current state with ability to predict the future. Being an expert on how things ARE does not make you an expert on how things WILL BE. Current-state analysis and future forecasting are different skills.

**Detection:** Ask "Am I claiming to know what WILL happen, or what IS happening? If I'm forecasting, am I applying the same rigor as I did to current-state analysis?"

**Mitigation:** Explicitly separate current-state assessment from future forecasting in your analysis. For forecasts, use scenario analysis (multiple possible futures) rather than single-point predictions. Assign confidence levels to forecasts that are lower than current-state assessments.

### Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc
**What it is:** "After this, therefore because of this." Mistaking temporal sequence for causation. Because A happened before B, you assume A caused B — but you need more analysis to establish causation.

**Detection:** Ask "Am I claiming causation based on sequence? What other factors changed at the same time? Could B have happened without A?"

**Mitigation:** For every causal claim, identify at least one alternative explanation. Check: did A consistently precede B across multiple instances, or just once? Look for confounding variables that changed simultaneously.
