# Step 4: Build a Great Workplan

A good workplan saves enormous wasted effort. The key principles:
- **No analysis without a hypothesis.** Never build a model without knowing what question it answers.
- **Dummy the output.** Visualize what form the end product might take — if you wouldn't want it when you had it, don't do it.
- **Order logically.** Knock-out analyses first, then priority analyses, then nice-to-haves.
- **Be painfully explicit.** Who does what analysis by when.

## Workplan Worksheet

For each priority branch of the pruned logic tree:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Issue** | The specific question from the logic tree |
| **Hypothesis** | Your best current guess at the answer |
| **Analysis Required** | What analytical work will test this hypothesis |
| **Data/Source** | Where does the data come from? |
| **Output Format** | What does the end product look like? (Chart, table, calculation) |
| **Responsibility** | Who will do this work? |
| **Due Date** | When is it needed? |

## Chunky Workplans + Lean Project Plans

Avoid 30-page encyclopedic workplans that become outdated immediately. Instead:

**Chunky Workplans**: Short, highly specific, covering 2-3 weeks of the most important analyses. Constantly revised as insights emerge.

**Lean Project Plans**: Rougher Gantt-chart format plans that capture fixed milestones and ensure the overall project stays on track from a time perspective.

## One-Day Answers

At any point in the process, articulate your current best understanding in three parts:

### Situation-Observation-Resolution (S-O-R)

1. **Situation**: The state of affairs at the outset. Sets up the problem.
2. **Observation/Complication**: What changed, or what tension exists. Captures the dynamic that created the problem.
3. **Resolution**: Best current idea of what to do. Early on, this is rough and speculative. Later, it becomes refined.

### One-Day Answer Best Practices
- Keep it crisp and concise — not 40 bullet points of unorganized facts
- Use it as a strawman to attack and stress-test
- Update it as each analysis completes
- It's your "elevator test" answer — can you explain it in 30 seconds?

### Example: Hardware Competition

> **Situation**: Hechinger is a dominant regional hardware retailer seeking to expand.
>
> **Complication**: A new competitor, Home Depot, has emerged with a warehouse superstore model growing much faster, driven by 15-18% lower pricing. Their business model compensates for lower prices through sourcing economies, lower-cost logistics, and higher asset productivity. They will soon overlap geographically.
>
> **Resolution**: To remain competitive, Hechinger needs to quickly reform inventory management and logistics systems, and develop lower-cost sourcing models to enable lower pricing — or it will face an existential threat even in core markets.

## Analysis Ordering

```
1. KNOCK-OUT ANALYSES → Can we eliminate entire branches?
2. PRIORITY ANALYSES  → The big levers we can move
3. NICE-TO-HAVE       → Important but not decision-changing
```

The order matters enormously. If Rob's solar payback was >10 years, there was no need to analyze panel cost trajectories or carbon footprint reduction.

## Tips for Helping Users Build Workplans

- Help them convert each priority branch into a testable hypothesis
- Ask: "What would the output look like if you had it?" (dummy the chart)
- Identify knock-out tests and put them first
- Keep the plan short and specific — resist the urge to be comprehensive
- Schedule a checkpoint to update the one-day answer after the first round of analysis
