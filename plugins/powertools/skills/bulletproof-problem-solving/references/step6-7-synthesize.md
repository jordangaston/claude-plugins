# Steps 6-7: Synthesize Findings and Tell a Compelling Story

Most problem solving fails not at the analysis stage but at the point where findings must be synthesized and communicated. Analytically-oriented teams often say "We're done" when analysis is complete, without thinking about how to synthesize and communicate to diverse audiences.

## Step 6: Synthesis

Synthesis brings together all separate pieces of analytical work and often yields new insights you didn't notice when in the weeds of analysis.

### From Analysis to Insight
1. **Lay out all findings.** Revisit each branch of the tree and what you learned.
2. **Look for patterns across branches.** Do findings from different analyses reinforce or contradict each other?
3. **Identify the "so whats."** For each finding, ask: What does this mean for the decision?
4. **Update the one-day answer.** Refine the Situation-Observation-Resolution with actual findings.
5. **Revisit the original problem statement.** Can you now answer the decision maker's question: "What should I do?"

### Reasoning Toward Implications
The evolution of reasoning through the process:

```
Early:   Observation вҶ’ "We think..." (hypothesis)
Middle:  Analysis вҶ’ "The data shows..." (finding)
Late:    Synthesis вҶ’ "Therefore you should..." (implication)
Final:   Story вҶ’ "Here's why and how..." (compelling narrative for action)
```

## Step 7: Tell a Compelling Story

### Avoid the "Anxious Parade of Knowledge"
The biggest storytelling mistake: presenting every analysis in the order you did it, without a governing narrative. This overwhelms audiences and buries the insight.

### Key Storytelling Structures

**1. Lead with the Answer (Pyramid)**
Best for: Receptive audiences, time-pressured settings.
Structure: "You should do X. Here's why: Reason A, Reason B, Reason C. Here's the evidence for each."

**2. Situation-Complication-Resolution**
Best for: Building the case, audiences that need context.
Structure: "Here's the situation. Here's what changed. Here's what to do about it."

**3. Decision Tree Reveal**
Best for: Difficult conclusions, resistant audiences.
Structure: Walk the decision maker through the evidence for each yes/no branch, slowly building toward the conclusion. You reveal the answer rather than leading with it.

### Arguments: Deductive vs. Inductive

**Deductive** (top-down): General principle вҶ’ Specific case вҶ’ Conclusion.
"All companies in declining markets must innovate. We're in a declining market. Therefore, we must innovate."

**Inductive** (bottom-up): Specific observations вҶ’ General conclusion.
"Company A innovated and survived. Company B innovated and survived. Company C didn't and failed. Innovation is critical in our situation."

Choose based on audience: Deductive is cleaner and faster. Inductive builds more carefully and is better when the audience is skeptical.

### Storyboarding

Before building slides or writing a document:
1. **Write the governing thought** вҖ” one sentence that answers the problem.
2. **Outline 3-5 supporting arguments** вҖ” each becomes a section.
3. **For each argument, identify the key exhibit/chart** that provides evidence.
4. **Write slide leads as arguments** вҖ” not boring titles like "Market Analysis" but active statements like "Market is shifting toward low-price competitors at 15% annual rate."
5. **Read across the leads** вҖ” do they tell a coherent story? (Horizontal logic check)
6. **Check vertical logic** вҖ” does the evidence under each lead actually support it?

### Data Visualization Principles

- Choose chart types that make the insight obvious
- Remove clutter вҖ” no 3D effects, gridlines, or decoration
- Use color purposefully to highlight the key data point
- Label directly on the chart, not in a legend
- Write an active title that states the insight, not just the topic

## Tips for Helping Users Synthesize and Tell Their Story

- Ask: "If you could only tell the decision maker one thing, what would it be?" вҖ” that's the governing thought
- Help them resist the urge to show all their work вҖ” curate ruthlessly
- Draft the S-O-R one-day answer as the narrative backbone
- If the conclusion is difficult, suggest the decision-tree reveal structure
- Challenge: "Read me just the headlines of your slides/sections вҖ” do they tell a complete story?"
- Remind them: The problem is not solved when the analysis is done. It's solved when the decision maker acts.
