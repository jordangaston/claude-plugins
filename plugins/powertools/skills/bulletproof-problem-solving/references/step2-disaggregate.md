# Step 2: Disaggregate the Problem (Logic Trees)

Good problem disaggregation is at the heart of the seven-step process. Any problem of real consequence is too complicated to solve without breaking it into logical parts that reveal drivers, causes, and potential solution paths. When you get good at cleaving problems apart, insights come quickly.

## Getting Started

You don't need expertise to begin — do a quick literature review, brainstorm the elements, and sketch a first-cut tree. Start with a large piece of paper, whiteboard, or sticky notes. Trial and error is expected. **Only do enough initial research to generate a first-cut tree** — the tree structure will guide further research more efficiently.

## Types of Logic Trees

### Component / Factor Trees
**When to use**: Early on when you don't know much about the problem. Just list the obvious factors.
**Example**: Factors affecting salmon populations → freshwater habitat, ocean conditions, fisheries, hatcheries, policy, consumer behavior.
**Limitations**: Doesn't yield strong hypotheses. Use as a stepping stone.

### Deductive Logic Trees
**When to use**: When you know the logical/mathematical structure of the problem. Branches add up mathematically to the objective.
**Structure**: General principle → specific data → conclusion.
**Example**: Airport capacity = Supply (runways × capacity × utilization) - Demand (regional share × growth).
**Key use**: Business problems with return on capital, profit levers, or supply/demand math.

### Inductive Logic Trees
**When to use**: When you know more about specific cases than general principles. Work backward from observations toward theories.
**Structure**: Observation + Observation + Observation → General assertion.
**Example**: Multiple historical figures assessed → general principles for contested legacies emerge.
**Caution**: Shows probabilistic relationships, not causal ones.

### Hypothesis Trees
**When to use**: After some iteration, when you can state active, testable hypotheses on each branch.
**Key shift**: Move from vague labels ("market factors") to specific hypotheses ("Market share will decline by 15% if competitor enters at 20% lower price").
**Benefit**: Drives analysis and action — vague labels don't.

### Decision Trees
**When to use**: When the problem involves a series of binary choices leading to different actions.
**Structure**: Yes/No branching at each node leading to recommended actions.
**Example**: Should I install solar? → Is payback <10 years? (Yes/No) → Are costs still declining fast? (Yes/No) → etc.

## The MECE Principle

**Mutually Exclusive**: Branches don't overlap. Each concept is self-contained, not spread across branches.
**Collectively Exhaustive**: Taken together, branches cover ALL elements of the problem.

### Checking MECE
- Can any item fit on more than one branch? (If yes, not mutually exclusive)
- Is anything missing? (If yes, not collectively exhaustive)
- If hypotheses aren't yielding insight, the tree is probably not MECE

### Common MECE Violations
- Government policy shown as a separate branch when it actually affects every other branch
- Revenue split by product but missing a channel (e.g., online)
- Overlapping categories (e.g., "marketing" and "customer acquisition" as separate branches)

## Cleaving Frames (Advanced Disaggregation)

Expert problem solvers apply theoretical frameworks to cleave problems more elegantly. Try multiple frames — different slices yield different insights.

### Business Frames
| Frame | When to Use | Key Questions |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)** | Almost any business performance problem | What drives margin? What drives asset productivity? |
| **Price/Volume** | Revenue and competitive dynamics | Differentiated vs commodity? Elastic vs inelastic? |
| **Principal/Agent** | Contracting, incentive alignment | How to align incentives when principal can't fully observe agent? |
| **Assets/Options** | Investment decisions, M&A | What future strategic moves does this asset enable? |
| **Collaborate/Compete** | Competitive strategy | Where to compete head-on vs. where to cooperate? |

### Policy/Societal Frames
| Frame | When to Use | Key Questions |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| **Regulate/Incent** | Policy design | Legal mandate vs. tax/subsidy/nudge? |
| **Equality/Liberty** | Social policy tradeoffs | More equality vs. more individual freedom? |
| **Mitigate/Adapt** | Climate, risk management | Reduce the cause vs. adjust to the effect? |
| **Supply/Demand** | Resource allocation | Get more supply vs. reduce demand? |
| **Incidence/Severity** | Health, safety, risk | Is the problem frequency or magnitude? |

### Personal Frames
| Frame | When to Use |
|-------|-------------|
| **Financial/Non-financial** | Major life decisions (moving, career change) |
| **Near-term/Long-term** | Education, investment, career planning |
| **Work/Play** | Retirement, work-life balance decisions |

## Tree Construction Process

1. **Start with what you know.** Write components on sticky notes.
2. **Group and arrange.** Move notes around until a logical hierarchy emerges.
3. **Try 2-3 alternative disaggregations.** Different cuts reveal different insights.
4. **Check MECE.** Fix overlaps and gaps.
5. **Move from components to hypotheses.** Replace vague labels with testable statements.
6. **Try a cleaving frame.** Apply a theoretical lens and see if it cracks the problem faster.
7. **Do back-of-envelope math.** Confirm the chosen frame yields insight worth pursuing.

## Tips for Helping Users Build Trees

- Encourage visual thinking — sketch it out, don't just discuss it
- Start simple; sophistication comes with iteration
- If the user is stuck, suggest a cleaving frame to try
- For business problems, **always try ROIC or profit lever trees first**
- Remind the user: "We'll prune this in the next step — right now, get it all down"
- Generate the tree as a markdown outline, mermaid diagram, or structured artifact the user can edit
