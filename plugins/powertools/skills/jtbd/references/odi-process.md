# ODI Process & Universal Job Map — Deep Reference

Based on Anthony Ulwick's "Jobs to be Done: Theory to Practice" (2016).

## The Universal Job Map

All jobs consist of some or all of 8 fundamental process steps. This framework is the backbone for uncovering desired outcomes.

### The 8 Steps

| Step | Goal | Sub-activities | Example (Listen to Music) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. DEFINE** | Plan the approach, determine goals, select resources | Plan, Select, Determine | Decide what genre/mood to listen to |
| **2. LOCATE** | Gather materials and information needed | Gather, Access, Receive | Find the songs or playlist |
| **3. PREPARE** | Set up the environment and organize inputs | Set Up, Organize, Examine | Queue up songs, set up speakers |
| **4. CONFIRM** | Verify readiness before executing | Validate, Prioritize, Decide | Confirm the playlist order is right |
| **5. EXECUTE** | Carry out the core task | Perform, Transact, Administer | Play the music |
| **6. MONITOR** | Track progress and results | Verify, Track, Check | Check volume levels, sound quality |
| **7. MODIFY** | Make adjustments as needed | Update, Adjust, Maintain | Skip a song, adjust volume |
| **8. CONCLUDE** | Finish up, store, close out | Store, Finish, Close | Save the playlist, turn off |

### Job Map Rules
- Describes what the customer is **trying to get done**, not what they are **doing** (needs view, not solution view)
- Is NOT a customer journey or experience map (those describe buying/using the product, not the underlying job)
- Must be accurate for ALL customer situations, regardless of the products being used
- Represents the ideal process flow with steps in optimal order
- Is solution-agnostic

### Example: Cut Wood in a Straight Line (Bosch)
1. **Define** — Determine what type of cut to make (rip, crosscut, bevel)
2. **Locate** — Gather the wood and saw
3. **Prepare** — Mark the cut line, set blade height and angle
4. **Confirm** — Verify the cut line is correct, blade is properly set
5. **Execute** — Guide the blade along the cut line
6. **Monitor** — Ensure the cut stays on track
7. **Modify** — Adjust if the cut drifts off line
8. **Conclude** — Complete the cut, secure the saw

### Strategic Value of the Job Map
- Lays out the **long-term strategy**: devise a solution that gets the entire job done on a single platform
- Points out **holes and inefficiencies** in existing offerings
- Serves as a **framework for capturing desired outcomes** (capture outcomes for each step)

## The 10-Step ODI Process

### I. Define the Customer
Identify the three key customer types:
1. **Core Job Executor** — person who uses the product to get the core functional job done
2. **Product Lifecycle Support Team** — people who install, maintain, repair, etc.
3. **Purchase Decision Maker** — person who seeks out, evaluates, and decides what to buy

### II. Define the Job-to-be-Done
Define the core functional job correctly:
- Format: verb + object + contextual clarifier
- Not too narrow (just one step) or too broad (impossible to tackle)
- Purely functional — no emotional or outcome statements mixed in
- From the customer's perspective, not the company's

### III. Uncover Customer Needs
1. Create the job map using the Universal Job Map framework
2. Capture desired outcome statements for each step (50-150 typical)
3. Capture related jobs (5-20)
4. Capture emotional/social jobs (5-25)
5. Capture consumption chain job outcomes
6. Capture buyer's financial desired outcomes (40-80)

### IV. Find Segments of Opportunity
Use Outcome-Based Segmentation:
1. Survey 180-3,000 customers on importance and satisfaction for each outcome
2. Apply factor analysis and cluster analysis to discover segments with unique unmet needs
3. Profile each segment to understand what makes them struggle more than others

### V. Define the Value Proposition
Align company strengths with the segment's top unmet outcomes:
1. Know WHERE in the job customers are underserved
2. Define a value proposition that communicates your intent to satisfy those needs
3. Fulfill the promise: point out existing strengths → accelerate pipeline features → invent new features

Example: Coloplast wound care — discovered nurses' top unmet needs were about "preventing complications" not "healing faster." Changed messaging without changing products → double-digit growth in <6 months.

### VI. Conduct the Competitive Analysis
Evaluate competing offerings against desired outcomes (not feature-to-feature "speeds and feeds"):
- Reveals which offerings get the job done better/worse
- Pinpoints which outcomes to address to offset competitor strengths
- Reveals underserved outcomes for leapfrogging all competitors
- Identifies where competitors have costly features addressing overserved outcomes (wasteful)

### VII. Formulate the Innovation Strategy
Using the Growth Strategy Matrix, decide:
- Which outcome-based segments to target
- Which underserved outcomes to address
- What strategy (differentiated, dominant, disruptive, discrete, sustaining) fits each segment
- Order of targeting and timeline for implementation

### VIII. Target Hidden Growth Opportunities
Use the Opportunity Algorithm and Opportunity Landscape to prioritize:
- **Opportunity Score = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)**
- Score ≥ 10 = underserved (unmet need)
- Upper-right = table stakes (important AND satisfied — must match)
- Far-left = overserved (cost reduction targets)
- Lower-right = most underserved (top innovation targets)

### IX. Formulate the Market Strategy
1. Target existing offerings at outcome-based segments
2. Communicate product strengths to each segment
3. Include outcome-based value proposition in all communications
4. Build digital marketing (AdWords, SEO) around unmet outcomes
5. Assign leads to ODI-based segments
6. Arm sales team with segment-specific tools

### X. Formulate the Product Strategy
1. Identify weaknesses of existing/pipeline products against unmet outcomes
2. Determine value creation opportunities per segment
3. Determine cost reduction opportunities per segment
4. Facilitate ideation for existing products, pipeline products, and new platforms
5. Gain cross-functional agreement on product strategy

## The 84 Practitioner Steps (Summary)

### Phase I: Initiate (Steps 1-15)
Scope, plan, and initiate the project. Gain agreement on customer, job definition, and preliminary job map.

### Phase II: Uncover Needs (Steps 16-33)
Capture the complete set of customer needs: desired outcomes on core job, related jobs, emotional/social jobs, consumption chain outcomes, and financial outcomes.

### Phase III: Gather Quantitative Data (Steps 34-51)
Design, field, and manage a survey to a statistically valid sample (180-3,000 customers). Collect importance and satisfaction ratings for every outcome.

### Phase IV: Discover Opportunities (Steps 52-62)
Run Outcome-Based Segmentation, competitive analysis, and other analyses. Create segment profiles. Identify underserved/overserved outcomes in each segment.

### Phase V: Formulate Market Strategy (Steps 63-74)
Target offerings at segments, define value propositions, create messaging, build digital strategy, arm sales team.

### Phase VI: Formulate Product Strategy (Steps 75-84)
Identify weaknesses, facilitate ideation for existing products, pipeline products, and new platforms. Create product strategy deliverables.
