# Jobs-to-be-Done Needs Framework — Deep Reference

Based on Anthony Ulwick's "Jobs to be Done: Theory to Practice" (2016).

## Overview

The JTBD Needs Framework categorizes ALL customer needs into 6 types. A diverse group of customers in a given market collectively has well over 100 needs. In complex markets (healthcare, social media), customers may have 200+ needs.

All need statements must be:
- **Mutually exclusive** — defined independent of each other
- **Collectively exhaustive** — incorporating all needs for the job
- **Stated separately** and categorized correctly

---

## 1. The Core Functional Job-to-be-Done

The underlying process the end user is trying to execute. It is the anchor around which all other needs are defined.

### Three Defining Characteristics
1. **Stable over time** — The job doesn't change; only the delivery vehicle / technology changes. (People have used record players → tape players → CDs → MP3s → streaming to "listen to music.")
2. **No geographical boundaries** — People worldwide share the same jobs. Solutions and satisfaction levels vary, but the jobs and desired outcomes are universal.
3. **Solution-agnostic** — The job doesn't care if your solution is hardware, software, or service. It has no solution boundaries.

### Rules for Defining the Core Functional Job

**Format:** `verb + object of the verb (noun) + contextual clarifier`

Examples:
- "Cut a piece of wood in a straight line"
- "Pass on life lessons to children"
- "Monitor a patient's vital signs"
- "Listen to music while on the go"
- "Get breakfast while commuting to work"

**DO:**
- Take the customer's perspective ("prevent weeds from impacting crop yields" not "kill weeds")
- Keep it one-dimensional and mutually exclusive
- Ask "Why are you using that product? What job are you ultimately trying to get done?"
- Consider whether the company can/will address this job end-to-end over time

**DON'T:**
- Define it too narrowly (just "boil water" when the real job is "prepare a hot beverage for consumption")
- Define it too broadly (impossible for the company to tackle end-to-end)
- Mix in emotional/social needs ("accurately, safely and quickly cut wood" — strip those out)
- Include desired outcomes in the job statement
- Define a *situation* rather than a job ("overcome boredom" is a situation; "stay informed on topics of interest" is a job)
- Ask "What job did you hire that product to do?" (this is product-centric and may not reveal the entire job)

## 2. Desired Outcomes on the Core Functional Job

These are the metrics customers use to measure success and value as they execute each step in the job. They are the real key to innovation success. Typically 50-150 desired outcome statements per core functional job.

### Desired Outcome Statement Format

`direction of improvement + performance metric + object of control + contextual clarifier`

**Directions of improvement:** "minimize" or "increase" (occasionally "maximize" or "reduce")
**Performance metrics:** Usually "the time it takes to…", "the likelihood that…", or "the number of…"

Examples:
- "Minimize the time it takes to get the songs in the desired order for listening"
- "Minimize the likelihood that the music sounds distorted at high volume"
- "Minimize the time it takes for the corn seeds to germinate"
- "Minimize the likelihood that the plants fail to emerge at the same time"
- "Minimize the likelihood of inadvertently moving off the cut line when dust covers the path"

### Rules for Desired Outcome Statements
- Must be **measurable** — uses concrete metrics (time, likelihood, number)
- Must be **controllable** — something a solution could actually improve
- Must be **actionable** — points toward design direction
- Must be **devoid of solutions** — no references to specific products or technologies
- Must be **stable over time** — won't change as technology changes
- Must be **structured for surveying** — can be rated for importance and satisfaction on a 1-5 scale

### Why Outcomes Matter
A complete set of desired outcome statements is a long-term company asset because outcomes don't change over time — only the solutions that address them do. With these in hand, a company can conduct quantitative research to discover exactly which outcomes are underserved.

## 3. Related Jobs

Other functional jobs the end user wants to get done *in conjunction with* the core functional job. Typically 5-20 related jobs.

Getting more related jobs done on a single platform makes the platform more valuable.

Example: While making a presentation (core job), a knowledge worker may also want to:
- Emphasize a point projected on a screen
- Advance slides
- Time the presentation
- Shut off the projector

This explains how the telescopic pointer evolved into today's wireless presenter device.

---

## 4. Emotional and Social Jobs

**Emotional jobs:** How customers want to *feel* or *avoid feeling* as a result of executing the core functional job.
**Social jobs:** How the customer wants to *be perceived* by others.

Typically 5-25 emotional/social jobs per core functional job.

Example (core job = "pass on life lessons to children"):
- Emotional: "Feel appreciated"
- Social: "Be perceived as a caring parent"

These inform value proposition, marketing, positioning, and product design decisions. They are defined as separate statements, NOT mixed into the functional job definition.

---

## 5. Consumption Chain Jobs

Jobs related to the product lifecycle — not why people buy the product, but the tasks required to own and use it:

| Consumption Chain Job | Description |
|---|---|
| Purchase | Research, evaluate, transact |
| Receive | Take delivery |
| Install | Set up initially |
| Set up | Configure for use |
| Learn to use | Understand how it works |
| Interface with | Interact during use |
| Transport | Move from place to place |
| Clean | Maintain cleanliness |
| Store | Keep when not in use |
| Maintain | Keep in working order |
| Upgrade | Improve over time |
| Repair | Fix when broken |
| Dispose | Get rid of when done |

Each consumption chain job has its own set of desired outcome statements. A product that simplifies consumption differentiates itself (e.g., Dyson's bagless system, non-iron shirts).

---

## 6. Financial Desired Outcomes (Buyer's Metrics)

The financial metrics the purchase decision maker uses when deciding which product/service to buy. Typically 40-80 financial outcomes.

Example (hospital administrator buying medical devices):
- "Reduce the patient's length of stay"
- "Reduce morbidity rates"
- "Reduce the likelihood of a recurrence"

These drive business model innovation and pricing strategy. When the buyer is also the user, make sure they wear the "buyer's hat" when describing financial metrics.

---

## Three Key Customer Types

| Customer Type | What They Provide | Example (Surgical Tool) |
|---|---|---|
| **Core Job Executor** (end user) | Functional desired outcomes, emotional jobs, related jobs | Surgeon: "minimize the likelihood of removing healthy tissue" |
| **Product Lifecycle Support Team** | Consumption chain desired outcomes | Bio-med: outcomes for sterilizing the tool |
| **Purchase Decision Maker** (buyer) | Financial desired outcomes | Hospital admin: "reduce the patient's length of stay" |

When a product addresses the unmet needs of all three customer types: influencers recommend it, distributors carry it, social media promotes it, people buy it, and internal stakeholders benefit.
