# Jobs-to-be-Done Growth Strategy Matrix — Deep Reference

Based on Anthony Ulwick's "Jobs to be Done: Theory to Practice" (2016).

## Core Insight

New products and services win in the marketplace if they help customers get a job done **better** (faster, more predictably, with higher output) **and/or more cheaply**.

This leads to a 2x2 matrix with 5 distinct growth strategies.

## The Matrix

```
                    Charge MORE              Charge LESS
                 ┌─────────────────────┬─────────────────────┐
  Get JOB DONE   │  DIFFERENTIATED     │  DOMINANT            │
  BETTER         │  STRATEGY           │  STRATEGY            │
                 │  → underserved      │  → ALL customers     │
                 │    customers only   │                      │
                 ├─────────────────────┤                      │
                 │  SUSTAINING STRATEGY │                      │
                 │  → existing customers│                      │
                 ├─────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
  Get JOB DONE   │  DISCRETE           │  DISRUPTIVE          │
  WORSE          │  STRATEGY           │  STRATEGY            │
                 │  → restricted       │  → overserved        │
                 │    customers        │    customers &       │
                 │                     │    nonconsumers      │
                 └─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┘
```

## The Five Strategies

### 1. Differentiated Strategy
**When:** A highly underserved segment exists with unmet needs, willing to pay premium.
**How:** Target underserved customers with a product that gets the job done significantly better at a higher price.
**Result:** Disproportionate profit share.

**Examples:** Nest thermostat ($250 vs $35 incumbents, captured 25%+ profit share with <10% market share), Apple iPhone 2G, Dyson vacuum, Bang & Olufsen, BMW, Herman Miller Aeron chair, Nespresso.

**Strategic path:** Enter high-end → capture profit share → work down-market over time (as Apple did with iPhone series), transitioning toward a dominant strategy.

### 2. Dominant Strategy
**When:** You can get the job done both significantly better AND significantly cheaper.
**How:** Target ALL consumers with a superior, less expensive offering.
**Result:** Incumbents cannot defend against this. Most appealing strategy for new entrants.

**Threshold:** Products that get the job done ≥20% better AND ≥20% cheaper are likely to win.

**Examples:** Google Search, Google AdWords, UberX, Netflix streaming, Vanguard personal investing, Bosch CS20 circular saw, Kroll Ontrack electronic evidence discovery.

**Why incumbents rarely pursue this:** It could cut margins and may require new platforms, capabilities, and resources.

### 3. Disruptive Strategy
**When:** Overserved customers or nonconsumers exist. Overserved = getting features they don't need/value. Nonconsumers = can't afford current solutions.
**How:** Target with a product that gets the job done more cheaply but not as well.
**Result:** Captures value from those overpaying for performance they don't need.

**Examples:** Google Docs (vs. Microsoft Office), TurboTax (vs. traditional tax services), Dollar Shave Club (vs. Gillette), eTrade (vs. traditional brokerages), Coursera (vs. traditional universities), UberPOOL.

**The Process of Disruption:** Introduce V1 that's cheaper/worse → build on that platform → V2, V3, V4 get progressively better → eventually V4+ gets the job done better AND cheaper (becoming a dominant strategy). This matches Christensen's description of "relentlessly moving up market."

**Key insight:** Works for overserved customers (willing to sacrifice for lower cost) AND nonconsumers (who are actually underserved in that they can't afford ANY solution — a cheaper option gets the job done better than nothing).

### 4. Discrete Strategy
**When:** Customers have restricted access to alternatives (legal, physical, emotional, or other restrictions).
**How:** Take an existing product and sell it in a unique situation that justifies higher prices.
**Result:** Can be very profitable but risks being perceived as exploitative.

**Examples:** Airport drinks past security, stadium concessions, check-cashing/payday-lending, ATMs in remote locations, StubHub resale, surge pricing.

**Warning:** Can generate public backlash (e.g., Mylan/EpiPen controversy).

### 5. Sustaining Strategy
**When:** Product gets the job done only slightly better (<5%) and/or slightly cheaper.
**How:** Incremental improvements to maintain market position.
**Result:** Good for incumbents retaining share; BAD for new entrants.

**Critical threshold:** Customers generally switch to new products only when they get the job done ≥20% better. Sustaining improvements don't clear this threshold for new entrants.

## Choosing a Strategy — Decision Logic

You MUST know whether underserved/overserved segments exist before choosing. Without this, you're guessing.

| If the target segment is... | Use... | Avoid... |
|---|---|---|
| Highly underserved (many unmet needs, willing to pay more) | Differentiated | Disruptive (they don't want cheaper/worse) |
| Overserved (getting features they don't need) | Disruptive | Differentiated (they don't want to pay more) |
| Mix of segments | Different strategies for different segments | One-size-fits-all |
| All segments | Dominant (if feasible) | — |
| Restricted-access situations | Discrete | — |
| Existing customers, no clear under/overserved segments | Sustaining | — |

## Multi-Product Strategy

A company can employ different strategies for different products in the same market. Uber example:
- **UberBLACK** → Differentiated (premium, better)
- **UberX** → Dominant (better AND cheaper than taxis)
- **UberPOOL** → Disruptive (cheaper but less convenient)

## How to Discover Segments

The ONLY reliable way to discover whether underserved/overserved segments exist is **Outcome-Based Segmentation** — segmenting the market around a complete set of prioritized desired outcome statements using factor analysis and cluster analysis.

Demographics, psychographics, and behavioral data nearly ALWAYS fail to explain why customers have different unmet needs. A 28-year-old man from Montana can have the same unmet needs as a 55-year-old woman from Florida. Personas built on demographics create "phantom targets."

## The Opportunity Algorithm

```
Opportunity Score = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)
```

- **Importance:** % of customers rating the outcome 4 or 5 on a 1-5 scale (converted to 0-10)
- **Satisfaction:** % of customers rating satisfaction 4 or 5 on a 1-5 scale (converted to 0-10)
- **Score ≥ 10:** The outcome is underserved (an unmet need / opportunity)
- **Satisfaction > Importance:** The outcome is overserved (cost reduction target)

### The Opportunity Landscape

A 2D plot with Importance on Y-axis and Satisfaction on X-axis:
- **Upper right (high importance, high satisfaction):** Table stakes — must satisfy to compete
- **Lower right (low importance, high satisfaction):** Overserved — cost reduction targets
- **Upper left (high importance, low satisfaction):** Most underserved — top innovation targets
- **Middle zone:** Appropriately served

## Measuring Concept Viability

Evaluate proposed concepts against ALL desired outcomes. Products that get the job done ≥20% better are very likely to win. This can be measured with high precision when evaluating a concept against a complete set of desired outcome statements.
