# Phase 1: Planning Your Research

## The Mini Research Plan

Before talking to anyone, answer three questions:

### 1. What decision do you want to make?

This is the most skipped and hardest step. Don't settle for vague goals like "understand my customers." Instead:

- Think about the meeting you'll be in 2-3 weeks from now. What do you want this research to have informed?
- Make it specific and actionable:
  - BAD: "I want to understand my market"
  - GOOD: "I want to decide which customer segment to build for first"
  - GOOD: "I want to refine my core value proposition for [specific audience]"
  - GOOD: "I want to decide which of three features to prioritize in our MVP"

**Common trap:** People jump straight to learning goals when asked about decisions. If someone says "I need to understand the deep problems of my customers," push them: "In order to do what? What will you do differently once you know that?"

### 2. What are your 1-2 learning goals?

What do you need to learn that you don't already know, in order to make that decision?

- Limit to 1-2 goals per research sprint
- Trying to learn about 10 things at once produces shallow responses
- Multiple learning goals can introduce conflicting variables
- Ask: "What do I know the LEAST about that would most inform this decision?"

**Examples:**
- "What are the top workflow pain points for [target customer] related to [problem area]?"
- "How do [target customers] currently solve [problem] and what resources do they invest in it?"
- "What is [target customer]'s reaction to our core value proposition?"

### 3. Who will you talk to?

Define a specific target customer profile. Talking to many different types of people about the same questions produces conflicting, noisy data.

#### Start with psychographics (how they think/behave):
- What behaviors suggest they'd need your solution?
- What mindset or situation are they in?
- Example: "Chefs in overloaded, high-volume kitchens" vs "Chefs focused on culinary innovation"

#### Then layer on demographics (observable attributes):
- Geography, company size, years of experience, role, etc.
- Choose demographics that correlate with your psychographic hypothesis
- Example: "Large-city restaurant, serves 200+ covers/night"

#### Cohort strategy:
- If you're unsure which segment matters most, create 2-3 cohort hypotheses
- Do secondary research first (forums, conventions, your own experience) to narrow before interviewing
- Start with the cohort you believe is most promising — run 5 interviews
- If it's not gaining traction, move to the next cohort
- Don't exhaust yourself testing every segment

## Research Sprint Structure

Chunk your research into sprints of 5-8 conversations:

```
Sprint 1 (5-8 interviews)
├── Same target customer profile
├── Same 1-2 learning goals
├── Same core questions
└── Debrief after each → synthesize at end

Sprint 2 (5-8 interviews)
├── May be same or different profile
├── Updated learning goals based on Sprint 1
├── Refined questions
└── Debrief after each → synthesize at end
```

After 3-4 interviews in a sprint, you'll likely start seeing patterns — that's a sign you're well-focused. By 5-8, you should have enough conviction to act.

## Pre-Interview Preparation

Before your first interview:

1. **Note-taking template** — Can be a blank doc or structured by your Zoom Out/In/Out sections. Include space for DIA debrief fields at the bottom.
2. **Recruit a note-taker** — Your co-founder or teammate. They hear the data live (faster alignment) and free you to focus on the conversation.
3. **Set up recording + transcription** — Ask permission. Frame it as: "I'd like to record so I don't miss anything. We can't type fast enough. Would that be comfortable?"
4. **Schedule 15-min buffer** after each interview for debrief.

## Recruitment Tips

- Share enough context to attract the right people, but minimal info about your product
- BAD: "We're building the fastest-heating pan ever — want to chat?"
- GOOD: "I'd like to tap your expertise as a chef working in a high-volume kitchen"
- Make scheduling frictionless (Calendly or similar)
- Incentivize their time, especially outside your network
- Don't reveal your specific solution in the recruitment message — it creates acquiescence bias before the conversation even starts
