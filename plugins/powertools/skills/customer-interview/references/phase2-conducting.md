# Phase 2: Conducting Interviews — Ask Better Questions

## Two Objectives

1. **Get better qualitative data** — Prevent bias from corrupting your signal
2. **Uncover unexpected insights** — Discover things you didn't know to ask about

---

## Objective 1: Combat Three Bias Types

### Bias Type 1: Confirmation Bias
*You hear what you want to hear because you're invested in your idea.*

**How to prevent it:**

- **Eliminate leading questions.** A leading question implies an expected answer.
  - Test: Can it be answered yes/no? Does it start with "Do you...?" → It's leading.
  - Test: Does it contain an adjective implying your opinion? ("How easy...", "How frustrating...") → It's leading.

- **Rephrase pattern:**
  - BAD: "Do you struggle with slow pans?"
  - BAD: "How frustrating is it when your pan takes too long to heat?"
  - GOOD: "Tell me about your experience preparing meals during a busy service."
  - GOOD: "To what extent was this easy, if at all?" → then always ask the counter: "What did you find difficult about this?"

- **Always ask the counter.** After any positive signal, probe the negative:
  - "What comes to mind?" → then "What concerns do you have?"
  - "What works well?" → then "What doesn't work well?"
  - If someone says "this product is great" — that's your alarm bell to push: "What would you change? What concerns do you have?"

- **Monitor during your sprint.** After 3-4 interviews, you'll see patterns forming. That's the moment to ask: "Am I just hearing what I want to hear? Can I ask a question that would challenge this emerging pattern?"

- **"Don't have happy ears."** Don't listen selectively for what sounds good.

### Bias Type 2: Acquiescence Bias
*People want to be nice and helpful. They'll tell you what they think you want to hear.*

**How to prevent it:**

- **Neutral framing from the start.** Don't reveal your product in recruitment emails or interview intros.
  - BAD: "I'm building an amazing fast-heating pan and want your feedback"
  - GOOD: "I'd like to better understand how chefs manage time pressure in busy kitchens"

- **Interview intro script:**
  > "I'm [name], wanting to learn more about [broad area]. I'm going to eventually show you an early idea, but first I want to better understand you and your world. Feel free to elaborate on what's most true for you. You don't have to answer anything you're not comfortable with."

  This tells them: (a) their broad answers ARE what's useful to you, and (b) they're the expert.

- **"Microphone on your brain" technique.** When showing your concept, say:
  > "Imagine you have a microphone on your brain. I really want to hear your unfiltered thoughts as we talk through this."
  
  This unusual framing creates psychological permission to be honest. People often preface honest feedback with "Well, if I had a microphone on my brain, I'd say..."

- **Don't show your hand.** If they can tell you care about pans, they'll find 50 things to say about pans — even if pans don't actually keep them up at night.

- **Watch your own preamble.** Avoid lengthy lead-ins that signal what you want to hear. Don't recap what they said earlier (let them potentially contradict themselves — that's interesting data).

### Bias Type 3: Future-Prediction Bias
*People are bad at predicting their future behavior. They also have unreliable memories.*

**How to prevent it:**

- **Never ask:** "Will you use this?" / "How much will you pay?" / "Would you switch to this?"
- **Instead, ask about current and recent behavior:**
  - "Walk me through how you prepared dinner service yesterday."
  - "Tell me about the last pan you purchased. What led to that decision?"
  - "What resources do you currently invest in kitchen tools?"
  - "How has your process changed over time? What prompted that?"

- **For willingness-to-pay specifically:**
  - Ask what they currently spend in the category (not just on the exact product type)
  - Ask about budget allocation: "How do you think about your kitchen tools budget?"
  - Use extreme anchoring: "Even if this pan were $1,000, what comes to mind?" — people will blurt out their real ceiling ("No way, I'd never spend more than $150")
  - Ask about past switching behavior: "What made you switch tools in the past?"

## Objective 2: The Zoom Out → In → Out Framework

Structure every interview in three sections:

### Section 1: ZOOM OUT (Beginning)
*Understand their world before narrowing to your topic.*

Purpose: Catch blind spots, understand context, see what they REALLY care about before you bias them toward your topic.

**Example questions:**
- "Tell me about yourself and your role."
- "Walk me through last week at work."
- "What takes up most of your time and energy?"
- "What do you wish you could spend less time on?"

Spend MORE time here during discovery-stage research. This is where you find out what actually matters to them — which may not be what you assumed.

### Section 2: ZOOM IN (Middle)
*Dig into your specific topic and test your concept.*

Two sub-sections:

**A) Behaviors first:**
- "Walk me through your process for [specific activity] step by step."
- "Tell me about a specific recent time you [did relevant thing]."
- "What tools/resources do you use to support this?"
- "How has this changed over time? What prompted those changes?"

**B) Then attitudes:**
- "What works well about [current solution] for you today?"
- "What doesn't work well?" (Don't give them an out on this one — make them answer)
- Ask the same topic 2-3 different ways:
  - "If someone new were starting, what advice would you give them about [topic]?"
  - "If you had a magic wand, what would you change about [topic]?"

**C) Concept feedback** (when ready to share your idea):

1. Re-ground them: "I'd love to shift to a really early concept. Your constructive, unfiltered feedback is most helpful. You can help us get this right."
2. State the concept in one neutral sentence — strip all adjectives
   - BAD: "We've built an amazing revolutionary pan that heats incredibly fast"
   - GOOD: "We're working on a pan that heats in half the time of conventional pans."
3. Pause. Ask: "What comes to mind when you hear that?"
4. Let them supply the adjectives and reactions
5. Probe: "What, if anything, sounds valuable?" → "What, if anything, does NOT sound valuable?"
6. "What open questions do you have?" / "What concerns do you have?"
7. "Who do you think this would be good for?" → "Who would this NOT be good for?"
8. "If this were available tomorrow, what would you do?" → "If this were NEVER available, what would you do?"

For multi-faceted concepts: Break into chunks. Share piece 1, get open reaction. Share piece 2, get open reaction. Ask detailed comparison questions only after all pieces are shared.

### Section 3: ZOOM OUT (End)
*Calibrate how important your topic actually is in their world.*

- "If we could wave a magic wand and do anything to help you with [broad goal], what would it be?"
- "Is there anything else on your mind related to [broad area] that we haven't discussed?"

Often their answer here differs from what you zoomed into — that's valuable calibration data.

## Universal Tactical Rules

### Ask one short question at a time
- No preamble. No multi-part questions.
- BAD: "You mentioned earlier that you have trouble with X, and I was thinking about how that relates to Y, so what do you think about X and also would you say Y is important and how does Z factor in?"
- GOOD: "What do you think about X?" [wait] ... "How does Y factor in?" [wait]
- People fill silence. One short question often gets 3 questions' worth of answers.

### Let contradictions happen
- Don't recap their earlier answers. If they say something different later, that's interesting data — not a mistake to correct.

### Balance behaviors and attitudes
- Always ask about what they DO (behaviors, specific examples) before asking how they FEEL about it (attitudes, opinions).

### When in doubt, go open-ended
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What was that like?"
- "Help me understand..."
- "What else?"

### The magic question for any moment: "Why?"
- Use the 5 Whys technique to dig beneath surface answers
- "Why is that important to you?" → "And why does that matter?" → keep going
