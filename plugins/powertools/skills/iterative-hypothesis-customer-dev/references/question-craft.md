# Crafting Questions from Hypotheses

This reference covers the art of transforming hypotheses into interview questions that actually produce useful information.

## The Four Criteria

Every interview question should:

1. **Confirm or negate the hypothesis** — this is the whole reason the question exists
2. **Not hint at any specific answer** — avoid biasing the response
3. **Invite a specific answer** — get concrete detail, not just yes/no
4. **Invite more information** — leave room for things you didn't think to ask

## Bad vs. Good Question Patterns

The pattern: take a hypothesis, notice what a "selling" question would look like, then rewrite it as a genuine discovery question.

### Pattern: Identity and Self-Description

**Hypothesis:** Users call themselves "bloggers."

**Bad:** "Do you consider yourself a 'blogger?'" (Yes/no, and it supplies the word you're testing for.)

**Good:** "When you meet someone new, how do you explain what you do in a few sentences?" (Open-ended; the interviewee will naturally use whatever word they actually use.)

### Pattern: Frequency and Behavior

**Hypothesis:** Serious bloggers publish at least four times per week.

**Bad:** "Do you publish often, so Google ranks you high in SEO and there's a lot of surface area for people to find you?" (Leading — implies the "right" frequency is high, and explains why, priming the interviewee to agree.)

**Good:** "How often do you publish new content? Why at that rate — what led you to that decision?" (Gets the actual number, plus the reasoning, which may reveal motivations you hadn't considered.)

### Pattern: Spending and Value

**Hypothesis:** Some bloggers spend thousands on consultants to make blogs fast and scalable.

**Bad:** "Would you spend $2000 on a consultant, if it meant your blog would become much faster and more scalable, so you rank higher on Google search results and get more traffic?" (Classic leading question: states the benefit, names a price, and frames spending as obviously rational.)

**Good:** "How valuable is the speed of your website? Have you ever spent money to improve it? If so, how much, and what did you do? Did it work? Were you happy with that investment?" (Multi-layered open question. Gets at perceived value, actual spending behavior, satisfaction, and outcomes — all without suggesting what the "right" answer is.)

### Pattern: Pain and Concern

**Hypothesis:** All WordPress bloggers worry about getting hacked.

**Bad:** "Blogs get hacked all the time, and when they do it's devastating, right? Would you like it if your hosting company had extra security measures?" (States the problem as fact, describes it as devastating, then offers the solution. Everyone will agree.)

**Good:** "Do you ever think about website security? If so, how do you think about that? Do you do anything about it today?" (Neutral opening that lets the interviewee reveal whether they care at all, how they frame it, and what actions they've taken — or haven't.)

### Pattern: Testing Real Value via Price

**Hypothesis:** Bloggers care about security.

**Good (advanced):** "Would you pay extra for a security package that really worked, or do you not really worry about being singled out for getting attacked?" (Tying a price question to a feature tests whether the person actually values it. By almost suggesting they shouldn't bother, you create space for honest "no" answers. Cohen found most bloggers claimed security was important but admitted they wouldn't pay extra.)

## Question Writing Checklist

Before finalizing each question, run through this:

- [ ] Could someone answer "yes" or "no" and be done? → Rewrite to require elaboration
- [ ] Does the question contain adjectives that signal the "correct" answer? (e.g., "devastating," "essential," "amazing") → Remove them
- [ ] Does the question explain the benefits before asking for an opinion? → Remove the benefits pitch
- [ ] Does the question supply the specific word or concept you're testing for? → Rewrite so the interviewee has to produce it themselves
- [ ] Is there a follow-up baked in? ("How much?" "Why?" "What happened?") → Good, keep it
- [ ] Could the question surface something you haven't considered? → If not, broaden it

## Structuring the Interview

Order questions from broad context to specific topics:

1. **Opening context** — "Walk me through a typical day/week" questions that establish their world
2. **Goal-aligned exploration** — Questions mapped to your top goals, starting with behavior and workflow, then moving to pain points
3. **Specific hypothesis tests** — Your most targeted questions
4. **Price discussion** — Near the end, once you've established context and rapport
5. **Open close** — "Is there anything I should have asked that I didn't?" or "What would you tell me if I were starting a company in this space?"

## The Follow-Up Arsenal

When something surprises you (surprise = learning), use these:

- "Tell me more about that."
- "What do you mean by [their exact word]?"
- "Can you walk me through the last time that happened?"
- "What did you do next?"
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "Why do you think that is?"
- Silence. Just wait. People fill silence with their real thoughts.

## Handling "I Would Buy That"

When someone says they'd buy your product, don't celebrate. Instead:

"Great — can you walk me through, step by step, how you'd actually use this? When would you open it up? What's the first thing you'd do? How does the output get into your existing workflow?"

This forces them to think concretely. The barriers and objections that would actually prevent a purchase tend to surface during this exercise, even when the person genuinely believes they want the product.
