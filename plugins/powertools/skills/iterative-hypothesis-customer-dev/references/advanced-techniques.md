# Advanced Techniques

Techniques for getting more signal out of interviews, recognizing patterns, and avoiding common traps.

## Emergent Segmentation

As you accumulate interviews, you may notice that certain types of customers cluster around a consistent set of answers while another type clusters differently. This is emergent segmentation — the market is telling you it has structure.

When you suspect segmentation:

1. **Write hypotheses about the segments themselves.** What do you think distinguishes Segment A from Segment B? Company size? Role? Experience level? Budget authority?
2. **Create screening questions.** Ask these early in each interview to identify which segment this person belongs to.
3. **Keep separate hypothesis tracking per segment.** The same hypothesis may be true for one segment and false for another (e.g., marketing departments at large companies think about security proactively; independent bloggers almost never do).
4. **Decide which segments to pursue.** You might target one, some, or all. Regardless, understanding the landscape prevents building a product that's "meh" for everyone instead of "essential" for someone.

Segmentation often emerges from pricing discussions — different segments react viscerally and differently to the same price point.

## Discussing Price

Some practitioners argue pricing shouldn't come up in early interviews because it conflates financial considerations with discovery of attitudes and pain. Cohen disagrees, and the reasoning is worth understanding:

**Price is inseparable from meaning.** How someone reacts to "$50/month" reveals how they think about the problem, their budget authority, their expectations, and their alternatives. The price-tag is not a separate question — it's woven into the customer's entire relationship with the problem space.

**Price determines the business model.** A $5/month product requires millions of users and viral growth. A $500/month product requires enterprise sales. This can't be "figured out later" — it shapes everything.

**How to discuss price:**

- Float a specific number near the end of the interview, after you've built context. "Imagine a service that did X, Y, and Z — fast, secure, great support. What if it cost $50/month?"
- Watch for visceral reactions. Some people will be shocked at how high it is. Others will say it's suspiciously cheap. Those groups are probably different segments.
- You can also test price+value: "Would you pay extra for a security package, or is that not something you worry enough about to spend money on?" This reveals whether a stated concern has real economic weight behind it.

Quoting a specific price is an acceptable breach of the "no leading" protocol because it mimics what will actually happen when someone visits your pricing page.

## Expecting Contradictions

Your data will be noisy. People are different — different goals, values, histories, roles, and sometimes for no reason at all. This is normal.

Some hypotheses will end up reflecting variation rather than resolving to a clean answer. "This number varies wildly" or "there's no pattern in opinions on X" are valid findings. Knowing which patterns don't exist prevents false assumptions.

Real patterns will stand out from the noise. There may not be many strong patterns. That makes the ones you find even more valuable — those are your foundational truths to build around.

## Step-by-Step Usage Walkthrough

Classic failure mode: Customer says they have the problem. Customer says they'd buy your product. You build it. They don't buy.

The antidote: Ask them to describe, in painstaking detail, exactly how they would use the product in their daily life.

- When would they open it?
- What's the first thing they'd do?
- Which features would they use?
- How would they move outputs into their existing systems?
- Where does it fit in their existing workflow?

This forces concrete thinking. Barriers, missing integrations, workflow mismatches, and deal-breaking gaps tend to surface here, even when the customer genuinely believes they want the product.

## Capturing Exact Customer Language

Discord uses "server" instead of "room" or "community" because founder Jason Citron discovered early users were setting up virtual servers to host audio. The early pitch became "Get a free server!" — which matched the customer's mental model, not the company's investor deck.

Record your interviews (with permission). Note the exact words customers use. These words become your positioning, your ad copy, your feature names. Your language should mirror theirs, not the other way around.

## Handling "Market Guru" Mode

Almost every interviewee will, at some point, stop talking about their own experience and start speaking on behalf of "most people" or "the market." Examples:

- "I wouldn't pay $50/month, but a lot of people would."
- "I care about security, but most people are clueless."
- "Well I use a free tool for that, but most people don't."

This is well-intentioned but unreliable. They haven't done dozens of interviews. They don't know what "most people" think — and neither do you yet.

How to handle it:
1. Be polite. "That's great insight, thank you."
2. Translate: "everyone else wants X" might actually mean "I want X" — or it might mean nothing.
3. Redirect: "That's interesting — but I'm really curious about your specific situation. Can you tell me more about how you handle [topic] personally?"

Successful companies often find untapped niches that experts missed. Even if your interviewee is a genuine market expert, you're specifically looking for holes and insights the experts haven't noticed.

## STFU Principle

If you're talking, you're not learning. To maximize learning, minimize talking.

When you do talk, it should be for one of three reasons:
1. **Clarifying** what they just said — "When you say X, do you mean...?"
2. **Digging deeper** on the current topic — "Tell me more about that."
3. **Opening a new topic** — transitioning to your next question

Everything else is selling, and selling during a discovery interview produces zero useful information.

## When to Stop: The Boredom Signal

The stopping rule is elegant: stop when you're bored. Not "bored" as in unengaged, but "bored" as in nothing is surprising anymore. When a new interviewee says things you've already heard from the last five people, learning has plateaued.

At this point, your next step is NOT more interviews. It's building something — a prototype, a proof of concept, a landing page with a signup form, an SLC (Simple, Lovable, Complete). A different method for making progress.

**Warning signs of premature stopping:**
- Fewer than 5-8 interviews completed
- You still feel excited/anxious rather than confident
- You stopped because it's hard or uncomfortable, not because it's boring
- You haven't found any contradictions to your original hypotheses (you probably aren't asking hard enough questions)

**Warning signs the idea itself might be wrong:**
- No consistent patterns after many interviews
- People agree it's a problem but no one has ever tried to solve it or spend money on it
- The pain exists but it's ranked #47 on their priority list
- You can't find enough people who even have the problem
