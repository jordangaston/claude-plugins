# The Value Chain

The value chain is the basic tool for diagnosing competitive advantage and finding ways to create and sustain it. Competitive advantage cannot be understood by looking at a firm as a whole. It stems from the many discrete **activities** a firm performs in designing, producing, marketing, delivering, and supporting its product. Each of these activities can contribute to a firm's relative cost position and create a basis for differentiation. The value chain disaggregates a firm into its strategically relevant activities so the behavior of costs and the existing and potential sources of differentiation can be understood.

## Value, not cost

The value chain displays **total value**, which consists of **value activities** and **margin**.

- **Value** is the amount buyers are willing to pay for what a firm provides. A firm is profitable if the value it commands exceeds the costs of creating the product. Value — not cost — must be used in analyzing competitive position, because firms often deliberately raise cost to command a premium price through differentiation.
- **Value activities** are the physically and technologically distinct activities a firm performs — the building blocks by which a firm creates a product valuable to its buyers.
- **Margin** is the difference between total value and the collective cost of performing the value activities.

## The nine generic activity categories

Value activities divide into two broad types.

### Primary activities — creating, selling, and supporting the product

1. **Inbound logistics** — receiving, storing, and disseminating inputs: material handling, warehousing, inventory control, vehicle scheduling, returns to suppliers.
2. **Operations** — transforming inputs into the final product: machining, packaging, assembly, equipment maintenance, testing, facility operations.
3. **Outbound logistics** — collecting, storing, and physically distributing the product to buyers: finished-goods warehousing, order processing, delivery, scheduling.
4. **Marketing & sales** — providing the means by which buyers can buy and inducing them to do so: advertising, promotion, sales force, channel selection and relations, pricing.
5. **Service** — enhancing or maintaining the value of the product: installation, repair, training, parts supply, product adjustment.

The relative importance of each category varies by industry. For a distributor, inbound and outbound logistics dominate. For a bank, marketing and operations are key. For a high-service supplier, service is decisive. Every category, however, is present and contributes to some degree.

### Support activities — supporting the primary activities and each other

6. **Procurement** — the *function* of purchasing inputs (not the inputs themselves): raw materials, supplies, machinery, lab equipment, services. Spread throughout the firm, it tends to be diffuse and its true cost underestimated. Purchasing practices can significantly affect the cost and quality of purchased inputs.
7. **Technology development** — the know-how, procedures, and technology embodied in the process equipment that goes into *every* value activity, not just the product (R&D, process design, media research, servicing procedures).
8. **Human resource management** — recruiting, hiring, training, development, and compensation of all types of personnel. Affects competitive advantage through its role in determining the skills and motivation of employees and the cost of hiring and training.
9. **Firm infrastructure** — general management, planning, finance, accounting, legal, government affairs, quality management. Unlike the others, infrastructure usually supports the entire chain rather than individual activities; it is sometimes a source of advantage (e.g., superior information systems for cost or differentiation).

## Activity types within each category

Within any category, activities fall into three types, all of which can matter for both cost and differentiation:

- **Direct** — activities directly involved in creating value for the buyer (assembly, parts machining, sales-force operation, advertising, product design).
- **Indirect** — activities that make direct activities possible on a continuing basis (maintenance, scheduling, facility operation, sales-force administration, record keeping). Indirect cost is large and growing in many industries and is easy to overlook; it is often where competitors' cost positions actually diverge.
- **Quality assurance** — activities ensuring the quality of other activities (monitoring, inspecting, testing, reviewing, adjusting, reworking). Quality assurance is not synonymous with quality management; it appears in many activities throughout the chain.

## Defining the value chain

Defining relevant value activities requires isolating activities that are technologically and strategically distinct. The categories are not ends in themselves — disaggregate as far as analysis usefully allows.

**Disaggregate an activity into a separate one when it:**
- has *different economics* (different cost drivers),
- has a *high or rapidly growing* proportion of cost or assets, or
- has a *high potential to differentiate* — a real or potential impact on buyer value.

Otherwise, group activities together; pointless detail obscures the analysis. The order of activities should broadly follow the process flow, but the chain is a *system* of interdependent activities, not a sequence of independent ones.

## Linkages within the value chain

Activities are related by **linkages** — relationships between the way one value activity is performed and the cost or performance of another. Competitive advantage often comes from linkages as much as from the activities themselves, because rivals find linkages hard to see and to manage. Linkages create advantage in two ways:

- **Optimization** — trading off between activities to achieve the same result (e.g., a more costly product design that slashes after-sale service cost; more inspection that lowers field-service cost).
- **Coordination** — coordinating linked activities (e.g., on-time delivery requires operations, outbound logistics, and service to work in concert; coordination lowers inventory).

Exploiting linkages usually requires **information flows** across activities and often requires optimizing across organizational lines — which is exactly why competitors struggle to copy them.

## Vertical linkages and the value system

A firm's value chain sits in a larger stream of activities — the **value system**:

> **Supplier value chains → the firm's value chain → channel value chains → buyer value chains**

**Vertical linkages** connect the firm's activities to those of suppliers and channels. The way a supplier performs its activities affects the firm's cost and performance, and vice versa. These linkages are *not* zero-sum: coordinating with suppliers or channels (e.g., supplier-managed delivery schedules, channel co-marketing) can lower total cost or raise differentiation so that *both* parties gain. Capturing this requires looking beyond the firm's own boundaries.

## The buyer's value chain and buyer value

A firm's product is a **purchased input** to its buyer's value chain. The source of differentiation is the firm's ability — through its own value chain — to **lower the buyer's cost or raise the buyer's performance**. To find it, you must understand the buyer's value chain, not just your own. Buyers have value chains too (for industrial, commercial, and institutional buyers literally; for households, the "chain" is the activities and needs of the consumer). Value is created for the buyer when a firm's activity affects the buyer's chain favorably. (Buyer value is developed fully in `differentiation.md`.)

## Competitive scope and the value chain

Competitive scope shapes the value chain because it determines how activities are configured and shared:
- **Segment scope** — the product varieties and buyers served.
- **Vertical scope** — the division of activities between the firm and its suppliers/channels (make vs. buy).
- **Geographic scope** — the range of regions/countries the firm competes in with a coordinated chain.
- **Industry scope** — the range of related industries the firm competes in with a coordinated strategy (the basis for interrelationships — see `corporate-strategy.md`).

A *broad* scope can yield advantage through sharing activities across segments, geographies, or related industries; a *narrow* scope can yield advantage through tailoring the chain to a specific segment. Coalitions (joint ventures, alliances) extend scope without full integration.

## The value chain and organizational structure

The value chain is a more meaningful way to examine competitive advantage than the firm's formal organization chart. Organizational lines often *cut across* the activities and linkages that matter, which is why advantage from linkages is hard to manage and hard to imitate. Aligning structure with the value chain — and with the linkages that need coordinating — improves a firm's ability to create and sustain advantage.
