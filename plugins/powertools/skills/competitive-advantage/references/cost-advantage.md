# Cost Advantage

A firm has a cost advantage if its cumulative cost of performing all value activities is lower than competitors'. Cost advantage is sustainable if the *sources* of it are hard for rivals to replicate or imitate. The value chain is the basic tool for cost analysis: a firm's cost behavior depends on how it performs its discrete activities, not on the firm "in the aggregate."

## The value chain and cost analysis

Begin by defining the value chain and **assigning operating costs and assets to value activities**. Disaggregate activities into separate categories if they (a) represent a significant or growing proportion of cost, (b) have different cost behavior, or (c) competitors perform them differently. Assign costs *and* assets (assets are large in many activities, and their cost — capital — must be counted). A **first-cut analysis** quickly reveals where most cost lives; concentrate effort there.

## Cost drivers

Cost behavior in each activity is governed by structural factors Porter calls **cost drivers**. An activity's cost position is the cumulative result of the drivers operating on it. There are **ten cost drivers**:

1. **Economies of scale** — costs fall (or rise — *dis*economies) with the scale of an activity, through more efficient operation at larger volume, the ability to spread fixed costs, or greater bargaining power. Note that scale economies differ by activity, and the *relevant* scale (national, regional, plant, global) varies.
2. **Learning and spillovers** — cost declines over time as learning improves efficiency (better layout, scheduling, labor productivity, product design for manufacturability). Learning can *spill over* between firms via suppliers, equipment makers, and employees — proprietary learning is more valuable.
3. **Pattern of capacity utilization** — when an activity has substantial fixed cost, the cost per unit depends on capacity utilization. The *cost of changing* utilization matters, so the issue is the pattern over the cycle, not a snapshot.
4. **Linkages** — cost is affected by how *other* activities are performed (internal linkages) and by suppliers' and channels' activities (vertical linkages). Exploiting linkages to lower total cost is often invisible to competitors and therefore a sustainable source of advantage.
5. **Interrelationships** — sharing an activity with another business unit (see `corporate-strategy.md`) lowers cost, as does sharing know-how. The cost benefit depends on the share of cost the shared activity represents.
6. **Integration** — the level of vertical integration (make vs. buy) affects cost. Integration can lower cost (avoid market transaction costs, capture supplier margins) or raise it (loss of flexibility, scale). The choice should be made activity by activity.
7. **Timing** — first-mover advantages (early access to cheap raw materials, prime locations, learning, brand) or late-mover advantages (buying current-generation equipment, avoiding development cost). Timing can be the source of cost advantage independent of the other drivers.
8. **Discretionary policies** — policy choices independent of the other drivers: product configuration and features, mix and variety, service level, channel choice, wage levels, process technology, spec/feature choices. These are where managers have the most direct control — and where false economies lurk.
9. **Location** — geographic location affects costs of labor, management, materials, energy, transportation, and taxes.
10. **Institutional factors** — government regulation, tax, unionization, tariffs, local-content rules. Often the largest single driver, and the least within management's control — though it can sometimes be influenced.

For each significant activity, identify which drivers govern its cost and how the firm compares to competitors on each. Drivers **interact** (e.g., scale can magnify learning) and can **reinforce or counteract** one another.

## The cost of purchased inputs

Purchased inputs are part of every activity's cost and are frequently underexploited. Cost can be reduced through better **procurement practices**: information on input costs, optimizing specifications, leveraging purchases across units, choosing the right suppliers and managing their cost, and exploiting supplier linkages. Even small percentage savings on large purchased-input categories can swamp savings elsewhere.

## Cost dynamics

Costs shift over time. **Cost dynamics** are the way an activity's absolute and relative cost change as the industry and the firm evolve — from industry growth, differential scale sensitivity, different learning rates, technological change, inflation, and maturation. Anticipating dynamics lets a firm position for *future* cost advantage rather than today's.

## Determining the relative cost of competitors

Estimate competitors' value chains and the cost of their activities the same way you analyze your own. The goal is to understand the *drivers* behind each competitor's cost, not just the number — because that reveals what is sustainable and what is vulnerable. Competitors with different strategies or scope will have different chains and different costs.

## Gaining cost advantage

There are two principal ways to gain cost advantage:

1. **Control the cost drivers** — gain advantage in the drivers of significant activities: capture scale economies, lead in learning, raise capacity utilization, exploit linkages and interrelationships, choose integration wisely, time investments well, and adjust discretionary policies that add cost without adding buyer value.
2. **Reconfigure the value chain** — adopt a *different and more efficient* way to design, produce, distribute, or market the product: a new process technology, direct sales instead of distributors, a new distribution channel, a new raw material, major changes in automation, or a fundamentally different business model. Reconfiguration often yields the most dramatic and most sustainable cost advantage because it changes the basis of competition rather than playing the same game more cheaply.

A cost leader pursues *both*, attacking cost throughout the value chain — not just in manufacturing.

## Sustainability of cost advantage

Cost advantage is sustainable when its sources are hard to replicate. Sustainability varies by driver: **scale, interrelationships, linkages, proprietary learning, and policy choices that reconfigure the chain** tend to be more sustainable; advantages based on a single low-cost input or an easily-imitated practice are not. The most sustainable positions come from *multiple* cost drivers reinforcing one another. Implementation matters too — cost discipline must be an ingrained part of the organization.

## Pitfalls in cost-leadership strategies

- **Focusing only on manufacturing/operations** and ignoring the cost of other activities (logistics, service, marketing, infrastructure).
- **Ignoring procurement** and the cost of purchased inputs.
- **Overlooking indirect and small activities** that collectively add up.
- **A false sense of competitor cost** — misreading rivals' drivers and chains.
- **Ignoring linkages** within the chain and with suppliers/channels.
- **Sacrificing differentiation** — cutting cost in ways buyers notice and dislike, forcing deep discounts.
- **Cross-subsidizing** cost reductions in a way that invites attack or hides an unprofitable segment.
- **Undermining advantage** by introducing variety or features that raise cost without raising willingness to pay.

## Steps in strategic cost analysis

1. Define the value chain and assign costs and assets.
2. Diagnose the cost drivers of each significant activity and how they interact.
3. Identify competitors' value chains and determine relative cost and its sources.
4. Develop a strategy to lower relative cost — by controlling cost drivers and/or reconfiguring the value chain — while maintaining acceptable differentiation.
5. Test the chosen actions for **sustainability**.
6. Ensure cost-reduction efforts do not erode differentiation, and watch the pitfalls above.
