# Competitive Scope: Segmentation, Focus, and Substitution

Competitive scope — how broadly or narrowly a firm competes — is a strategy variable in its own right. This file covers **industry segmentation** and **focus** (how to choose where to compete) and **substitution** (the boundary on what the industry can charge).

---

## Industry segmentation and competitive advantage

An industry is not homogeneous. **Industry segmentation** divides an industry into the parts where competitive advantage differs, so a firm can choose segments where it can build advantage and decide whether to compete broadly or focus. Segments differ because their **optimal value chains differ** — the cost and differentiation of serving them is not the same.

### Bases for segmentation

Segments arise from variation along four **structural variables**, each of which can affect the value chain:

1. **Product varieties** — the distinct products (or product types) the industry produces, including physically distinct items and differences in service, technology, or quality.
2. **Buyer types** — distinct kinds of end buyers (e.g., industry, size, ownership, sophistication, purchasing behavior).
3. **Channels** — the distribution channels used to reach buyers (direct, distributor, retailer, OEM).
4. **Geography** — regions, countries, or country groups where buyers and competitive conditions differ.

Any of these creates a segment when it implies a **different value chain** to serve it well or a different structure (different five forces). Test candidate segments for whether they have meaningful differences in *structural attractiveness* and in the *value chain* required.

### The industry segmentation matrix

Cross the most strategically significant variables (typically product variety × buyer type, sometimes adding channel and geography) to build a **segmentation matrix**. Cells in the matrix are candidate segments. Combine cells that have similar value chains and similar structure; separate those that don't. The matrix surfaces:
- **Relationships among segments** — which share activities and can be served together.
- **Unserved or poorly served segments** — white space.
- **New segments** — by finding new varieties, buyers, channels, or geographies others have missed.

### Segment attractiveness and the choice of focus

For each segment assess: its **structural attractiveness** (the five forces within the segment), its **size and growth**, and the firm's ability to build a sustainable advantage there given **interrelationships** with other segments (shared value chains that lower the cost of serving several segments).

Then decide scope:
- **Broadly-targeted strategy** — serve many segments and exploit interrelationships among their value chains (sharing activities to gain cost or differentiation advantage across segments). The broad competitor wins when interrelationships are strong.
- **Focus strategy** — serve one or a few segments and **tailor the value chain** to them. Focus wins when the target segment's optimal value chain differs enough from the rest that a broad competitor serving everyone is forced into compromises a focuser can exploit. Focus comes in two forms — **cost focus** and **differentiation focus**.

### Feasibility and sustainability of focus

A focus strategy is feasible when target segments are large enough to be profitable, have good growth potential, and are *not* central to the success of major broad-based competitors. Focus is **sustainable** against broad competitors when:
- The focuser's advantage from tailoring resists the broad competitor's advantage from sharing,
- Other segments are hard to serve with the same chain (no easy straddling), and
- Sub-segmentation by other focusers is limited.

Focus is **eroded** when the cost/differentiation gap between broad and narrow scope widens, when the differences between the target and other segments narrow, or when rivals find finer sub-segments within the target. A focuser must guard against **becoming stuck in the middle** by drifting to serve everyone.

## Substitution

**Substitutes** are products or services that perform the *same generic function* for the buyer through different means. Substitution sets a ceiling on the price an industry can charge and is one of the five forces. A firm must understand the substitution threat to defend its position — or to *attack* through substitution.

### Identifying substitutes

Identify substitutes by the **function performed in the buyer's value chain**, not by physical similarity. Anything that meets the same buyer need is a candidate — including the option of doing without, doing it in-house, or using the product more efficiently. Cast the net wide; the dangerous substitute is often the one that doesn't look like the product at all.

### The economics of substitution

A buyer switches to a substitute based on its **relative value/price** versus **switching costs**:
- **Relative value/price** — the substitute's value to the buyer relative to its price, accounting for *all* the costs and benefits in the buyer's value chain (not just purchase price): performance, the cost of switching, and the value of any features lost or gained.
- **Switching costs** — the one-time costs the buyer faces in changing to the substitute (retraining, new equipment, testing, redesign, risk). High switching costs slow substitution even when the substitute is economically superior.
- **Buyer propensity to substitute** — buyers vary in willingness; some are more prone (resource-rich, risk-tolerant, sophisticated) and substitution often proceeds segment by segment.

### The path of substitution

Substitution typically follows an **S-shaped path** and proceeds unevenly across **segments** — penetrating the most receptive segments first. The path can be forecast by analyzing the relative value/price trajectory (which usually improves for the substitute over time through its own learning and scale) and the segments in order of propensity.

### Substitution and competitive strategy

A firm can take two stances:
- **Defense against substitutes** — raise switching costs, improve relative value/price, reposition toward segments least exposed, or improve the product to close the value gap. Defense can be **industry-wide** (collective action, e.g., joint promotion of the product category) or **firm-specific**.
- **Promotion of substitution** — when the firm *is* the substitute, accelerate it by improving relative value/price, lowering switching costs, targeting the most-prone segments first, and investing to move down the cost curve.

### Pitfalls

- Defining substitutes too narrowly (missing non-obvious threats).
- Misjudging the substitution path by ignoring segmentation.
- Defending a position substitution will inevitably overrun, instead of harvesting or repositioning.
- Failing to promote a favorable substitution aggressively enough while the window is open.
