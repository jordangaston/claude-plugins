# Corporate, Offensive, and Defensive Strategy

This file covers competitive advantage *beyond a single business unit* (interrelationships, horizontal strategy, complements) and *competitive dynamics* (technology strategy, competitor selection, offensive and defensive strategy, attacking a leader, and planning under uncertainty). The unifying idea: the value chain is still the analytical engine — it just extends across business units, across products, and across time.

---

## Interrelationships among business units

A diversified firm's competitive advantage at the corporate level comes from **interrelationships** — tangible and intangible links between the value chains of its business units. Horizontal strategy (managing across units) is what turns a collection of businesses into something worth more than the sum of its parts. Interrelationships are the reason a diversified firm can outperform a portfolio of independents — and the reason most diversification *fails to* add value (because the interrelationships were illusory).

### Three types of interrelationships

1. **Tangible interrelationships** — opportunities to **share value activities** among business units because they share buyers, channels, technologies, or other factors. Sharing a sales force, logistics system, component, R&D, or procurement lowers cost (if it raises scale, learning, or utilization in the shared activity) or enhances differentiation. Sharing has **costs** too: coordination, compromise (the shared activity may not be optimal for each unit), and inflexibility. Sharing creates advantage only when the benefit exceeds these costs. Match interrelationships by finding activities whose value chains overlap.
2. **Intangible interrelationships** — the transfer of **management know-how** between value chains that are *similar* but don't share activities (similar generic strategy, buyer type, configuration of activities, or kind of activity). Value comes from applying expertise gained in one unit to another. Harder to capture and easier to fool yourself about than tangible ones.
3. **Competitor interrelationships** — links that arise from competing with the **same rival in multiple businesses** (multipoint competitors). A move against a multipoint competitor in one business invites response in another; this changes the calculus of competition and must be managed deliberately.

### Horizontal strategy

**Horizontal strategy** coordinates the goals and strategies of related business units. It is explicit strategy at the *group/sector/corporate* level — without it, business units will pursue interrelationships haphazardly or not at all, and will undervalue them. Horizontal strategy should drive **diversification**: diversify into businesses where tangible interrelationships (or, more cautiously, intangible ones) create real advantage — including diversification through **beachheads** (entering a new area to build toward a network of related positions).

### Achieving interrelationships

Interrelationships fail not because they don't exist but because of **organizational impediments** — business-unit autonomy, incentives tied to unit (not shared) results, and the difficulty of coordinating across the org chart. Overcoming them requires **horizontal organization**: horizontal structure (groupings, shared activities managed jointly), horizontal systems (planning and capital-budgeting that account for shared benefit), horizontal human-resource practices (rotation, shared incentives), and horizontal conflict-resolution. The corporate role is to make these mechanisms work.

## Complementary products and competitive advantage

**Complements** are products used *together* with the firm's product (the function of one depends on the other). They can be a competitive weapon in three ways:

- **Control over complements** — competing in or controlling strategically important complements can create advantage (better joint performance, control of the standard, capturing more of the buyer's spend). Worth doing when the complement strongly affects the buyer's perception or use of the firm's product.
- **Bundling** — selling a set of products together as a package. Bundling creates advantage through cost savings, differentiation (an integrated solution), and raised barriers — but risks alienating buyers who want components, and inviting **unbundled** competitors who pick off pieces. Choose bundled vs. unbundled (or both) based on buyer needs and the structure of competition.
- **Cross-subsidization** — deliberately pricing one product low (even at a loss) to profit on a complement (razor/blades). Favored when buyers value the system, switching is locked in after the first purchase, and the firm can capture the follow-on profit. It carries the risk of attack on the profitable element.

Complements and bundling decisions interact with industry evolution — what is optimally bundled early may unbundle as the market matures (and vice versa).

## Technology and competitive advantage

Technological change is among the most powerful drivers of competitive advantage and industry structure — but technological change matters strategically *only when it affects competitive advantage or industry structure*; novelty for its own sake is not strategy.

- **Technology and the value chain** — technology is embodied in *every* value activity (not just R&D/product). A firm's "technologies" include product, process, logistics, marketing, and administrative technologies. Technological change creates advantage when it lowers cost or enhances differentiation in an activity and the lead is **sustainable**, when it shifts cost or uniqueness drivers in the firm's favor, or when it gives first-mover advantages.
- **Technology strategy** has three elements: *which technologies to develop* (those that most affect cost or differentiation and are sustainable), *whether to seek technological leadership or followership* (leadership pays when the lead is sustainable, there are first-mover advantages, and being first improves cost/differentiation; followership pays when imitation is cheap and first movers bear the cost of educating the market), and *the role of technology licensing* (license when it doesn't surrender a sustainable advantage and captures value — e.g., from "bad" licensees who won't become threats, to set a standard, or to access otherwise-unavailable markets).
- **Technological evolution** — distinguish *continuous* from *discontinuous* change and forecast it; discontinuities are where new entrants overturn leaders.

## Competitor selection

Counterintuitively, the **right competitors strengthen rather than weaken** a firm's position. "Good" competitors can:
- **Increase competitive advantage** — absorb demand swings, serve unattractive segments, raise the cost of differentiation for everyone (validating the category), and provide a cost umbrella.
- **Improve industry structure** — increase total demand, reinforce desirable structure, and aid market development.
- **Deter entry and substitution** — a well-populated industry with credible competitors is less inviting to entrants.

A **good competitor** plays by sound rules: it is credible and viable, understands the industry, holds realistic assumptions, knows its own costs, has a strategy that reinforces (rather than destabilizes) structure, and has modest goals for the firm's core segments. A firm should **influence the pattern of competitors** — help good competitors, contain bad ones, and steer toward an **optimal market configuration** (the mix and positioning of competitors that best supports the firm's own advantage and industry stability). Driving out *every* competitor can be a strategic mistake (antitrust exposure, loss of the benefits above, becoming the sole target).

## Offensive and defensive strategy

### Industry scenarios and strategy under uncertainty

When the future is genuinely uncertain, build **industry scenarios** — internally consistent views of how the industry's structure could evolve, built from the key **uncertainties** (independent and dependent). For each scenario, work out the implied structure and the right strategy. Then choose among approaches: **bet on the most probable scenario**, **hedge** (a strategy robust across scenarios), **preserve flexibility**, or **influence** the outcome toward the most favorable scenario. Sequence and combine moves as uncertainty resolves.

### Defensive strategy

Defense aims to **lower the probability of attack, divert attacks, or reduce their intensity** — making the firm a less attractive target so challengers go elsewhere or attack weakly. Three classes of defensive tactic:
1. **Raise structural barriers** — fill product/segment gaps, block channel access, raise buyer switching costs, raise the cost of an attacker's inputs, defensively invest in cost or differentiation.
2. **Increase expected retaliation** — signal commitment to defend (announcements, visible investment, a war chest), establish a reputation for vigorous response, and set "match any offer" precedents.
3. **Lower the inducement for attack** — reduce the profit targets that attract challengers (e.g., deliberate pricing discipline), and manage your own visible profitability.

The choice between **deterrence** (preventing the attack) and **response** (defeating it once underway) depends on cost and credibility. A firm should evaluate defensive tactics by cost vs. effectiveness, and recognize when **defense or disinvestment** (harvesting a position not worth defending) is the right call. Pitfall: defending a position that isn't worth the cost, or defending it in ways that damage the firm's own structure.

### Attacking an industry leader

Attacking a leader is high-risk but can succeed under the right conditions: the challenger has a **sustainable competitive advantage** (cost or differentiation), has **cost or differentiation proximity** in other activities, and faces **impediments to leader retaliation** (the leader can't or won't respond effectively — e.g., antitrust, prior commitments, conflicting goals, or its own reputation). Three **avenues** for attack:
- **Reconfiguration** — a different value chain (new process, new channel, new distribution or service model) that the leader can't easily match without abandoning its own.
- **Redefinition** — change the competitive scope: re-segment, broaden or narrow, change geographic or vertical scope to alter the game.
- **Pure spending** — simply outspend the leader to buy share (least attractive — only viable with deep pockets and a real advantage to spend behind). **Alliances** can supply the muscle to attack.

Read **signals of leader vulnerability** — industry signals (substitution, structural change, new channels) and leader signals (discontent among buyers, cash-flow harvesting, eroding capability, antitrust or regulatory constraints). Attack when these align with a sustainable advantage and impediments to retaliation.

## How this connects back

Every move here is evaluated by the same yardstick as the single-business analysis: does it improve the firm's **cost position or differentiation** in a **sustainable** way, given the structure of the industry and the configuration of competitors? Corporate, technology, and competitive-dynamics decisions are powerful precisely because they change the value chain, the scope, or the rules — not just how the existing game is played.
