# Differentiation

A firm differentiates itself from competitors when it provides something **unique that is valuable to buyers** beyond simply offering a low price. Differentiation allows a firm to command a **premium price**, sell more at a given price, or gain buyer loyalty. It leads to superior performance when the price premium achieved exceeds the extra cost of being unique. The fundamental questions: where can the firm be unique, and does that uniqueness create value the buyer is willing to pay for?

## Sources of differentiation and the value chain

Differentiation arises from the firm's value chain. **Virtually any activity is a potential source** of uniqueness — not just the product or marketing. Procurement of high-quality inputs, technology development, operations that yield consistency, outbound logistics that ensure availability, and service that maintains performance can all differentiate. To find the sources, examine every activity for the ways it does, or could, create something valuable for the buyer.

## Drivers of uniqueness

Just as cost has drivers, uniqueness in an activity is governed by **drivers of uniqueness**:
- **Policy choices** — what activities to perform and how (features, services offered, content, technology employed, skill and experience of personnel, information provided to buyers). The most pervasive driver; uniqueness is often a deliberate choice.
- **Linkages** — within the value chain (uniqueness depends on coordination, e.g., faster service from linking order processing and service), with suppliers, and with channels.
- **Timing** — being first to offer a differentiating attribute, or the advantages of being a late mover.
- **Location** — e.g., the most convenient sites.
- **Interrelationships** — sharing across business units to offer the buyer something rivals can't (a unified product line, single point of contact).
- **Learning and spillover** — learning to perform an activity in a uniquely good way (proprietary learning is more durable).
- **Integration** — taking on activities (or having suppliers/channels do them) to gain control over differentiation.
- **Scale** — large scale that permits an activity to be performed in a way impossible at smaller scale (e.g., the best service network).
- **Institutional factors** — e.g., a good union relationship enabling unique work practices.

A firm differentiates by **controlling these drivers** in ways rivals don't — and uniqueness is more sustainable when it stems from multiple drivers and from linkages rather than a single feature.

## Buyer value: the basis of differentiation

Uniqueness only matters if it creates **value for the buyer**. A firm creates value for its buyer that justifies a premium price in two basic ways:

1. **Lowering the buyer's cost** — anything that reduces the buyer's cost of using, operating, or owning the product: lower delivered cost, faster/more reliable delivery, lower failure rates, lower installation or training cost, lower maintenance, fewer rejects, less downtime. The product is an *input to the buyer's value chain*; differentiation that favorably affects that chain lowers buyer cost.
2. **Raising the buyer's performance** — enabling the buyer to perform better, meet its own needs more fully, or (for a consumer) gain greater satisfaction. For an industrial buyer, this means helping the buyer differentiate *its* product or improve *its* results.

To find these, you must understand the **buyer's value chain** and how your product is actually used within it. Differentiation requires knowing the buyer better than the buyer knows itself.

## The real buyer, and channels

The "buyer" is not monolithic. For industrial products, the *purchasing decision* may involve users, the purchasing department, technical staff, and senior management — each weighing different criteria. For consumer products, **channels** are buyers too and influence the end consumer. Identify *who* the real buyers and influencers are, and what each values.

## Buyer purchase criteria

Translate buyer value into the specific criteria buyers use, which fall into two categories:

- **Use criteria** — criteria stemming from the way the supplier affects *actual buyer value*: product quality, features, delivery time, application engineering, reliability, support. These measure what *creates* buyer value.
- **Signaling criteria** — criteria stemming from *signals of value*: the cues a buyer uses to infer or perceive value, such as reputation, advertising, packaging, appearance of facilities, the professionalism of personnel, installed base, price (as a quality signal). These measure how buyers *perceive* the presence of value.

Both matter. **Signaling criteria can be as important as use criteria**, because buyers won't pay for value they don't *perceive* — and often *can't* fully assess use value before (or even after) purchase.

## Signaling value

Because buyers cannot always perceive how well a firm meets their use criteria, the firm must **signal** value. A firm that creates real value but fails to signal it will not earn the premium it deserves. Conversely, signals are credible only when backed by real value over time. The **cost of signaling** (advertising, demonstrations, reputation-building) is part of the cost of differentiation. The gap between *value created* and *value perceived* is where signaling investment pays off.

## The cost of differentiation

Differentiation is usually costly — uniqueness often requires performing activities more expensively (better inputs, more service, more R&D, more training). The firm must weigh the cost of each source of uniqueness against the **premium it commands**:
- Differentiate in ways where the firm has a **cost advantage in differentiating** (it can be unique more cheaply than rivals).
- Reduce the cost of activities that **don't affect buyer value**.
- Achieve **cost proximity** in everything not related to the differentiation, so the premium isn't eaten by excess cost elsewhere.
- Exploit sources of differentiation that are **not costly** (linkages, coordination, timing).

The aim is a price premium that exceeds the cost incurred in being different.

## Differentiation strategy and routes to differentiation

A sound differentiation strategy makes the firm **uniquely valuable to buyers in a way that is sustainable**. Routes to enhancing differentiation:
- Enhance the sources of uniqueness across the value chain.
- Make the **cost of differentiation an advantage** (differentiate cheaply; control the cost of signaling).
- Change the rules to create uniqueness — reconfigure the value chain to differentiate in a wholly new way.
- **Transform the basis of competition** to the firm's unique strengths.
- Discover **unrecognized purchase criteria** and meet them before rivals do.
- Respond rapidly to changing buyer circumstances and criteria.

## Sustainability of differentiation

Differentiation is sustainable when it is built on:
- **Sources buyers continue to value** (durable buyer need),
- **Sources that are hard to imitate** — ideally resting on the firm's *unique skills and proprietary learning*, on *linkages and interrelationships* (hard to see and replicate), and on *first-mover signals/reputation*, and
- **Cost advantage in differentiating** so rivals can't match the uniqueness profitably.

Multiple sources of differentiation are far more sustainable than a single attribute. The firm must keep raising the bar as buyer needs evolve.

## Pitfalls in differentiation

- **Uniqueness that buyers don't value** — being different without creating buyer value.
- **Too much differentiation** — exceeding buyers' needs, so the premium can't be justified.
- **A price premium that is too high** — outrunning the value delivered.
- **Ignoring the need to signal value** — creating value buyers can't perceive.
- **Not knowing the cost of differentiation** — pursuing uniqueness that costs more than its premium.
- **Focusing on the product** instead of the whole value chain (and the buyer's value chain).
- **Failing to recognize buyer segments** with different needs and criteria (see `scope.md`).

## Steps in differentiation

1. Determine who the **real buyer** is (and channels/influencers).
2. Identify the **buyer's value chain** and the firm's impact on it.
3. Determine ranked **buyer purchase criteria** (use and signaling).
4. Assess existing and potential **sources of uniqueness** in the firm's value chain.
5. Identify the **cost** of each source of differentiation.
6. Choose the configuration of value activities that creates the **most valuable differentiation relative to its cost**.
7. Test the chosen differentiation for **sustainability**.
8. Reduce cost in activities that don't affect the chosen forms of differentiation.
