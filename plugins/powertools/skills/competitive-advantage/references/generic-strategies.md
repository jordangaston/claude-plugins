# The Five Forces and the Three Generic Strategies

## Industry structure: the five forces (context)

Competitive advantage is *relative*. Before positioning a firm, understand the structure of its industry — the five competitive forces that determine industry profitability and set the arena in which advantage is won. (This is the bridge from Porter's *Competitive Strategy*; here it is context, not the main event.)

1. **Rivalry among existing competitors** — price competition, advertising battles, new products, service. Intense when competitors are numerous or equally balanced, growth is slow, fixed costs are high, exit barriers are high, or the product lacks differentiation.
2. **Threat of new entrants** — held back by barriers to entry: economies of scale, product differentiation, capital requirements, switching costs, access to distribution, cost advantages independent of scale, government policy, and expected retaliation.
3. **Threat of substitute products** — alternatives that perform the same function cap the price the industry can charge (see `scope.md`).
4. **Bargaining power of buyers** — strong when buyers are concentrated, purchase in volume, face low switching costs, or the product is undifferentiated.
5. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — strong when suppliers are concentrated, the input is critical, or substitutes are scarce.

The collective strength of the forces determines the *average* profitability of the industry. A firm's job is to find a position within the industry where it can best defend against these forces or influence them in its favor. **Industry structure is relatively stable but not fixed** — a firm's strategy can change it, for better or worse.

## The two basic types of competitive advantage

Despite the multitude of strengths and weaknesses a firm can have, there are only **two basic types of competitive advantage**: **lower cost** and **differentiation**. Both stem ultimately from industry structure — they result from a firm's ability to cope with the five forces better than its rivals.

- **Lower cost** — the ability to design, produce, and market a comparable product more efficiently than competitors.
- **Differentiation** — the ability to provide unique and superior value to the buyer in terms of product quality, special features, or after-sale service, commanding a premium price.

## The three generic strategies

The two types of advantage, combined with the **scope** of activities a firm seeks to achieve them across, lead to three generic strategies for above-average performance:

| | **Lower Cost** | **Differentiation** |
|---|---|---|
| **Broad target** | 1. Cost Leadership | 2. Differentiation |
| **Narrow target** | 3a. Cost Focus | 3b. Differentiation Focus |

The fundamental point: **competitive advantage is at the heart of any strategy, and achieving it requires making a choice.** A firm must choose the *type* of advantage it seeks and the *scope* within which it will achieve it. Being "all things to all people" guarantees strategic mediocrity and below-average performance.

### 1. Cost leadership

The firm sets out to be the **low-cost producer** in its industry across a broad range of segments. The sources of cost advantage are varied (economies of scale, proprietary technology, preferential access to raw materials — see `cost-advantage.md`). A cost leader must find and exploit *all* sources of cost advantage. **A cost leader cannot ignore differentiation** — its product must be perceived as comparable or acceptable to buyers, or it will be forced to discount well below competitors and erode its advantage. If two or more firms vie for cost leadership, the rivalry is usually fierce because every point of share is critical. There can typically be **only one** cost leader.

### 2. Differentiation

The firm seeks to be **unique in its industry along dimensions widely valued by buyers**. It selects one or more attributes that buyers perceive as important and positions itself uniquely to meet those needs, rewarded with a **premium price**. The means of differentiation are specific to each industry (product, delivery, marketing, service, breadth). **A differentiator cannot ignore cost** — the premium must exceed the extra cost of being unique, and the firm should pursue cost parity or proximity in everything not related to its differentiation. Unlike cost leadership, there can be *more than one* successful differentiator if multiple attributes are widely valued.

### 3. Focus

The firm chooses a **narrow competitive scope** — a segment or group of segments — and tailors its strategy to serve them to the exclusion of others, seeking advantage *in its target segments even though it has no overall advantage*. Focus has two variants:
- **Cost focus** — seeking a cost advantage in the target segment.
- **Differentiation focus** — seeking differentiation in the target segment.

Focus exploits **differences in the optimal value chain** between the target segment and the industry as a whole — segments that are poorly served by broadly-targeted competitors, or segments with unusual needs. If the target segment is no different from others, focus fails (see `scope.md`).

## Stuck in the middle

A firm that **engages in each generic strategy but achieves none** is "stuck in the middle." It possesses no competitive advantage and is almost guaranteed below-average profitability. It loses high-volume, price-sensitive buyers to the cost leader and high-margin buyers to the differentiators; it loses both ends to focusers. Being stuck in the middle is often a sign of a firm's unwillingness to make choices about how to compete — it grabs at any advantage and loses all of them. **A focuser that becomes stuck in the middle** is one that exploited its target segment to grow but then lost focus and tried to serve everyone.

## Pursuing more than one generic strategy

The strategies are usually inconsistent because each requires different resources, skills, organizational arrangements, and management cultures, and because cost leadership and differentiation usually trade off. A firm should almost always make a clear choice. Pursuing both is possible only in specific circumstances:
- **Competitors are stuck in the middle** — leaving room temporarily; not durable.
- **Cost is strongly affected by share or interrelationships** — a firm that pioneers a major innovation or exploits sharing can gain both for a time.
- **A firm pioneers a major innovation** that simultaneously lowers cost and raises differentiation (e.g., a process technology, or a fundamentally better business model).

Even then, the dual position is usually temporary; competitors eventually force the firm to choose. **Treat the simultaneous pursuit as the exception, not the goal.**

## How each strategy defends against the five forces

Each generic strategy is a fundamentally different route to competitive advantage *and* a different way of insulating the firm from the five forces:
- A **cost leader** has a buffer against rivalry (can still profit when rivals compete away their margins), against powerful buyers (only to the floor of the next-most-efficient competitor's cost), against powerful suppliers (more flexibility to absorb input-cost increases), and high entry/substitution barriers from scale or cost advantage.
- A **differentiator** earns buyer loyalty and lower price sensitivity, fatter margins that ease supplier pressure, and barriers from uniqueness and switching costs; substitutes are held off by perceived distinctiveness.
- A **focuser** earns these defenses *within its segment* by serving it better than broadly-targeted rivals can.

## Sustainability

A generic strategy delivers above-average performance only if the advantage is **sustainable** — resistant to erosion by competitor behavior and industry evolution. Sustainability requires that the firm possess **barriers** that make imitation difficult. Each strategy carries characteristic risks:
- **Cost leadership** is eroded by technological change that nullifies past investment, low-cost imitation by followers, inability to see needed product change because of cost fixation, and inflation that narrows the gap to differentiators.
- **Differentiation** is eroded when the cost gap to low-cost rivals grows too wide, buyers' need for the differentiating factor falls, imitation narrows perceived differentiation, or focusers out-differentiate within segments.
- **Focus** is eroded when the cost or differentiation gap between broad and narrow scope widens, the differences between target and other segments narrow, or rivals find sub-segments within the focuser's target.

Sustainability ultimately comes from continuously investing to *improve* position, not just defending it. Strategy is choosing a *different* set of activities to deliver a unique mix of value — and the test of a real strategy is whether competitors would have to give something up to match it.
