## 1. Discover Phase Methods

The Discover phase is about building empathy and gathering evidence. The goal is to understand the problem space deeply enough that the team can make informed decisions about what to focus on.

### User Interviews
The most versatile discovery tool. Conduct 5-8 focused interviews with people who experience the problem you're exploring.

Planning an interview:
- Define 1-2 learning goals before writing questions
- Target specific user segments, not "anyone"
- Ask about past behavior, not hypothetical futures ("Tell me about the last time you..." not "Would you ever...")
- Record with permission and debrief immediately after each session

### Observation / Contextual Inquiry
Watch people in their natural environment as they encounter the problem. Don't just ask what they do — watch what they actually do. The gap between reported and observed behavior is where the best insights live.

Approach:
- Shadow users during relevant tasks
- Note workarounds, frustrations, moments of confusion
- Ask "why" in the moment when you see unexpected behavior
- Document with photos, sketches, or screen recordings (with consent)

### Stakeholder Interviews
Talk to internal stakeholders (product, engineering, sales, support) to understand business context, constraints, and existing knowledge. These conversations surface what the organization already knows (and assumes) about the problem.

Key questions:
- What do you believe the core problem is? (Surfaces assumptions to test)
- What would success look like for this project?
- What constraints should we be aware of?
- What prior research or data exists?

### Market and Competitor Research
Map the competitive landscape and existing solutions. Understand how others have approached similar problems and where gaps remain.

Activities:
- Audit 3-5 competitor products or services
- Read customer reviews of competitor products (goldmine for unmet needs)
- Analyze industry reports and trends
- Map the user's current journey across existing tools and touchpoints

### Data Analysis
Use quantitative data to complement qualitative research. Analytics, support tickets, NPS scores, churn data, and usage patterns can reveal problems at scale.

Look for:
- Drop-off points in user flows
- Features with low adoption despite high availability
- Support ticket themes and frequency
- Behavioral segments (power users vs. occasional users vs. churned)

### Journey Mapping
Map the end-to-end experience a user has with the problem space. Include their actions, thoughts, emotions, and pain points at each stage. Journey maps make the user's experience tangible for the whole team.

### Empathy Mapping
A lightweight tool to organize what you've learned about a user segment. Four quadrants: what they Say, Think, Do, and Feel. Useful as a warm-up or synthesis exercise during or after research.

## 2. Define Phase Methods

The Define phase takes the raw, messy output of Discovery and distills it into a clear problem statement that the team can act on. This is convergent thinking — filtering, prioritizing, and committing.

### Affinity Mapping
The workhorse synthesis method. Take individual observations, quotes, and data points (on sticky notes or digital equivalents), then cluster them into themes. Let the patterns emerge from the data rather than imposing categories.

Process:
1. Write each observation/quote on a separate note
2. Silently cluster related notes (no talking — let the data speak)
3. Name each cluster with a descriptive theme
4. Identify the themes that appear most frequently or with strongest emotional signal
5. Rank themes by importance and frequency

### "How Might We" (HMW) Framing
Transform insights into actionable design challenges using "How Might We..." questions. HMWs are broad enough to invite creative solutions but specific enough to provide direction.

Formula: "How might we [verb] [user/context] so that [desired outcome]?"

Examples:
- "How might we help busy marketing teams gather client feedback so that projects stay on track?"
- "How might we reduce the time new users spend configuring their account so that they reach value faster?"

Good HMWs are neither too broad ("How might we improve the product?") nor too narrow ("How might we add a dropdown to the settings page?").

### Problem Statement
A more structured alternative to HMW. A good problem statement includes:
- Who has the problem (specific user segment)
- What the problem is (the gap between current and desired state)
- Why it matters (impact on the user and/or business)
- What constraints exist

Template: "[User segment] needs a way to [user need] because [insight from research], but currently [current state/barrier]. This matters because [impact]."

### Root Cause Analysis
When you've identified a problem area, dig deeper to find the root cause rather than treating symptoms.

Methods:
- **5 Whys**: Ask "why" five times in succession to drill past symptoms to root causes
- **Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagram**: Map contributing factors across categories (people, process, technology, environment) to a central problem

### Prioritization Frameworks
When multiple problems surface, use a framework to decide which to tackle:

- **Impact/Effort matrix**: Plot problems on a 2x2 of user impact vs. effort to solve. Start with high-impact, low-effort.
- **User story mapping**: Arrange user needs into a map of activities, tasks, and stories to identify the critical path.
- **Dot voting**: Quick democratic prioritization — each team member gets limited votes to place on their top choices.

## 3. Facilitating the Transition

The bridge from Discover to Define is where many teams stall. They have a mountain of research and don't know how to move forward. Here's a practical sequence:

1. **Debrief after each research session** (15 min). Capture key quotes, surprises, and hypotheses while they're fresh.
2. **Run a synthesis workshop** (2-4 hours). Bring the team together, do affinity mapping, identify top themes.
3. **Draft 3-5 HMW questions** from the top themes. Share with the team and stakeholders.
4. **Vote and align** on 1-2 HMWs to carry into Diamond 2.
5. **Write a design brief** that captures the chosen problem, the evidence behind it, any constraints, and the success criteria.

The design brief is the handoff artifact. It gives Diamond 2 clear direction without prescribing solutions.

## 4. Deliverable Templates

### Research Plan Template
```
# Research Plan: [Project Name]

## Decision to Inform
What decision will this research help us make?

## Learning Goals
1. [What we need to learn]
2. [What we need to learn]

## Target Participants
- Segment: [who]
- Criteria: [specific attributes]
- Recruitment: [how we'll find them]
- Sample size: [how many]

## Methods
| Method | Purpose | Timeline |
|--------|---------|----------|
| [e.g., User interviews] | [what we'll learn] | [when] |

## Logistics
- Timeline: [start] to [end]
- Team roles: [who does what]
- Tools: [recording, note-taking, etc.]
```

### Design Brief Template
```
# Design Brief: [Project Name]

## Problem Statement
[HMW or structured problem statement]

## Evidence
- [Key finding 1 with supporting data/quotes]
- [Key finding 2]
- [Key finding 3]

## User Segment
[Who we're solving for, based on research]

## Success Criteria
- [How we'll know if we've solved the problem]

## Constraints
- [Timeline, technical, budget, team limitations]

## Out of Scope
- [What we're explicitly NOT solving in this cycle]
```
