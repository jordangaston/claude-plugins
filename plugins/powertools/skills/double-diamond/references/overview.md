## 1. Origins and Mental Model

The Double Diamond was popularized by the British Design Council in 2005, adapted from Béla H. Bánáthy's 1996 divergence-convergence model. The core insight is simple: good design requires two distinct cycles of opening up (exploring possibilities) and closing down (making decisions).

The shape matters. Each diamond widens as the team explores, then narrows as they converge. The widest point of each diamond is the moment of maximum ambiguity — and that's by design. Premature convergence (jumping to solutions too early) is the most common failure mode in product development.

**Mental model**: Think of it as two questions answered in sequence:
- Diamond 1: "Are we solving the right problem?"
- Diamond 2: "Are we solving it the right way?"

## 2. The Two Diamonds Explained

### Diamond 1: The Problem Space

The first diamond is about understanding before acting. Teams resist the urge to build and instead invest in deeply understanding the people they're designing for, the context they operate in, and the real (not assumed) problems.

**Discover** (divergent): Cast a wide net. Talk to users, observe behavior, analyze data, study competitors, map the ecosystem. The goal is to surface surprises — things the team didn't know they didn't know.

**Define** (convergent): Synthesize all that messy research into a clear problem statement. Filter, prioritize, and align the team around a single challenge worth solving. The output is a brief or problem frame that gives the second diamond clear direction.

The transition between Diamond 1 and Diamond 2 is a critical handoff. If the problem statement is weak, everything downstream suffers.

### Diamond 2: The Solution Space

The second diamond takes the well-defined problem and explores how to solve it. Teams generate many ideas before narrowing to the strongest, then test and refine until they're confident enough to ship.

**Develop** (divergent): Brainstorm broadly. Sketch, wireframe, prototype. Explore multiple solution directions — don't fall in love with the first idea. Use low-fidelity artifacts so ideas are cheap to create and easy to throw away.

**Deliver** (convergent): Test the most promising solutions with real users. Iterate based on feedback. Increase fidelity as confidence grows. Ship, measure, and learn.

## 3. Phase Summary Table

| Phase | Mode | Key Question | Primary Activities | Key Output |
|-------|------|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| Discover | Divergent | What's really going on? | User interviews, observation, market research, data analysis, stakeholder interviews | Research findings, empathy maps, journey maps |
| Define | Convergent | What's the real problem? | Affinity mapping, insight synthesis, prioritization, problem framing | Problem statement / HMW / design brief |
| Develop | Divergent | How might we solve this? | Brainstorming, sketching, Crazy 8s, low-fi prototyping, concept testing | Shortlisted concepts, prototypes |
| Deliver | Convergent | Does this actually work? | Usability testing, A/B testing, pilot programs, iteration, launch | Validated solution, shipped product |

## 4. Common Pitfalls

**Skipping Diamond 1**: The most frequent mistake. Teams jump straight to solutions because they think they already understand the problem. They usually don't — or they understand a symptom rather than a root cause.

**Convergence theater**: Going through the motions of the Define or Deliver phase without actually making hard choices. Every idea gets kept, nothing is prioritized, and the team moves forward with a fuzzy mandate.

**Stakeholder-driven problem statements**: When a senior stakeholder declares the problem upfront, teams often treat it as given rather than a hypothesis to validate. Discovery exists to challenge assumptions — including from leadership.

**Prototype fidelity mismatch**: Building high-fidelity prototypes too early wastes time and makes teams reluctant to throw ideas away. Low-fi first, increase fidelity as confidence grows.

**One-pass thinking**: Treating the process as strictly linear. Real projects loop back. New information in the Develop phase might reframe the problem. A failed test in Deliver might send you back to Develop. This is normal and healthy.

## 5. When to Use (and When Not To)

**Strong fit:**
- Complex, poorly-defined problems
- New product development
- Service design and experience design
- Situations where the team disagrees on what the problem is
- Projects where user needs are unclear or assumed

**Less necessary:**
- Well-understood problems with clear technical solutions
- Minor bug fixes or incremental improvements
- Situations where extensive prior research already exists (you might skip or compress Diamond 1)
- Emergency fixes where speed matters more than exploration

**Adapt, don't skip**: Even when compressing, maintain the diverge-converge rhythm. A one-day version still benefits from: explore → focus → ideate → test.

## 6. The Evolved Double Diamond (2019)

In 2019, the Design Council released an updated version acknowledging two things the original model under-emphasized:

1. **Iteration**: Explicit loops between phases. The process is not waterfall — teams move back and forth as they learn.
2. **Enabling factors**: The conditions that make the process work — leadership buy-in, diverse teams, psychological safety, and a culture that values exploration alongside delivery.
3. **Design principles**: User-centered design, visual communication, collaboration, and iteration are embedded throughout, not just in specific phases.

The evolved model also positions the Double Diamond within a broader "systemic design" framework, acknowledging that real-world problems exist within complex systems and the framework should account for interdependencies.

For practical purposes, the four phases remain the same. The evolution is a reminder that the process is iterative, the environment matters, and design doesn't happen in a vacuum.
