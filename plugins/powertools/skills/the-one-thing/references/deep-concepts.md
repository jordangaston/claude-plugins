## 1. The Focusing Question

The Focusing Question has two parts, and both matter:

**Big-picture version**: "What's my ONE Thing?"
Used for direction-setting — choosing which domain or goal to pursue.

**Small-focus version**: "What's my ONE Thing right now?"
Used for execution — deciding the next action in service of the big picture.

**The full question**: "What's the ONE Thing I can do such that by doing it everything else will be
easier or unnecessary?"

Three forces are at work inside this question:

- **"What's the ONE Thing I can do"** — forces a single answer, not a list. It also implies agency:
  something you *can* do, not something you wish would happen.
- **"such that by doing it"** — establishes a causal link. The ONE Thing isn't just important in
  isolation; it creates a chain reaction.
- **"everything else will be easier or unnecessary"** — this is the leverage test. If doing this thing
  doesn't make other things easier or eliminate them, it's not the lead domino.

**How to use the question across life areas:**

Apply it per domain. "For my [work / health / relationship / finances / learning], what's the ONE
Thing I can do such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"

Then apply it across time horizons using Goal Setting to the Now (GSTTN).

**The path to great answers:**

The book distinguishes between three types of answers:

1. **Doable** — something you already know how to do. Low bar, low impact.
2. **Stretch** — at the edge of your current ability. Better, but often not enough.
3. **Possibility** — requires research, new learning, or innovation. This is where extraordinary
   results live. The answer may not be obvious yet, and that's the point.

When helping someone find their ONE Thing, push them toward possibility answers. If their answer
feels immediately comfortable and achievable, ask: "Is there a bigger version of this that would
require you to do things differently?"

## 2. The Six Lies Between You and Success

These are beliefs that sound true but mislead. Diagnose which ones are active in a user's thinking.

### Lie #1: Everything Matters Equally

**The truth**: Things are never equal. Pareto's Principle (80/20) proves that a minority of efforts
produce the majority of results. Most to-do lists are survival lists, not success lists. A success
list is built by applying Pareto until you reach the essential ONE thing.

**Diagnostic signs**: The user has a long to-do list with no hierarchy. They describe many things as
"important." They say "I need to get to everything." They feel busy but not productive.

**Antidote**: Convert to-do lists into success lists by applying Extreme Pareto (see section 4).

### Lie #2: Multitasking

**The truth**: Humans cannot focus on two cognitive tasks simultaneously. What looks like
multitasking is actually task-switching, which exacts a severe cost: up to 28% of an average workday
lost to switching inefficiency. Chronic multitaskers make more mistakes, make worse decisions, and
experience more stress.

Research by Clifford Nass at Stanford found that heavy multitaskers were outperformed on every
measure — they were, in his words, "lousy at everything."

**Diagnostic signs**: The user describes doing multiple things at once as a virtue. They keep many
browser tabs, apps, or contexts open simultaneously. They describe constant interruptions as normal.

**Antidote**: Time blocking. Protect focused work time and batch similar tasks together.

### Lie #3: A Disciplined Life

**The truth**: You don't need to be disciplined in everything — you need just enough discipline to
build one habit at a time. Success is not a marathon of disciplined action; it's a sprint fueled by
discipline just long enough for habit to kick in (research suggests ~66 days on average). People who
look "disciplined" have simply trained a handful of key habits.

**Diagnostic signs**: The user says "I need to be more disciplined." They try to overhaul their
entire routine at once. They set up elaborate systems with many rules.

**Antidote**: Pick one habit. Apply discipline only until it becomes automatic. Then move to the next.

### Lie #4: Willpower Is Always on Will-Call

**The truth**: Willpower is a finite resource that depletes throughout the day. It's like a battery —
full in the morning, drained by evening. This is why diets fail at dinner and resolutions collapse by
February. Decision fatigue is real.

**Diagnostic signs**: The user schedules their hardest, most important work for late in the day.
They make big decisions after a long day of small ones. They eat poorly late at night.

**Antidote**: Do your ONE Thing first, when willpower is highest. Don't fight your biology — use it.

### Lie #5: A Balanced Life

**The truth**: A perfectly balanced life is an unachievable myth. The pursuit of balance means giving
everything equal time, which means nothing gets extraordinary attention. Instead, think of life as
counterbalancing — going to extremes in one area for a period, then correcting back.

The key distinction: in your *work* life, go to extremes and counterbalance. In your *personal* life,
keep the counterbalancing shorter — don't let relationships, health, or personal growth go neglected
for too long.

**Diagnostic signs**: The user feels guilty about focusing on one thing because other areas are
"falling behind." They try to give equal time to every life area every day.

**Antidote**: Accept that extraordinary results require going out of balance. The question is not
*whether* to go out of balance, but how long to stay there before counterbalancing.

### Lie #6: Big Is Bad

**The truth**: Big is not bad — big is necessary. People who set extraordinary goals and fail still
accomplish more than people who set modest goals and succeed. The fear of big goals leads to small
thinking, which leads to small results. The Domino Effect proves that massive outcomes start with
small actions — but you have to be aiming at something worth the chain reaction.

**Diagnostic signs**: The user sets goals that feel "realistic" rather than extraordinary. They
hedge their ambition with phrases like "I'd be happy just to..." or "I'm not trying to change the
world, just..."

**Antidote**: Think big, act small. Set an extraordinary someday goal, then use GSTTN to find the
small action available right now.

## 3. The Domino Effect

A single domino can knock down another domino that is 50% larger. This was demonstrated by
physicist Lorne Whitehead in 1983 and reproduced experimentally.

The geometric progression is staggering:
- Domino #1: 2 inches
- Domino #10: roughly as tall as Peyton Manning
- Domino #18: rivals the Leaning Tower of Pisa
- Domino #23: taller than the Eiffel Tower
- Domino #31: taller than Mount Everest
- Domino #57: would bridge the earth to the moon

The metaphor encodes the core insight: **extraordinary success is sequential, not simultaneous.**
Each domino represents doing the right thing, which sets up the next right thing. Over time, the
effect is geometric, not linear.

Practical application: Don't try to knock down the biggest domino directly. Find the lead domino —
the smallest one that starts the chain reaction — and give it all your energy.

## 4. Extreme Pareto

Pareto's Principle (80/20) states that a minority of causes produce a majority of results. The book
pushes this further: apply 80/20 recursively.

Starting with a list of, say, 25 tasks:
- Round 1 (80/20): 5 tasks produce most of the results
- Round 2 (80/20 of 80/20): 1 task produces most of THOSE results

No matter how long the list, you can always narrow to ONE. This is Extreme Pareto, and it's how
to-do lists become success lists.

The narrowing process:
1. List everything you could do
2. Ask: "Of these, which 20% would produce 80% of the results I want?"
3. Of that 20%, ask again: "Which of these would produce the most impact?"
4. Repeat until ONE thing remains

## 5. The Three Commitments

To protect your ONE Thing and follow through, the book identifies three commitments:

### Commitment #1: Follow the path of mastery

Pursue being the best at your ONE Thing. Mastery is an asymptotic journey — you never fully arrive,
but the pursuit itself is the engine of extraordinary results. The research on deliberate practice
(~10,000 hours) suggests that 4 hours per day of focused effort for 10 years produces world-class
skill.

Mastery requires adopting a growth mindset: abilities are developed, not fixed. Each plateau is a
staging ground, not a ceiling.

### Commitment #2: Move from "E" to "P"

This is the shift from an Entrepreneurial approach (figuring it out yourself) to a Purposeful
approach (seeking the best practices, models, and mentors). Early on, you can wing it. But to
achieve extraordinary results, you must eventually research how the best people in your domain
operate and adopt their methods.

Ask: "Has anyone else achieved what I'm trying to achieve? What did they do? What's the current
best-known approach?"

### Commitment #3: Live the accountability cycle

Hold yourself accountable to your ONE Thing. This means:
- Finding an accountability partner (coach, mentor, peer)
- Reviewing your progress regularly (the weekly planning time block)
- Owning your outcomes — both the results and the lack of results

Accountability is not about punishment. It's about visibility and honest assessment. People who
write down their goals and share progress with someone else achieve significantly more.

## 6. The Four Thieves of Productivity

These are the forces that steal your focus even after you've identified your ONE Thing:

### Thief #1: Inability to Say No

Every yes to your ONE Thing requires saying no to many other things. This is the hardest thief
to defeat because saying no feels socially costly. But every time you say yes to something that
isn't your ONE Thing, you're saying no to your ONE Thing.

When Steve Jobs returned to Apple, he cut the product line from 350 to 10. That's 340 "nos."

Strategies: Practice saying "not now" instead of "never." Batch requests and respond to them
outside your time block. Give people a specific time when you'll be available. Remember that
people will respect clear boundaries more than they'll respect overcommitment.

### Thief #2: Fear of Chaos

When you focus on your ONE Thing, everything else *will* pile up. Emails will go unanswered.
Dishes will stack up. Minor tasks will queue. This is not a bug — it's the cost of extraordinary
focus. The fear of this chaos causes people to abandon their ONE Thing and retreat to managing
the small stuff.

Strategy: Accept chaos as the price of admission. Do a "chaos cleanup" during non-ONE-Thing
hours. Let some things simply not get done — they may turn out not to have mattered.

### Thief #3: Poor Health Habits

Physical energy is the fuel for focus. Without it, willpower depletes faster, focus degrades,
and the ONE Thing suffers. The book emphasizes four energy domains:

- **Physical**: Eat well, exercise, sleep enough
- **Emotional**: Spend time with people who matter
- **Mental**: Practice goal-setting, visualization, and planning
- **Spiritual**: Meditate, reflect, connect with purpose

Strategy: Time block your health first (it's part of "time off"). Morning routines that include
exercise, good nutrition, and adequate sleep are non-negotiable foundations.

### Thief #4: Environment Doesn't Support Your Goals

The people around you, your physical workspace, and the cultural norms you operate in all either
support or undermine your ONE Thing. If everyone around you multitasks, you'll feel pressure to
multitask. If your workspace is full of distractions, you'll be distracted.

Strategy: Curate your environment. Surround yourself with people who focus. Set up a physical
space that supports deep work. Communicate your time-blocking practice to the people affected by
it so they understand and support it rather than resisting it.

## 7. Purpose, Priority, Productivity

The book frames these three as an iceberg:

- **Productivity** (visible above the waterline) — your output, your results
- **Priority** (below the waterline) — what you've chosen to focus on
- **Purpose** (deepest layer) — why it matters to you

They're connected top to bottom:
- Purpose drives priority (knowing your "why" tells you what matters most)
- Priority drives productivity (knowing what matters most tells you where to focus your time)

If someone is struggling with productivity, the problem is often not time management — it's
unclear priority. And if priority is unclear, the problem is often undefined purpose.

The word "priority" was originally singular — there was no such thing as "priorities." It meant
THE most important thing. The pluralization of the word diluted its meaning. The ONE Thing skill
restores its original power.

## 8. Time Blocking

Time blocking is the operational system that protects your ONE Thing. Three blocks, in this order
of importance:

### Block 1: Time Off
Schedule vacations, rest days, and personal time FIRST. This goes on the calendar before anything
else. Counterintuitive but essential — you need recovery to sustain extraordinary focus.

### Block 2: Your ONE Thing
Block a minimum of 4 hours per day for focused work on your ONE Thing. This block is sacred:
- No interruptions
- No email, phone, or meetings during this time
- Early in the day when willpower is highest
- Same time every day to build habit

If 4 hours is impossible at first, start with the largest block available and grow from there.

### Block 3: Planning Time
One hour per week to:
- Review your GSTTN cascade
- Assess whether your current ONE Thing is still the lead domino
- Recalibrate weekly and daily goals
- Anticipate the Four Thieves for the coming week

The weekly planning review is what keeps the system alive. Without it, the cascade goes stale
and you drift back into reactive mode.

**Protecting time blocks**: A time block is only as good as your ability to defend it. Treat it
like a meeting with your most important client — because it is. Communicate it to others. Put it
on your calendar. Build a bunker around it.
