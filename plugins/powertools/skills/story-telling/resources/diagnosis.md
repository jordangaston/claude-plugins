# Diagnosis

A workflow for diagnosing and revising a draft, scene, outline, or pitch. Use this when a writer hands you work and asks "what's wrong?" or "how do I make this better?" Work top-down: structural failures first, line-level polish last, because polishing a broken structure wastes the writer's effort.

## How to run a diagnosis

1. **Read for what's working first.** Find the genuine strengths and name them specifically. This isn't politeness — it tells the writer what to protect during revision, and it earns the trust that makes hard notes land.
2. **Find the central value and the spine.** State, in one sentence each: what value the story moves and which way, and what the protagonist's driving quest is. If you *can't* state them from the draft, that's the headline problem — the story doesn't yet know what it's about.
3. **Walk the structural questions below**, in order. Stop at the first level that's broken; that's where revision must start.
4. **Give concrete fixes, not just notes.** "This scene doesn't turn" is a diagnosis; "open it with her confident she's got the job, end it with her realizing she's been used as leverage" is a fix. Offer options and let the writer choose.

## The diagnostic questions, top-down

### Level 1 — Premise & meaning
- Can you name the **central value** and its final charge? Can you state the **controlling idea** as one sentence?
- Is the **counter-idea** given real force, or is the theme argued unopposed (and therefore preachy)?
- Does any character *announce* the theme aloud? (If so, push it into subtext.)

### Level 2 — Structure & spine
- Is there a clear **inciting incident** that knocks the protagonist's life out of balance? Does it arrive early enough?
- Is there a single **spine** every major scene connects to? Which scenes drift off it?
- Does the middle **escalate** or merely **repeat**? Are the obstacles genuinely harder each time?
- Is there a real **crisis** — a hard choice between two goods or two evils, costly either way? Or is the "hard choice" actually easy?
- Does the **climax** flow from the protagonist's *choice*, or is it resolved by coincidence, rescue, or luck?
- Is the **resolution** the right length, or does it overstay?

### Level 3 — Character
- Is the protagonist **active** (drives events) or **passive** (events happen to them)? If passive, is that intentional and is the drive relocated inward?
- Is true character **revealed through choice under pressure**, or just asserted through characterization?
- Is the protagonist **dimensional** — built on a real contradiction that the story pays off?
- Is the **antagonism strong enough**? Could you strengthen the opposing forces until the desired thing is genuinely hard-won? (This is the most common fixable weakness.)
- Does each supporting character **illuminate or pressure** the protagonist, or are some inert?
- Which **levels of conflict** (inner / personal / extra-personal) are engaged, and which are missing?

### Level 4 — Scene
- Does **every scene turn** a value from one charge to its opposite? Mark the scenes that don't.
- Does each significant scene contain a real **gap** — an action that meets a stronger or contradictory reaction than expected?
- Do scenes **enter late and leave early**, or are they padded with arrival and aftermath?
- Is the dialogue **on-the-nose**, or does it carry **subtext**?
- Is **exposition dramatized as ammunition**, or dumped in lumps?

### Level 5 — Prose & line (last)
- Is description **rendered** (concrete, sensory, specific) or **reported** (abstract, told)?
- Is the **point of view** chosen to maximize tension, and held consistently?
- Is there **decoration that neither turns a value nor reveals character** — and can it be cut?
- Only now: rhythm, word choice, clarity, grammar.

## Common root causes and their fixes

| Symptom the writer reports | Likely root cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "It feels flat / nothing's at stake" | Scenes don't turn; no value change | Make each scene flip a charge; name the value |
| "The middle drags" | Repetition instead of escalation | Reorder so each obstacle outweighs the last |
| "It feels low-stakes / too easy" | Weak antagonism | Build the negative pole to its strongest form |
| "The character is boring" | Characterization without revealed true character | Force choices under pressure; add a real contradiction |
| "The ending feels cheap / unearned" | Climax resolved by coincidence or rescue | Make the climax flow from the protagonist's own choice |
| "The dialogue feels stiff / fake" | On-the-nose; no subtext | Push real meaning beneath the surface words |
| "It starts slow" | Inciting incident too late; too much front-loaded setup | Move the inciting incident earlier; enter scenes later |
| "It wanders" | Scenes off the spine | Cut or reroute scenes that don't serve the central quest |
| "It feels preachy" | Theme stated, counter-idea ignored | Dramatize the opposing view with real force; remove stated theme |

## Revision order

When the diagnosis is done, revise in roughly this sequence so you don't polish what you'll later cut:

1. Fix premise/meaning and spine (you may need to rebuild the central question).
2. Fix structure — inciting incident, escalation, crisis, climax causality.
3. Fix character — antagonism, active protagonist, dimension, missing conflict levels.
4. Fix scenes — turns, gaps, subtext, exposition, enter-late/leave-early.
5. *Then* polish prose and line.

Remind the writer that the goal is never "now it follows the rules." The rules are scaffolding. The goal is a story that makes a reader feel something true — and sometimes the right move is to break a rule on purpose, knowingly, for effect. The craft is in knowing which rule, and why.
