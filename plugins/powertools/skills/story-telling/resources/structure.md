# Structure

Structure is the **selection and arrangement of events** to arouse specific emotion and express a view of life. Out of the thousands of things that could happen to a character, the storyteller chooses a few and orders them so that each builds on the last toward a meaningful end. Structure is choice, not formula.

## The five-part shape

Most stories, however they vary on the surface, move through five functional stages. These are not arbitrary "acts" — each does a job the others can't.

1. **Inciting Incident.** The event that knocks the protagonist's life out of balance and arouses a conscious desire to restore it. It must be *causal*, not trivial, and it must matter enough to launch a whole story. Place it early — a long delay before the story actually starts is a common reason openings drag. It can arrive by coincidence, but from here on, coincidence should fade and causality take over.

2. **Progressive Complications.** The long middle: the protagonist pursues the object of desire and meets escalating resistance. The key word is *progressive* — each obstacle must demand more than the last, force riskier action, and narrow the protagonist's options. Stakes rise; retreat becomes harder; the gap between expectation and result widens each time. A sagging middle is almost always a middle that *repeats* difficulty at the same level instead of *escalating* it. (See "Diagnosing a sagging middle" below.)

3. **Crisis.** The obligatory moment of ultimate decision. The protagonist, having exhausted lesser options, faces the hardest choice of the story — between two incompatible goods, or the lesser of two evils. The crisis is where true character is laid bare, because the choice is genuinely costly either way. The whole middle exists to drive the protagonist to *this* dilemma. If the late-story "hard choice" is actually easy, the structure has failed regardless of polish.

4. **Climax.** The action that flows from the crisis decision and delivers the story's irreversible change. This is the scene the whole story was written to produce — the moment the central value settles into its final charge. It must be *caused* by the protagonist's choice, not by luck or rescue. Everything earlier is preparation for this.

5. **Resolution.** Everything after the climax: the new balance, the consequences, the breath the reader needs to absorb what changed. Keep it brief once the central question is answered — overlong resolutions bleed energy.

## The spine (the through-line)

The **spine** is the protagonist's deep quest to restore the balance that the inciting incident destroyed — the single driving desire that runs unbroken from inciting incident to climax. Every major scene should connect to it. When a story feels like it's "wandering," scenes have usually drifted off the spine. State the spine as one sentence ("to win back the family he abandoned," "to expose the truth whatever it costs her") and test each scene against it.

Note the difference between the **object of desire** (the conscious, stated want — the job, the person, the verdict) and the deeper need the character may not even see. The richest stories open a gap between what the protagonist *wants* and what they truly *need*, and the climax often grants one at the cost of the other.

## Act design

Acts are movements that end on major reversals — the big turns that change the direction of the whole story, not just a scene. A two-hour film classically runs three acts; a novel may run more. What matters is not the count but that:

- Each act ends on a turning point of greater magnitude than the scene-level turns within it.
- The **last act is the shortest and most intense**, accelerating into the climax.
- The act climaxes escalate against each other — turn two outweighs turn one, the final outweighs them all.

Don't impose a fixed act count. Derive the movements from where the story's biggest reversals naturally fall, then strengthen them.

## The structure spectrum

Classical, tightly-causal design is one option, not the only valid one. Think of a spectrum:

- **Classical / closed design.** Single active protagonist, clear desire, external and consistent causality, conflict driven largely by forces outside the self, a closed ending that answers the central question. The default of mainstream film and most commercial fiction. Maximally satisfying; risks formula if hollow.

- **Minimalist design.** Pares the classical elements down: a more passive or internal protagonist, multiple protagonists, quieter conflict (often inner or personal rather than epic), an **open ending** that leaves the central question deliberately unresolved for the reader to complete. The register of much literary fiction. Demands more from the reader; rewards with resonance.

- **Anti-structure.** Actively breaks classical expectations — coincidence over causality, non-linear or fractured time, inconsistent realities, irony as the dominant mode. Powerful in the right hands; collapses into noise without rigorous control underneath.

Match the design to the writer's intent. A writer aiming for a haunting, ambiguous literary piece should not be pushed toward a neat closed climax; a writer aiming for a propulsive thriller should not be encouraged toward open ambiguity. Diagnose what they're *trying* to do, then strengthen the structure appropriate to it.

## Ending: closed vs. open

- A **closed ending** answers the dramatic question and settles the central value. Use when the writer wants catharsis and clarity.
- An **open ending** leaves the answer to the reader — but only earns its ambiguity if the story has supplied enough for the reader to complete it meaningfully. Open is not the same as unfinished. The test: could a thoughtful reader argue a defensible answer from what's on the page? If not, it's not open — it's incomplete.

## Diagnosing a sagging middle

When the middle drags, check, in order:

1. **Is it escalating or repeating?** Are the obstacles genuinely harder each time, or just *more* of the same difficulty? Repetition at one level is the number-one cause. Reorder and intensify so each turn outweighs the last.
2. **Is the desire still urgent?** If the protagonist could walk away with no real loss, urgency has leaked out. Re-tighten the stakes or raise the cost of quitting.
3. **Are scenes turning?** A middle made of flat, non-turning scenes will drag even if events are "interesting." Make each scene flip a value (see `scene-craft.md`).
4. **Is everything on the spine?** Cut or reroute scenes that don't serve the central quest.
5. **Is the antagonism strong enough?** Weak resistance makes the middle feel weightless. Strengthen the opposing forces (see `character.md` on antagonism).
