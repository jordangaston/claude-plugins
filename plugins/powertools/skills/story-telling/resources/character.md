# Character

Readers don't fall for plots; they fall for people. Plot exists to put characters under the pressure that reveals who they are. Get character right and the reader will follow you anywhere.

## Characterization vs. true character

- **Characterization** is the sum of observable traits: age, looks, speech, job, manner, the car they drive, the way they laugh. It's the *mask* — everything an observer could catalogue.
- **True character** is what's revealed by the **choices a person makes under pressure** — especially when the stakes are high and the choice is hard. The deeper the pressure, the deeper the truth exposed.

The gap between the two is where character work lives. A man *characterized* as gentle who, cornered, chooses brutal self-preservation has just had his true character revealed. The revelation — mask vs. truth, or mask confirmed at great cost — is one of the most powerful tools in storytelling. A character whose true character merely matches their characterization (the nice guy who is, under pressure, nice) is flat. Build the contradiction.

## Dimension is contradiction

A "dimensional" character is one built on **consistent contradiction** — a true contradiction within the characterization, or between the mask and the truth. The compassionate killer. The cynic who secretly hopes. The devoted parent capable of cruelty. These contradictions must be *consistent* (rooted, not random) and they must eventually be *paid off* by the story. Dimensions are not quirks; a limp and a stutter are characterization, not dimension. Dimension is an axis of genuine inner tension that pressure can pull on.

Minor characters need fewer dimensions; the protagonist should be the most dimensional figure in the story, because they'll be put under the most pressure.

## The protagonist

A protagonist is usually:

- **Willful.** Possessed of a conscious desire strong enough to drive the story, and ideally a deeper unconscious need in tension with it.
- **Capable of pursuit.** Able to convincingly chase the object of desire against real resistance.
- **At the center of the spine.** The story's central quest is theirs.

The protagonist need not be likable, but they must be **compelling** — the reader must want to follow them. Empathy ("this person is recognizably human; I understand their want") matters more than sympathy ("I approve of them"). We follow villains, addicts, and monsters when we recognize ourselves in their wanting.

**Active vs. passive:** the strongest protagonists *drive* — they choose and act, and events flow from their choices. A passive protagonist, to whom things merely happen, drains a story of momentum. Internal/literary stories can sustain a more passive protagonist by relocating the drive *inward* (the conflict is within), but the reader still needs the felt sense of a will straining against something.

## Character arc

An **arc** is the transformation a character undergoes across the story — the change in their true character, worldview, or moral center, driven by the escalating pressure of the plot. The arc and the plot are not two things; the plot *is* the pressure that forces the arc.

- A **change arc**: the character is fundamentally different at the climax (the coward who becomes brave, the liar who chooses truth at a cost).
- A **steadfast arc**: the character holds firm while the world tries to break them, and proves who they are by *not* changing (the incorruptible figure who pays the price for integrity).
- A **fall**: the character changes for the worse, often by getting what they wanted.

Not every character must arc, and not every story is about change — but the protagonist's relationship to change should be *intentional*, never accidental. A character who is the same at the end as the beginning for no reason signals a story that didn't put them under enough pressure.

## The three levels of conflict

Resistance to desire comes from three arenas. The richest stories braid all three:

1. **Inner conflict** — within the self: the protagonist's own fears, contradictions, divided wants, conscience, doubt.
2. **Personal conflict** — within intimate relationships: family, lovers, friends, colleagues.
3. **Extra-personal conflict** — with larger forces: society and its institutions, the physical world, time, fate, the antagonist's power structure.

A common weakness is a story that lives at only one level — all external action and no inner life (hollow spectacle), or all interiority and no external pressure (static navel-gazing). Diagnose which levels a draft is using and push to engage the missing ones. The most resonant climaxes force a collision *across* levels — an external choice that is simultaneously a personal betrayal and an inner reckoning.

## The principle of antagonism

**A protagonist and their story can only be as compelling as the forces of antagonism make them.** This is one of the most actionable laws in all of story craft. A hero who overcomes weak opposition has proven nothing; a hero who overcomes the strongest conceivable opposition becomes legendary.

So: **build the negative side first and hardest.** Whatever value the protagonist pursues, construct the fullest, most credible, most powerful version of its opposite. If the story is about justice, the forces of injustice must be formidable, intelligent, and genuinely threatening — not a strawman. If the protagonist seeks love, the obstacles to love must be real and weighty.

The "negation of the negation" (covered in `meaning.md`) is the deepest expression of this: push the negative pole past mere absence into its most twisted, self-justifying, powerful form. The villain who believes they're the hero. The injustice dressed as law. That's where the strongest antagonism lives.

When a draft feels low-stakes or unearned, the antagonism is almost always too weak. Strengthen what opposes the protagonist before anything else.

## The supporting cast

Every secondary character exists to **illuminate the protagonist** by contrast or pressure. Design them to reflect different facets of the central conflict — to embody the road not taken, the tempting shortcut, the cost of the choice, the mirror that shows the protagonist who they're becoming. A supporting character who doesn't pressure or illuminate the protagonist is a candidate for cutting or merging.
