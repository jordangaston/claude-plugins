# Scene Craft

The scene is the basic unit of storytelling. Structure happens at the macro level, but a story is *experienced* scene by scene. Get scenes right and most other problems dissolve.

## A scene is a turn

**A scene is an action through conflict that turns the value charge of the character's situation.** Open on one charge, close on the opposite:

- positive → negative (the date that starts hopeful and ends in humiliation)
- negative → positive (the interrogation the suspect walks into trapped and walks out of free)
- or a shift in degree along the same value (safe → mortally threatened)

If a scene ends on the same charge it began, **no event has occurred.** It may contain lovely writing, exposition, or "character development," but structurally it's inert. The fix is almost never better prose — it's giving the scene a genuine turn, or cutting it and folding its essential content into a scene that turns.

This is the single most useful diagnostic in this whole skill. When a writer says "this scene feels flat / pointless / draggy," ask: *what value flips, and where exactly is the turn?* Usually the answer is "nothing" — and that's the problem.

## Beats: the inside of a scene

A **beat** is an exchange of action and reaction — one behavior met by another. Scenes are built of beats. The beats escalate: each action provokes a reaction that shifts the scene a little, until one beat tips the whole scene over into its opposite charge. That tipping beat is the **turning point** of the scene.

To build a scene:
1. Know the value at stake and which way it will turn.
2. Know what the character *wants in this scene* (the scene-objective) and what opposes it.
3. Write the beats as a rising progression of action/reaction, not idle chat.
4. Find the turn — the beat where the gap opens and the charge flips.
5. Get out. Leave the scene the moment after it turns.

## The gap, at scene level

Inside a scene, the **gap** is the moment a character acts expecting one response and gets a stronger or contradictory one. That gap is where the scene comes alive and where the reader leans in. A scene with no gap — where every action gets exactly its expected reaction — is a scene where nothing is at risk. Engineer at least one real gap into every significant scene.

## Enter late, leave early

Begin each scene as close to its turning point as the scene can bear, and exit the instant the turn lands. Most flat scenes are padded with arrival, small talk, and settling-in before anything happens, then linger after the point is made. Cut the front and the tail. The reader will happily fill in the connective tissue.

## Text and subtext

**Text** is what is said and done on the surface. **Subtext** is the true thought and feeling running underneath. In life, people almost never say exactly what they mean — they're managing, hiding, performing, hoping. The same is true of good dialogue.

**On-the-nose dialogue** — where characters state their feelings and intentions plainly ("I'm angry at you because you abandoned me and now I can't trust anyone") — is the mark of an inexperienced draft. It kills tension because there's nothing for the reader to read *into*. The fix:

- Push the real meaning beneath the surface. Let characters talk about the small thing while the big thing pulses underneath.
- Make characters *want* something in the conversation and pursue it indirectly.
- Trust the reader to feel the gap between what's said and what's meant.

A useful test: if you could play a scene with the characters thinking the *opposite* of what they're saying and it would still make sense, you have subtext. If they're just announcing their interiors, you don't.

## Exposition as ammunition

Exposition is the information the reader needs — backstory, world facts, relationships. The amateur move is to deliver it in lumps ("As you know, Bob, ever since the war...") or in inert description. The craft move is to **convert exposition into ammunition**: information that characters *use against each other* inside conflict, revealed only at the moment it costs or wounds or wins something.

- Dramatize, don't summarize. Let the reader infer the past from present behavior.
- Reveal facts through conflict, at the worst possible moment for someone.
- Pace it — release information on a need-to-know basis to create curiosity, not all at once.
- The best exposition lands without the reader noticing it was exposition.

## Description and prose (in fiction)

In prose narrative, the same scene principles apply, plus:

- **Render, don't report.** Put the reader inside the experience through concrete, specific, sensory detail rather than telling them how to feel.
- **Specificity is credibility.** "A dog" is generic; "the three-legged shepherd that growled at the mailman" is a world. Specific detail makes a fictional thing feel real.
- **Point of view is a choice with consequences.** Whose head are we in, how close, and how much do they know? Pick the POV that maximizes the gap between what the viewpoint character understands and what's actually happening.
- **Cut what doesn't turn or reveal.** Beautiful prose that neither advances the value nor reveals character is decoration. Decoration in moderation is fine; decoration as structure is fatal.
