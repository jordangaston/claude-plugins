# Chapter 2: Building Your Sales Narrative

The narrative is the foundation of everything. Before slides, emails, demos, or outreach — you need the story. All sales collateral is simply the narrative expressed in a different medium.

**The narrative format**: Problem-Solution-Specifics. Inductive structure: each element builds on the last, so if a prospect disagrees early, you can stop rather than waste time on irrelevant sections.

---

## The 7 Questions

### 1. What is the Problem?

Articulate the business pain as crisply as possible so the prospect can immediately evaluate whether it applies to them.

**Test**: Describe the problem to someone in the industry. If they say "yes" and can continue the conversation about it, you've got it. If they look confused, keep refining.

**Structure**:
- Lead with the functional pain, not a solution
- Make it vivid enough that the prospect recognizes their own experience
- One focused problem (nuances come later in more advanced conversations)

**Examples from the book:**
- TalentBin: "Technical recruiting is really hard. Finding engineering talent with the right skills is tough, and even when you find them, engaging with them and staying on top of all those conversations can be a massive time sink."
- Salesforce: "B2B sales is tough. You have to manage dozens of concurrent conversations, follow up at the right time, and not drop any balls."
- Groupon: "It's hard for small local businesses to acquire new customers. With all the time you spend running day-to-day operations, who has time to figure out how to drive new business through the door?"

### 2. Who Has the Problem?

Identify the specific functional role whose *job it is* to solve this problem. Not the person most impacted — the person responsible for fixing it.

**Rule of thumb**: The person who controls the budget or labor resources allocated to this pain. If they can say "well, that's not really my job," you have the wrong person.

**Scaling consideration**: As organizations grow, the role becomes more specialized. A small company has a CEO focused on sales performance. A mid-size company has a Sales Director. A large company has a dedicated Sales Operations Manager. Know which title you're targeting at which company size.

**Why this matters**: It determines your ICP, your prospecting targeting, and who should be in the room during a pitch.

### 3. What Are the Costs?

Quantify the pain. This is your ROI argument and the reason the prospect should spend money.

**Hard ROI** (quantifiable cost reductions):
- Headcount costs saved
- Cost per unit of something (cost per hire, cost per support ticket resolved)
- Direct cost of an alternative solution they're currently paying for

**Soft ROI** (opportunity costs — harder to prove but often larger):
- Revenue missed because of inefficiency
- Deals that didn't close because of dropped follow-ups
- Customers churned because of poor support
- Time cost of manual processes (labor hours × hourly rate)

**Guidance**: Understand the unit economics of the problem. For support software: cost of a support agent × number of tickets per agent. For CRM: cost of a dropped deal × number of deals dropped per quarter. This becomes your "without us, you're leaving $X on the table."

### 4. How is it Currently Solved? Why Does That Fail?

Know the full competitive landscape — not just direct competitors but all alternatives including doing nothing.

**The four solution categories:**

**No solution**: The prospect doesn't currently address the problem at all. Your job is to convince them it's worth solving. The cost of non-solution (from question 3) is your primary argument.

**Solution via process**: They've built a manual workaround — spreadsheets, manual data entry, CC'ing the manager on every email. Understand the specific process. Your argument: the time cost, the fragility, the error rate.

**Solution via service provider**: They outsource it — recruiting agencies, PR firms, contractors. Your argument: the cost premium of the service provider margin AND the loss of control/quality/speed.

**Solution via product**: They use an existing product (competitive). Know competitors' specific weaknesses relative to your strengths. Also know their strengths — a credible narrative acknowledges real trade-offs.

**Key insight**: Knowing the competitive strengths matters as much as knowing weaknesses. Credibility requires that you honestly acknowledge what alternatives do well. This makes your "but here's why our approach is better" much more persuasive.

### 5. What Has Changed That Enables a New Solution?

Something in the world has changed — technologically, behaviorally, or economically — that makes your approach possible now.

This is the "why now" of your narrative. Without it, the prospect asks: "If this problem has existed, why hasn't it been solved before?"

**Examples:**
- Salesforce: Rise of always-connected browser technology made web-delivered CRM practical
- TalentBin: Explosion of professional activity on GitHub, Stack Overflow, Twitter created discoverable passive talent data
- Groupon: Email list scale + local merchant desperation post-2008 created a two-sided market opportunity
- Mobile CRM tools: Smartphone adoption means reps are rarely at a desk — mobile-first becomes necessary

**Two types of change:**
- Change that *enables* a new solution (new technology opens a door)
- Change that *demands* a new solution (new market condition creates urgent need)

Either type is valid. Know which one applies to you.

### 6. How Does the New Solution Work?

Explain the mechanism of your solution clearly enough that the prospect understands *why* it works, not just *that* it works.

**Tips:**
- Compare to something familiar: "It's like [known thing], but [key difference]"
- Focus on the mechanism that addresses the change you identified in question 5
- Level of detail should match the audience — operational users want feature specifics; executives want outcome logic

**Examples:**
- Salesforce: "It's like your traditional CRM, but delivered through a browser — accessible anywhere, always updated, no IT required."
- TalentBin: "We aggregate the professional activity people leave on GitHub, Stack Overflow, and Twitter into profiles with skill signals and personal email addresses. Recruiters search these profiles and reach out directly."

### 7. How Do You Know It's Better?

Prove your superiority with the same metrics the prospect uses to evaluate the problem space.

**Quantitative proof** (strongest):
- Direct metric comparisons: "Our customers see 5x the search results vs. LinkedIn Recruiter"
- Outcome metrics: "Reps using our CRM update their pipeline 3-10x more frequently"
- ROI case studies: "Customer X reduced cost per hire from $37,500 (agency) to $8,000 (us)"

**Qualitative proof** (supplements quantitative):
- Customer testimonials (ideally featuring specific outcomes)
- Named case studies
- Press and analyst coverage
- Awards

**Third-party validation**: logos, customer counts, analyst placements. These say "and others agree."

**Competitive reframing**: When competitors cite metrics that don't matter for your use case, redirect. "200 million profiles sounds impressive, but if you're hiring iOS developers in Denver, what matters is how many of those 200 million are iOS developers in Denver — and ours will surface 3x as many."

## Pricing

**Charge from day one.** Free users don't validate PMF. Customers who don't pay don't use the product seriously and don't give useful feedback.

**Start low, raise over time.** Early pricing is a hypothesis. Bias toward giving away more value than you capture initially to maximize learning and customer acquisition. Raise prices as you learn what the market will bear.

**Two approaches to initial pricing:**

1. **Existing solutions comparison**: Find the most comparable solutions and price relative to them. Match the pricing model customers are used to (per-seat, per-usage, etc.) to minimize friction.

2. **ROI/value pricing**: Determine the value you provide, and price to capture a portion of it. If your product saves the customer $200,000 and you charge $20,000, that's a 10x ROI — compelling and defensible.

**Avoid**:
- Pricing to perfection (too high → bad win rates, churn if value isn't delivered)
- Premature segmentation (3 tiers early on = complexity without benefit)
- Free (no urgency, no feedback, no validation)

**Price thresholding**: Be aware of the organizational purchasing thresholds your price point crosses — individual expense, team budget, departmental discretionary, procurement/legal. Slipping under a threshold can dramatically accelerate the sale.

## Assembling the Narrative

Once you have answered all 7 questions, assemble them into a coherent flow. Test it as an elevator pitch: 3 minutes, to someone with industry familiarity, covering all 7 elements.

**The narrative template:**

```
FOUNDING SALES NARRATIVE: [Company Name]
════════════════════════════════════════

THE PROBLEM
  Pain:          [Crisp problem statement]
  Who has it:    [Functional role]
  The cost:      [Hard ROI / Soft ROI]

STATUS QUO
  Current paths: [No solution / Process / Service / Product]
  Why they fail: [Specific failure modes]

THE CHANGE
  What shifted:  [Technology or market change]

YOUR SOLUTION
  Mechanism:     [How it works, in plain language]
  The proof:     [Key metrics + qualitative evidence]

PRICING
  Model:         [Per seat / per usage / flat / etc.]
  Price:         [$X / unit / year]
  ROI argument:  [Value delivered vs. cost]
```

**The narrative is a living document.** Every customer conversation will add nuance, surface new objections, and sharpen your proof points. Treat it as a product that ships continuously, not a document that gets finished.
