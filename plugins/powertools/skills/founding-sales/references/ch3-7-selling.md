# Chapters 3–7: Materials, Prospecting, Outreach & Pitching

---

## Chapter 3: Sales Materials

### The Core Principle: Speed Over Polish

At early stage, a rough asset that exists is worth ten polished assets that don't. Shorter feedback loops between customer objections and updated collateral beat perfection every time. Ship it.

### The Essential Kit

**1. Sales Deck**
Structure each slide to correspond to a narrative step:
1. Problem
2. Who has the problem
3. Cost of the problem
4. How it's currently solved (and why that fails)
5. What has changed
6. How the new solution works (demo setup slide)
7. Proof (metrics, logos, testimonials)
8. Pricing

Start with one bullet-point slide per section. Evolve from there. The deck is a work in progress — always. Never say it's "done."

**2. Outreach Email Templates**
- Subject line: Short, specific, curiosity-creating. Reference something about their specific situation if possible.
- Body: 3-5 sentences max. Problem → your role in solving it → one specific CTA (book a 20-minute demo).
- Personalization: At least one sentence specific to them (their role, their company, a relevant signal).
- Do not send walls of text.

**3. Phone Scripts**
- Cold call opening (15 seconds max): "Hi [name], I'm [you] from [company]. We help [role] solve [pain]. Do you have 2 minutes?"
- Voicemail: 20-30 seconds. State problem, state your solution in one sentence, leave callback number.
- Gate-keeper handling: Be direct about why you're calling. Don't try to trick them.

**4. Demo Script**
Structure the demo around the narrative, not the feature list:
1. Set context: "Before I dive in, let me confirm I understand your situation..." (confirm their pain)
2. Narrate what you're doing and why: "What I'm showing you here solves [specific pain point] by..."
3. Don't show everything — show the features that address their confirmed pain points
4. Pause and check in at each section: "Does this reflect the challenge you're seeing?"
5. End with next steps, not a feature dump

**5. Video Content**
- Rough screencasts of key features beat production-quality explainers at early stage
- 10 five-minute feature walkthroughs on YouTube > 1 polished overview video
- Record with your laptop mic. Ship it now.

## Chapter 4: Early Prospecting

### Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

The ICP defines which companies are most likely to be qualified buyers. Narrow it aggressively — spreading too wide means wasted conversations.

**ICP dimensions:**
- **Industry/vertical**: Which industries have this pain most acutely?
- **Company size**: Revenue, employee count, or department size (correlates with budget and problem intensity)
- **Organizational signals**: Do they have a specific role in-house? Are they growing? Did they recently hire for a relevant function?
- **Technology signals**: What tools do they use that indicate maturity or budget?
- **Geographic signals** (if relevant)
- **Pain signal**: What external indicators suggest they have your problem right now? (Job postings, news articles, funding rounds, etc.)

**The ICP test**: Does an account without these characteristics almost never buy? If so, add it to the ICP. Does having it always predict a qualified prospect? If so, it's a strong signal.

### Account Sourcing

Start manual. Use every tool available:
- **LinkedIn Sales Navigator**: Filter by title, company size, industry, geography
- **Job postings**: A company hiring a VP of Sales is actively growing their sales team — they have sales tooling budget
- **Intent data platforms** (Bombora, G2 Buyer Intent): Signals of active research
- **Industry associations, conference attendee lists**: People who show up to industry events are invested in their domain
- **Your own network**: Who do you know in the target persona?
- **Competitor customers**: If they use a competitor, they've validated the pain and budget

Build lists in a spreadsheet or directly in your CRM. Enrich with contact information (name, title, direct email, phone).

### Point-of-Contact Discovery

Find the person whose *job it is* to solve the problem — not just the most senior person.

**Signals of the right person:**
- Their title indicates functional responsibility for the pain area
- They control budget for solutions to this pain
- They spend meaningful labor hours on this problem

**In small orgs**: Often the CEO or founder wears this hat.
**In mid-size orgs**: A functional director or manager.
**In large orgs**: A specialized operations or enablement role.

When in doubt, look for the person who would be fired if the problem gets worse.

## Chapter 5: Prospect Outreach & Demo Appointment Setting

### The Sales Cycle Stages

1. **Prospect** → identified in ICP list
2. **Contacted** → outreach initiated
3. **Demo scheduled** → appointment on calendar
4. **Demo completed** → pitch delivered
5. **Proposal sent** → pricing shared
6. **Negotiating** → in active closing conversation
7. **Closed won** or **Closed lost**

### Cadence: Multi-Touch, Multi-Channel

No single touch converts at scale. Build a sequence:
- **Week 1**: Email #1 (personalized) → Day 2: Call + voicemail → Day 4: Email #2 (follow-up)
- **Week 2**: Email #3 (different angle/proof point) → Day 2: Call + voicemail
- **Week 3**: Email #4 (breakup email — "I'll stop reaching out after this") → Day 2: Final call

6-8 total touches over 2-3 weeks is standard. Then recycle into a nurture list for later.

### Email Best Practices

**Subject lines that work:**
- "[Shared connection] suggested I reach out"
- "Question about [specific thing at their company]"
- "[Their company] + [your company]"
- Direct: "[Your product] for [their role] at [their company]"

**Body structure:**
1. One sentence of credibility/context
2. The problem, specifically framed for their role
3. What you do about it, in one sentence
4. Social proof in one sentence (X customers, specific metric)
5. One specific ask: "Would a 20-minute call this week make sense?"

**What to avoid:**
- Long paragraphs
- Feature lists
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- Attaching a deck in the first email (it signals mass outreach)

### Cold Calling

Most calls go to voicemail. Plan for it. Leave punchy voicemails that match your email sequence.

When you get someone live:
1. Identify yourself immediately
2. State why you're calling in 15 seconds
3. Get permission to continue: "Do you have 2 minutes?"
4. If yes: state the problem and ask if it resonates
5. If no: ask for a better time

Rejection on a cold call is not rejection of you or your product — it's a timing mismatch. Record the call, move on.

### Referral Prospecting

Warm introductions convert at 5-10x the rate of cold outreach. Build a systematic ask:
- After every won deal, ask: "Who else do you know who has this problem?"
- After every lost deal (especially good-fit ones), ask: "Even though the timing wasn't right, is there someone in your network I should talk to?"
- Ask your investors, advisors, and board — they have networks you don't

Track referral sources in your CRM. Optimize the channel that converts best.

### Inbound Lead Response

Speed is the competitive advantage in inbound. Studies consistently show conversion rates drop dramatically beyond 5 minutes of response delay.

**Process:**
1. New form fill → auto-trigger acknowledgment email + alert to you
2. Call within 5 minutes if possible
3. If no answer, leave voicemail + send email simultaneously
4. Attempt 3-4 times in first 48 hours before dropping to standard cadence

## Chapter 7: Pitching — Preparation, Presentation & Objections

### The Goal of Pitching

Not to "present your product." The goal is to:
1. Confirm the prospect has the pain you solve
2. Demonstrate that your solution addresses their specific pain
3. Establish credibility as an expert who can help
4. Move to a concrete next step (proposal, trial, contract)

### New-Technology Sales Persuasion Formula

This is the structured arc of a winning pitch:
1. **Establish the problem** — get explicit agreement it exists
2. **Establish the costs** — make the pain vivid and quantified for their context
3. **Establish current solution failure** — show why status quo is insufficient
4. **What has changed** — why a new solution is now possible
5. **Demo** — show how the solution works against their specific pain
6. **Prove it** — metrics, testimonials, case studies relevant to their situation
7. **Pricing** — present with confidence, not apology
8. **Ask for the sale** — explicitly. "Does this make sense to move forward?"

### Pre-Call Planning

10-15 minutes before every call:
- Review CRM notes from any prior interactions
- Check LinkedIn: role, tenure, background, any shared connections
- Check company news: recent funding, hires, press
- Identify the 1-2 things you want to confirm/learn in this call
- Know which objections are most likely for this persona and have your responses ready

### Pitch Introduction

Open by setting context and getting permission:
- "To make sure this is a good use of your time, let me share what we do in 2 minutes, and then I'd love to hear more about what you're working on. Sound good?"
- Deliver the problem + who has it + cost summary → get their reaction before going further

### Running the Demo

The demo serves the narrative — don't show everything. Show what solves their confirmed pain.

**Structure:**
1. Transition from narrative: "Let me show you how we address exactly that..."
2. Narrate what you're demonstrating and WHY it matters for their situation
3. Check in at each section: "Does this match what you're dealing with?"
4. Reserve a few minutes to surface and address questions as you go
5. End with a summary: "So in summary, we've shown you [X, Y, Z] — does this address what you were looking for?"

### Handling Objections

Every objection is information. The stated objection is often not the real one.

**The objection-handling framework:**
1. **Acknowledge** — "That's a fair concern."
2. **Clarify** — "Can you help me understand more about what's driving that?" (get to the root)
3. **Respond** — Address the actual concern with evidence or reframing
4. **Confirm** — "Does that address your concern, or is there something else there?"

**Common objections and approaches:**

| Objection | Often means | Response angle |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| "Too expensive" | ROI isn't clear, or not the decision-maker | Quantify the value; identify if budget holder is in the room |
| "Not the right time" | Low priority or no budget cycle | Find out when "the right time" is; set a specific revisit date |
| "We already use [X]" | Satisfied with status quo or switching cost concern | Probe on specific pain points with current solution; compare on the metrics that matter |
| "I need to check with my team" | Not the decision-maker, or not convinced enough to champion | Identify who else is involved; offer to present to the broader group |
| "Send me more information" | Polite brush-off or genuine interest in async review | Send a targeted follow-up (not a generic deck); book a specific follow-up call |
| "We don't have budget" | Budget not approved, wrong time in cycle | Understand budget cycle; reframe to ROI that justifies creating budget |

**The objection audit**: Track every objection you hear. After 20 pitches, you'll see patterns. Build permanent responses for the top 5-10. Practice them until they're fluent.

### After the Demo

Always end every pitch with a concrete next step agreed on the call:
- "Let's get a call scheduled with [decision-maker] for next Thursday."
- "I'll send the proposal by EOD today — can we connect Friday to review?"
- "Let's set up the trial agreement — what does your procurement process look like?"

Never let a call end with "I'll think about it" as the only outcome. If they're not ready, find out what they need to become ready, and set a specific date to revisit.

**Demo follow-up:**
- Send a follow-up email within 2 hours of the call
- Recap: what you discussed, what they said their pain was, what you showed them, and the agreed next step
- Attach any relevant proof points or materials they asked for
- Confirm the next meeting time

**Close lost gracefully**: When you lose a deal, send a note thanking them for their time and asking if they'd be willing to share what drove the decision. This feedback is gold. It also keeps the door open for a future conversation.
