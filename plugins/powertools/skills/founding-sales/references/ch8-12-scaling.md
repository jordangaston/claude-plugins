## Chapter 8: Down-Funnel Selling — Negotiation, Closing & Pipeline Management

### Negotiation

When you reach pricing conversations, know your walk-away point in advance. Price is not the only lever.

**Negotiation levers beyond price:**
- **Payment timing**: Annual upfront vs. monthly vs. quarterly changes cash flow for both sides
- **Contract length**: Longer term often justifies a discount; shorter term reduces their risk
- **Seats/usage**: Adjust scope rather than per-unit price
- **Implementation support**: Offer more onboarding in lieu of discounting
- **Expansion commitments**: Agree on pricing for future seats/modules now

**Principles:**
- Give concessions on terms before giving concessions on price
- Every concession you give should come with something in return (timeline acceleration, expanded scope, referrals)
- Know your pricing floor. Discount with conviction — not apology, not anxiety
- Document all negotiated terms clearly. Verbal agreements are not agreements.

### Closing

The close must be explicit. Prospects do not close themselves.

**Types of closes:**
- **Direct**: "Ready to move forward? I can get the contract over today."
- **Assumptive**: "I'll have the agreement over by EOD — who should I address it to?"
- **Alternative**: "Do you want to start with a 6-month or 12-month agreement?"
- **Summary**: "Based on what we've discussed, this solves [X] for [Y], and at [price], you're getting [Z ROI]. Does it make sense to move forward?"

**Closing blockers**: If a prospect isn't closing, something is unresolved. Common blockers:
- The economic buyer isn't in the room (find out who approves budget and get a meeting)
- Legal/procurement is the delay (get on a call with procurement to understand the process)
- Internal champion isn't sold (offer to present again to the team)
- Product concern (offer a trial or POC with specific success criteria)

### Close Won — What to Capture

When you win, document:
- What specific pain drove the purchase
- Why your solution won over alternatives
- What objections were raised and how they were resolved
- Who the key stakeholders were in the decision
- The contract terms

This is your replicable playbook. Run a win review with your team for every significant deal.

### Close Lost — What to Capture

When you lose, document honestly:
- Loss reason (timing, price, product gap, competition, champion left, no decision)
- Who won (if a competitor)
- What would have changed the outcome
- Whether and when to re-engage

Loss reviews should be as rigorous as win reviews. Pattern-matching across loss reasons reveals the most impactful product or positioning improvements.

### Pipeline Management

**The core principle**: Every opportunity must have a next action and a next action date. An opportunity with neither is not a real opportunity — it's wishful thinking clogging your CRM.

**Pipeline stages and exit criteria** (define these for your sales motion):
- Each stage should have a clear criterion that defines when a deal moves in and out
- Examples: "Demo Scheduled" = meeting on calendar. "Demo Completed" = meeting happened, confirmed pain exists. "Proposal Sent" = pricing document delivered.

**Pipeline math**: To hit a revenue target, work backwards:
- Quota ÷ Average Contract Value = number of deals needed
- Deals needed ÷ Win rate = qualified opportunities needed in pipeline
- Qualified opportunities ÷ Demo-to-qualified rate = demos needed
- Demos needed ÷ Outreach-to-demo rate = outreach contacts needed

If your pipeline doesn't cover 3-4x quota in qualified opportunities, you have an activity problem.

**Weekly pipeline review questions:**
- Which deals have moved forward this week? Why?
- Which deals have gone stale? What's the plan?
- Which deals are at risk? What's the intervention?
- Are we on track for the month/quarter? What do we need to accelerate?

## Chapter 9: Customer Success — Implementation, Ongoing Success & Renewals

### Why Customer Success Matters

Revenue from retained customers is 5-10x cheaper to earn than revenue from new customers. Churn is the enemy of compounding growth. Every customer you lose sets you back further than it appears — because the cost of winning them is sunk.

**The formula**: Net Revenue Retention > 100% means your existing customers are growing — expansion + upsell offsets churn. This is the engine of durable SaaS businesses.

### Implementation

The first 30-90 days determine whether a customer becomes a long-term partner or churns quietly.

**Keys to successful implementation:**
- Define "success" explicitly with the customer before you start — what metric proves value was delivered?
- Set a structured onboarding timeline with milestone dates
- Get the product in use quickly; don't let implementation drag
- Identify the internal champion who will drive adoption inside the account
- Check in proactively — don't wait for them to complain

**Lighthouse customers**: Early customers who pay less but engage deeply and provide feedback are worth their weight in gold. Invest heavily in their success. Their case studies and referrals will fuel the next stage of growth.

### Mechanisms for Ongoing Success

**Regular check-ins**: Cadence depends on contract value and complexity. Enterprise customers might warrant weekly check-ins initially, then monthly QBRs. SMB customers might be served by automated email sequences + triggered human outreach.

**Health scoring**: Track leading indicators of churn:
- Product login frequency
- Feature adoption breadth
- Support ticket volume and sentiment
- Response rate to communications
- Champion turnover (if your internal champion leaves, the account is at risk)

**QBRs (Quarterly Business Reviews)**: For higher-value accounts. Review:
- What has been accomplished since last QBR
- ROI delivered vs. what was promised at sale
- Roadmap items relevant to their goals
- Expansion opportunities
- Renewal timeline

### Renewals

Renewals are not events — they are the outcome of ongoing success. If you're starting the renewal conversation 30 days before expiration, you're already behind.

**Renewal timeline:**
- 90 days out: Review health score and relationship quality. Identify risks early.
- 60 days out: Initiate formal renewal conversation. Confirm continued fit, address any concerns.
- 30 days out: Close the renewal paperwork. All obstacles should already be resolved.

**Expansion during renewal**: The renewal is the natural moment to discuss expansion — more seats, additional modules, upgraded tier. A customer renewing is a customer who has validated your value; they're more receptive to expansion than they'll ever be post-renewal.

### Learning from Customer Success

Customer success is a feedback pipeline to product. Track:
- What questions customers ask most (documentation and feature gap signals)
- Which features customers use vs. don't use (product focus signals)
- Common implementation challenges (onboarding improvement signals)
- Why customers churn (product-market fit signals)

Formalize the loop: CS team → weekly summary of top themes → product team.

## Chapter 10: Early Sales Management

### Scaling Anti-Patterns

**The most dangerous**: Scaling before PMF. Adding headcount before you have a repeatable, documented playbook means new hires have no foundation. They'll underperform, you won't know if it's the rep or the playbook, and you'll burn cash.

**Signs you're ready to scale:**
- You can explain to a new hire exactly what to say, to whom, in what order
- You have documented your ICP, narrative, email templates, call scripts, and demo flow
- You have win/loss data across enough deals to know what predicts success
- Your win rates and sales cycle lengths are stable (not all over the map)

**Other anti-patterns:**
- **Premature specialization**: Splitting SDR/AE before you have enough volume to feed two roles
- **Hiring your first VP of Sales too early**: They're built to manage a known playbook, not discover one
- **Delegating the playbook**: Every early sales leader must have done the selling themselves

### Abstraction and Specialization

As you scale, roles specialize in sequence:
1. **Founder/generalist** does everything (prospect, outreach, demo, close, CS)
2. **SDR/MDR** takes over top-of-funnel (prospecting, outreach, appointment setting)
3. **AE** focuses on mid-funnel (demos, proposals, closing)
4. **CSM** takes over post-close (implementation, ongoing success, renewals)
5. **Sales Ops** handles data, tooling, reporting

Don't jump steps. Each specialization only makes sense when there's enough volume to fill the role.

### The Role of the Sales Manager

An early sales manager (often the founder transitioning out of the IC role) must:
- Ride along on calls, listen to recordings, give specific feedback
- Run weekly 1:1s focused on pipeline: deal by deal, what's the plan
- Coach on the plays (narrative, objection handling) not just the numbers
- Protect the team's time from distraction
- Model the behaviors they want: activity, directness, CRM discipline

**Not**: Manage through spreadsheets and dashboards from a distance.

## Chapter 11: High-Impact Sales Hiring

### The Cost of a Bad Hire

A bad sales hire sets you back 12-18 months: 3 months to realize they're not working, 3 months of a performance improvement process, 3 months to backfill, and 3-6 months of ramp for the new hire. Get hiring right.

### The Right Profile

**For early-stage sales (Experimentation Mode):**
- Comfortable with ambiguity and incomplete playbooks
- Can craft narratives, not just deliver them
- Intellectually curious about the customer's world
- Strong communication and active listening skills
- Coachable — takes feedback and changes behavior

**For scaling sales (Scaling Mode):**
- Can execute a defined playbook consistently
- High activity discipline
- Competitive, metrics-driven
- Strong closing instincts

These are different profiles. Don't hire a playbook-executor during Experimentation Mode.

### Sources

- **Network and referrals**: Always first. Someone you know who vouches for them.
- **LinkedIn**: Post the role + search for profiles with relevant experience
- **Recruiters**: More appropriate once you have capital and urgency
- **Poaching from competitors**: Gets you someone with product/domain knowledge (useful) but may come with institutional baggage (watch for)
- **Overlooked talent**: Junior people at relevant companies who have strong instincts but limited title

### Screening and Interviewing

**Phone screen** (30 min): Does this person communicate clearly? Can they articulate what they've sold, to whom, and what results they achieved?

**Structured interview (60-90 min)**:
1. Walk me through a deal you're proud of — start to finish (tests: deal instincts, recall, ownership of outcomes)
2. Describe a deal you lost and why (tests: intellectual honesty, learning orientation)
3. How do you manage your pipeline? How do you decide what to prioritize? (tests: process rigor)
4. What do you know about our space / our customer? (tests: curiosity, preparation)
5. Roleplay: "Pitch me your last product" or "Handle this objection" (tests: on-feet performance)

**Reference checks**: Non-optional. Call former managers (not just the references they provide — dig for previous managers via LinkedIn). Ask specifically: "Would you hire this person again, and in what context? What environment do they struggle in?"

### The Closing Conversation

Selling yourself to the candidate matters. Top candidates have options. Be prepared to:
- Make the vision compelling
- Be honest about stage and risk
- Show them why this opportunity accelerates their career
- Move quickly — slow processes lose candidates

## Chapter 12: High-Impact Sales Onboarding & Training

### Why Onboarding Matters

A rep who ramps in 2 months instead of 4 delivers twice the first-year productivity. Onboarding is not overhead — it's a revenue multiplier.

### Onboarding Structure

**Week 1: Company and Product**
- Understand the narrative: why this problem, who has it, what changed
- Learn the product deeply: use it, break it, know it
- Shadow 5-10 calls with experienced reps or founders

**Week 2: Sales Process**
- Walk through the full sales cycle end to end
- Learn the ICP and how to source accounts
- Master email templates and phone scripts (understand why each choice was made)
- Learn the CRM: how to log activity, manage pipeline stages, record call outcomes

**Week 3: Certification and Practice**
- Deliver the pitch to the manager (pass/fail against defined criteria)
- Roleplay the top 5-10 objections (pass/fail)
- Run a mock demo (pass/fail)
- Not certified = not on the phone yet

**Week 4: Supervised Ramp**
- First live calls with manager listening
- Immediate debrief after each call
- Adjust and repeat

### Ongoing Learning & Development

- **Weekly call reviews**: Listen to recorded calls as a team. What worked? What would you do differently?
- **Win/loss reviews**: Post-mortems on closed deals. Extract generalizable lessons.
- **Competitive intelligence updates**: What are competitors saying? How does our positioning evolve?
- **New playbook elements**: When a new objection pattern emerges or a new proof point lands, add it to the playbook and train the team

The best sales teams treat the playbook as a living product — continuously improving based on market feedback.
